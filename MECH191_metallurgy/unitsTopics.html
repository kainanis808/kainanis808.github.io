Units and Topics

This is a simple page containing the projected units and topics of MECH 191.

Unit 1 — Foundations: What Metals Are and How They're Built

Goal: Establish the scientific and practical foundation students need before anything else — what metals are, how to describe them, what's happening inside them, and how processing changes their behavior. Lean on visuals and hands-on observation throughout.

  • What metallurgy is and how metals fit into the broader world of materials
  • The physical properties of metals — what density, melting point, conductivity, and magnetism mean and why they matter on the job
  • Tools used to measure temperature in the field: pyrometers, heat-indicating crayons, and temper colors
  • How to read a material data sheet and material specification
  • How metals are structured at the atomic and grain level — no chemistry background required
  • What grains look like under a microscope and why their size and shape affect how a metal performs
  • How to prepare and examine a metal sample in the lab (metallography)
  • What happens to metal when it's worked — cold working, hot working, and how heat can restore its properties
  • How temperature affects metal behavior — strength, expansion, creep, and performance at both high and low extremes
  • How to recognize ductile and brittle fractures by looking at a broken surface
  • How to read a phase diagram as a reference chart — no calculations involved
  • The basics of heat treatment: what annealing, quenching, and tempering do to steel and why

Moniz: Ch. 1 (Introduction to Metallurgy), Ch. 2 (Physical Properties and Temperature Measurement), Ch. 4 (Structure of Metals), Ch. 5 (Metallography), Ch. 6 (Phase Diagrams), Ch. 7 (Effects of Plastic Deformation and Heat), Ch. 14 (Heat Treatment of Steels — overview)
Kalpakjian: Ch. 1 (Introduction — materials overview, selection), Ch. 3 (Structure and Manufacturing Properties of Metals), Ch. 5.2–5.3, 5.11 (Solidification and Heat Treatment)

Unit 2 — Metal Families: Knowing What You're Working With

Goal: Survey the major metals a technician encounters in the field — how to recognize them, what makes each unique, how they're designated, and where they're used.

  • The steel family — carbon steels, stainless steels, tool steels, and cast irons — what makes each type different and where each is commonly used
  • How heat treatment affects steel in practice — what a technician monitors and verifies on the job
  • Aluminum — why it's used, how it's designated, and how it's worked and joined
  • Copper and its alloys — brasses, bronzes, and copper-nickels — their properties and common applications
  • Titanium — where it's used and why, particularly in aerospace and medical industries
  • Nickel and cobalt alloys — high-temperature and high-performance applications
  • Lead, tin, and zinc — their roles in coatings, soldering, and galvanizing
  • How to identify common metals in the field using visual cues, weight, magnetism, spark testing, and portable instruments

Moniz: Ch. 11–17 (Iron and steel family), Ch. 18–24 (Non-ferrous metals)
Kalpakjian: Ch. 3.10–3.11 (Ferrous and nonferrous overview), Ch. 5.6, 5.11 (Casting alloys and heat treatment)

Unit 3 — Manufacturing Processes: How Parts Are Made and What to Check

Goal: Introduce the core manufacturing processes a technician works around — focused on what each process produces, what defects to look for, and what quality checks apply at each stage.

  • How casting works — common casting processes, what defects look like, and how to inspect a casting for quality
  • Bulk deformation processes — forging, rolling, extrusion, and drawing — what each process does to a metal and what to look for when inspecting the result
  • Sheet metal operations — shearing, bending, stamping, and deep drawing — recognizing process characteristics and common defects
  • An overview of powder metallurgy and the types of sintered parts a technician might encounter
  • Machining basics — how to recognize tool wear, inspect surface finish, and understand what a machined surface is telling you
  • CNC machines and machining centers — what a technician monitors, measures, and documents during and after machining
  • Abrasive machining and finishing operations — grinding, deburring, and surface finishing
  • How tolerances and process selection affect the quality checks a technician is responsible for

Moniz: Ch. 25 (Casting), Ch. 26 (Powder Metallurgy, Forming, Machining, Grinding)
Kalpakjian: Ch. 5.7–5.13 (Casting processes), Ch. 6 (Bulk Deformation), Ch. 7 (Sheet Metal), Ch. 8 (Machining Processes), Ch. 9 (Abrasive and Material Removal), Ch. 11 (Powder Metallurgy)

Unit 4 — Keeping Metal in Service: Welding, Failure, Corrosion, and Modern Tech

Goal: Complete the picture with the skills technicians use to join metal, investigate failures, prevent corrosion, and work with modern manufacturing technology — finishing with a survey of NDE methods and the full range of tools available in the field.

  • Common welding processes — SMAW, GMAW, GTAW, resistance welding, and oxyfuel — what each produces and where each is used
  • Welding metallurgy basics — what happens to metal in the heat-affected zone and why some steels require preheat before welding
  • Welding inspection — how to read a welding procedure, apply welding codes, and interpret weld symbols on drawings
  • How to recognize common welding defects — cracking, porosity, incomplete fusion, and undercut — using visual inspection and NDE methods
  • Other joining methods — brazing, soldering, adhesive bonding, and mechanical fastening — and when each is the right choice
  • How to read a fracture surface and identify the type of failure — ductile, brittle, fatigue, or stress corrosion
  • What a technician collects and documents when investigating a component failure
  • The nine common forms of corrosion — how to recognize each one and what causes it
  • Corrosion prevention and protective coatings — electroplating, hot-dipping, painting, and powder coating — how they're applied and inspected
  • A full survey of NDE methods — visual, liquid penetrant, magnetic particle, ultrasonic, radiographic, electromagnetic, and acoustic emission — what each finds and when to use it
  • An introduction to additive manufacturing, automation, and CNC — what these technologies produce and what technicians are responsible for checking

Moniz: Ch. 8 (Fractography and Failure Analysis), Ch. 27 (Welding), Ch. 28 (Hard Facing and Surface Treatment), Ch. 29 (Nondestructive Examination), Ch. 30 (Effect of Temperature on Metals), Ch. 31 (Corrosion)
Kalpakjian: Ch. 10.12 (Additive Manufacturing), Ch. 12 (Joining and Fastening), Ch. 14 (Automation), Ch. 16 (Competitive Aspects of Design and Manufacturing)

Unit 5 — Testing, Inspection, and Quality: The Technician's Core Skills

Goal: Build the hands-on measurement and inspection skills that define a technician's daily work — running tests correctly, using instruments, applying standards, and documenting results.

  • How to run a tensile or compression test and understand what the results are telling you
  • Hardness testing methods — Brinell, Rockwell, and Vickers — how to perform each test and what the numbers mean
  • Fatigue, impact, and toughness testing — why these tests matter for parts that will be used in the real world
  • How to measure surface roughness and recognize wear and friction damage on parts
  • How to correctly use common measurement tools: calipers, micrometers, and gauges
  • How to read engineering drawings and understand dimensional tolerances
  • Quality assurance procedures and how to document your work in a manufacturing environment
  • How to look up and apply materials standards from organizations like ASTM, ASME, and AWS
  • How to identify an unknown material in the field using markings, documentation, and portable testing equipment
  • An introduction to nondestructive examination — visual testing, liquid penetrant, and magnetic particle — what each method finds and when to use it

Moniz: Ch. 3 (Mechanical Testing), Ch. 9 (Positive Materials Identification), Ch. 10 (Materials Standards), Ch. 29 (Nondestructive Examination — intro)
Kalpakjian: Ch. 2 (Fundamentals of Mechanical Behavior — practical treatment), Ch. 4 (Tribology, Metrology, and Product Quality)
