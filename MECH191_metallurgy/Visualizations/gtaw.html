GTAW Process Overview

Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW), commonly called TIG (Tungsten Inert Gas), uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode to establish the arc. A separate filler rod is fed manually (or omitted for autogenous welds). An inert shielding gas (argon, helium, or blends) protects both the weld pool and the electrode from atmospheric contamination. GTAW produces the highest-quality, cleanest welds of any fusion process, making it the preferred choice for aerospace, food-grade, and critical pipe applications.

Key advantage: No flux, no spatter, no slag — weld quality depends almost entirely on welder skill and parameter selection.

Polarity Selection

DCEN — DC Electrode Negative (Straight Polarity)

Electron flowElectrode → Workpiece
Heat distribution~70% at workpiece, 30% at electrode
Penetration profileDeep, narrow
Electrode diameterSmaller (less heat at tip)
Oxide cleaningNone
Primary materialsCarbon steel, stainless steel, titanium, nickel alloys
Deepest penetration Longest electrode life Most common polarity No oxide removal

With DCEN, electrons leave the tungsten tip and strike the base metal at high velocity. This concentrates the majority of arc energy into the workpiece rather than the electrode, allowing smaller tungsten diameters to carry high amperages. The tungsten tip retains a tapered point, which focuses the arc and produces a stable, narrow bead with excellent depth-to-width ratio.

DCEP — DC Electrode Positive (Reverse Polarity)

Electron flowWorkpiece → Electrode
Heat distribution~70% at electrode, 30% at workpiece
Penetration profileWide, shallow
Electrode diameterMuch larger (heavy heating of tip)
Oxide cleaningStrong cathodic cleaning action
Primary materialsRarely used in GTAW; SMAW only
Cathodic cleaning Electrode overheats rapidly Low amperage limit Shallow penetration

DCEP is almost never used for GTAW in practice. The electrode absorbs ~70% of the arc heat, requiring a very large tungsten ball to avoid burnback. Maximum current is severely limited (e.g., a 3/32" tungsten maxes around 15 A in DCEP vs. 150 A in DCEN). The cathodic cleaning effect is present but AC is preferred for cleaning without these electrode penalties.

AC — Alternating Current

Electron flowReverses each half-cycle
Cleaning half-cycleEP half → oxide removal
Penetration half-cycleEN half → fusion
Electrode tip shapeBalled end (pure or zirconia tungsten)
Primary materialsAluminum, magnesium
Balance controlAdjustable EN/EP ratio on inverters
Removes Al₂O₃ oxide layer Essential for aluminum More electrode heat than DCEN Wider, shallower bead

AC combines cathodic cleaning (EP half-cycle) with fusion penetration (EN half-cycle). Modern inverter-based TIG machines allow adjustment of the EN/EP balance — increasing EN% deepens penetration and reduces electrode heating; increasing EP% enhances oxide cleaning but builds more heat in the tungsten. Filler Addition Technique

Proper technique prevents the most common TIG defects. The filler rod tip must remain inside the argon shielding cone at all times to prevent oxidation.

Torch-rod angle15°–20° between filler and work surface
Feed methodDab — quick small additions at pool leading edge
Rod tip positionAlways inside shielding gas envelope
Common mistakeTouching rod to tungsten → contamination → grind and re-prep

Torch Angles & Travel Direction

Work Angle

The angle between the torch and the workpiece surface in a plane perpendicular to the weld direction. For flat butt welds: 90°. For fillet welds: 45° into the joint. For pipe: varies with position.

Travel Angle (Push)

GTAW almost universally uses a push (forehand) technique — torch tilted 5°–15° in the direction of travel. Push provides better gas coverage ahead of the pool and good visibility. Unlike SMAW/GMAW, there is no slag to push out, so drag technique offers no advantage.

Torch-to-Work Distance (Arc Length)

Optimal arc length ≈ tungsten diameter. Short arc (1× diameter) gives narrow, focused bead. Long arc increases arc voltage, widens bead, reduces penetration, and risks atmospheric contamination of the shielding envelope.

Travel Speed

Must be consistent to maintain uniform bead width. Too slow: excessive heat input, wider bead, burn-through risk. Too fast: incomplete fusion, undercut, narrow underfilled bead. Experienced welders match travel speed to pool size visually.

Why AC is Required for Aluminum

Aluminum rapidly forms a tenacious aluminum oxide (Al₂O₃) surface layer in air. This oxide has a melting point of approximately 2,072°C (3,762°F) — far above aluminum's melting point of 660°C (1,220°F). If the oxide is not removed, the welder sees a "muddy" pool that won't wet out properly, producing cold laps, incomplete fusion, and oxide inclusions. DCEN alone cannot remove this oxide. AC welding provides cathodic cleaning during every EP (electrode positive) half-cycle.

How Cathodic Cleaning Works

EP Half-Cycle — Cleaning Phase

During the electrode-positive (EP) half-cycle, electrons flow from the workpiece to the electrode. Positive argon ions accelerate toward the workpiece and bombard the oxide surface. This ion bombardment physically blasts away the Al₂O₃ crust, exposing clean aluminum beneath.

High electrode heating Oxide removal Wide, shallow arc

EN Half-Cycle — Fusion Phase

During the electrode-negative (EN) half-cycle, electrons flow from electrode to workpiece, just like DCEN. This concentrates arc energy into the base metal, establishing a weld pool in the cleaned zone. The electrode cools slightly, recovering from EP heating.

Deep penetration Electrode cooling Standard fusion

AC Balance Control

Modern inverter TIG machines allow adjustment of the EN/EP balance — the percentage of the AC cycle spent in each polarity. The default is typically 70–75% EN / 25–30% EP. This ratio is the primary control for balancing cleaning action against electrode life and penetration.

Reading the Cleaning Zone

Bright Cleaning Band

A properly set AC weld on aluminum shows a shiny, bright band on each side of the bead where the oxide has been blasted away. This is called the cleaning zone or bright zone. Width: typically 1–3× the bead width.

Too wide cleaning zone: EP% is too high — reduce EP, increase EN. Electrode is overheating.

No or narrow cleaning zone: Insufficient EP — oxide may not be fully removed. Check balance setting and gas flow.

Frequency Effects

AC frequency (typically 20–150 Hz on inverters) also affects bead shape:

Low frequency (20–40 Hz)Wider bead, more cleaning, hotter tungsten
Standard (60 Hz)General purpose balance
High frequency (80–150 Hz)Narrower, focused bead; less electrode heating; great for tight joints

Higher frequency also softens the AC transition (less zero-crossing instability), improving arc stability, especially at lower amperages.

TIG Root Pass — Why It Matters

In open-root pipe welding, the root pass is the most critical weld bead — it establishes complete joint penetration (CJP) and is impossible to repair without removing all subsequent fill and cap passes. The root pass must achieve full fusion at the root without burn-through, and it must produce a smooth, uniform inside bead (root bead) free from cold laps, icicles, or excessive reinforcement. GTAW is the preferred process for root passes in critical pipe (process, nuclear, and aerospace piping) because of its precise arc control and clean, slag-free deposit.

Joint Preparation

Standard Open-Root Groove Geometry

Bevel angle (each side)37.5° (total included angle: 75°)
Root face (land)1/16" – 3/32" (1.6–2.4 mm)
Root opening (gap)1/16" – 3/32" (1.6–2.4 mm)
Root face purposeControls melt-through; too thin → burn-through; too thick → cold lap

Consistent root face dimension is the single most important joint prep variable. It must be machined or carefully ground — not cut with a torch.

Tack Weld Placement

Tacks maintain root gap and alignment during welding. For 2G/5G/6G positions:

Tack count (typical)3–4 tacks evenly spaced (clock positions)
Tack length3/4" – 1" (19–25 mm)
Tacking sequence3 o'clock, 9 o'clock, 6 o'clock, 12 o'clock
Tack featheringGrind ends to a feather — allows root pass to blend
Tack inspectionNo cracks, porosity, or undercut before proceeding

Torch Angle & Technique

Work Angle

Torch held at 0°–5° from perpendicular to the pipe surface in the transverse plane. The torch should split the groove angle exactly, keeping the arc centered on the root opening. Drift to one side preferentially heats that bevel face, causing asymmetric fusion and potential lack-of-fusion on the opposite side.

Travel Angle

Push technique: torch tilted 5°–10° in the direction of travel. In the 5G (pipe horizontal, fixed) position, this means constant adjustment as the welder progresses around the pipe. At the 6 o'clock position, the torch may go slightly vertical to maintain shielding coverage.

Arc Length

Extremely short arc is critical for root passes — approximately 1/16" to 3/32" (1.6–2.4 mm), equal to the tungsten diameter. A longer arc deflects toward the bevel faces instead of the root, reducing penetration and risking cold laps at the root line.

Filler Addition Technique — Root Pass

1
Establish Pool
Start arc, allow a pool to form at the root opening. The root face will begin to melt back and a small "keyhole" opening appears — this is the indicator of full penetration.
2
Identify Keyhole
The keyhole is a small teardrop-shaped opening at the leading edge of the pool. It confirms the arc is burning through the root. No keyhole = incomplete penetration. Keyhole too large = burn-through risk.
3
Dab Filler
Feed filler into the front of the pool (not directly into the keyhole). Small, consistent dabs — rod never leaves the shielding gas cone. Each dab partially fills and slightly collapses the keyhole.
4
Advance Arc
After each dab, pause briefly to let the pool solidify slightly, then advance the torch. This prevents the pool from becoming too large and sagging (especially in 5G vertical-down sections).
5
Maintain Keyhole
The goal is consistent keyhole size throughout. Adjust travel speed and amperage (with foot pedal) to compensate for heat build-up as the pipe warms up during the pass.
6
Tie-In / Stop
At a stop, taper the amperage down using the foot pedal, add a final dab, and withdraw the torch slowly to avoid crater cracks. Grind crater to a feather before restarting.

Back Purging

Why Back Purge?

Without back purge, atmospheric oxygen contacts the inside of the root bead while it is molten. On stainless steel and reactive metals (titanium, chromium alloys), this causes severe oxidation — the root bead discolors, loses chromium from the HAZ, and the corrosion resistance is compromised. On carbon steel it causes excessive oxidation slag on the inside surface.

Without purge: oxidized root, sugaring, corrosion failure
With purge: clean silver/gold tint, full corrosion resistance preserved

Back Purge Setup

Gas100% Argon (most common); nitrogen for some stainless
Flow rate5–15 CFH; fill pipe slowly to displace O₂
Purge damsFoam plugs, paper/tape, commercial inflatable dams
O₂ level target<50 ppm for stainless; <10 ppm for titanium
Purge timeAllow 3–5 pipe volumes of gas before welding
Maintain during weldingKeep flow until root pass and first fill pass cool below 300°C

Monitor with oxygen analyzer for critical applications (pharmaceutical, nuclear)

Pipe Welding Positions — 5G & 6G

5G — Pipe Horizontal Fixed

Pipe axis is horizontal; pipe does not rotate. Welder must weld in all positions (flat at top, vertical on sides, overhead at bottom). The root pass is the most demanding in 5G because the welder must maintain consistent technique through gravity changes.

Top (12 o'clock)Flat — easiest; pool tends to sag if over-heated
Sides (3 & 9 o'clock)Vertical — pool flows toward 6 o'clock; use faster travel
Bottom (6 o'clock)Overhead — most difficult; risk of drip; reduce amperage

6G — Pipe Inclined 45°, Fixed

Pipe axis inclined at 45°; pipe does not rotate. This position combines all others simultaneously and is the qualification standard for pipe welders — a 6G qualification is universally accepted for all positions.

Qualification scopeQualifies welder for all pipe positions (2G, 5G, 6G)
Root pass challengeConstant position change every few inches of travel
Amperage controlFoot pedal essential — reduce at high-heat overhead zone

AWS ER Filler Metal Classification System

The AWS filler metal classification system uses alphanumeric designators that encode the filler's form, mechanical properties, base metal compatibility, and chemical composition. The ER prefix (Electrode/Rod) indicates the filler can be used as either a TIG rod or MIG wire. Understanding the system allows welders and engineers to quickly select the correct consumable from any manufacturer.
