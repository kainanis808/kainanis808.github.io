AEC Design Guidelines for Aluminum Extrusions

Minimum Wall Thickness

6063-T6: 0.8 mm min (thin wall)
6061-T6: 1.8 mm min
Max wall ratio: 3:1 thick:thin
Uniform walls reduce warping

Corner & Fillet Radii

Outside corner: R ≥ 0.4 mm
Inside fillet: r ≥ 0.8 mm
Larger radii improve die life, reduce stress concentration in part

Tongue Ratio (Semi-Hollow)

TR = Avoid / wopening²
TR ≤ 3.0 for semi-hollow die
TR > 3.0 requires full hollow (bridge/porthole) die

Circumscribed Circle

CCD = smallest circle enclosing the profile
Determines minimum press size
Standard CCDs: 5", 7", 8", 10", 12", 14"

Symmetry & Balance

Symmetric sections: lower die stress, no twist
Asymmetric: die offset needed
Balance metal distribution around CG to equalize flow velocity

Draft & Taper

Standard aluminum: 0° draft (no taper required)
Deep pockets: 0.5°–1° taper helps die fill
Unlike forging, extrusion requires no draft for part removal

Extrusion Process Animation

Direct Extrusion
Ram pushes billet through a stationary die. Billet slides against container walls, generating friction heat. A dead metal zone (DMZ) forms at the die corners. Accounts for ~85% of all extrusion production. Requires a discard (butt end) to prevent pipe defect contamination.
Indirect Extrusion
Die is on a hollow stem that advances into a stationary billet and container. No billet-container relative motion → no friction loss → 25–35% less ram force required. Smaller butt discard (no pipe defect). Less flexible for shape changes; tooling more complex.
Pipe Defect
Near the end of the billet, the dead metal zone (which contains surface oxides and contaminants) is drawn inward through the die by the flow field. This creates a pipe-shaped oxide inclusion inside the extrusion. The butt discard (15–20% of billet) is retained to prevent pipe from entering the finished extrusion.

Profile Complexity Classification & Die Design

The AEC classifies extrusion profiles into five complexity classes based on geometry. Higher complexity demands more sophisticated die design, lower extrusion speeds, and tighter process control.

Class 1 — Simple Solid

Rod, bar, flat, simple angle
Solid flat die

Die Types

Flat (Solid) Die

Single-piece die for solid profiles. Land (bearing) controls metal flow. Relief angle behind land prevents friction on extruded surface. Used for Classes 1 & 2.

Bridge / Porthole Die

Two-piece die (cap + die) with ports split around a central mandrel. Metal welds in the weld chamber under pressure and temperature. Creates integral hollow without a seam line. Used for Classes 4 & 5.

Spider Die

Variation of porthole die with arms (spider legs) supporting central mandrel. Used for larger hollow profiles and multi-void sections. More weld seams but better metal distribution for asymmetric sections.

How geometry affects extrusion speed: Extrusion speed is limited by the maximum allowable metal temperature at the die exit — if too hot, the surface cracks (hot shortness) or tears. Complex, thin-walled profiles generate more frictional heat per unit of material and restrict flow more, requiring slower ram speeds. Multi-void hollows also require the metal to split around multiple mandrel supports and re-weld, requiring more time at temperature. Speed reductions of 75% vs. simple solid profiles are common for complex hollow sections.

Alloy Selection Guide

Extrudability vs. Strength

Extrudability = ease of extrusion (speed, complexity achievable, die life). Higher extrudability → lower strength series.

Series overview:
  • 1xxx — Pure Al; best extrudability, corrosion resistance; low strength
  • 6xxx — Al-Mg-Si; dominant extrusion series; heat-treatable; T5 (at press) or T6 (solution HT + age)
  • 7xxx — Al-Zn-Mg; highest strength; difficult to extrude; requires quench, multi-step age
  • 2xxx — Al-Cu; aerospace; very difficult to extrude; not weldable; requires anodize or clad for corrosion
