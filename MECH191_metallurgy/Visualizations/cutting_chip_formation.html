Fundamentals of Cutting & Chip Formation

Orthogonal Cutting Model — DoITPoMS

In orthogonal cutting, the tool edge is perpendicular to the cutting velocity. Material ahead of the tool is forced to shear along a thin zone — the shear plane — at angle φ. The chip then slides along the rake face, experiencing a second deformation zone.

Merchant's Shear Angle Equation

φ = 45° + α/2 − λ/2

where α = rake angle, λ = friction angle (= arctan μ). Higher rake or lower friction → larger shear angle → thinner chip → less energy.

r = t₁/t₂ = sin φ / cos(φ − α)

Chip thickness ratio r. Since chip is thicker than uncut chip (t₂ > t₁), we always have r < 1.

Specific Cutting Energy

u = Fc / (t₁ · b) [J/mm³]

Fc = cutting force, b = width of cut. Specific energy depends on work material, chip thickness (size effect), and tool geometry. Harder materials → higher u.

MRR = Vc · t₁ · b [mm³/s]

Material removal rate. Power = u × MRR.

Force Components

Fc = τs · As / sin φ · cos(λ−α) / cos(φ+λ−α)

τs = shear yield stress, As = shear plane area = t₁·b/sinφ. Thrust force Ft acts perpendicular to Vc. For positive rake: Ft can be negative (tool is pulled into the work).

Chip Types

Continuous

Ductile materials at moderate speed. Smooth rake face, positive rake angle. Good surface finish but chip disposal problem. Preferred for finish cuts

Continuous with BUE

Built-up edge: work material cold-welds to rake face at low–medium speeds. Acts as unstable cutting edge — periodic fracture degrades surface finish. Avoid: adjust speed/coolant

Segmented (Serrated)

Periodic shear bands — thermally softened zones alternate with harder regions. Common in titanium, hardened steel, high-speed cutting. Not necessarily harmful. Ti alloys, Inconel

Discontinuous

Brittle materials (cast iron, hard brass) or negative rake cutting. Chip fractures into segments. Good chip control, but cyclic force variation can damage surface. Cast iron, ceramics

Three Deformation Zones — DoITPoMS

● Primary Zone — Shear Plane ● Secondary Zone — Rake Face ● Tertiary Zone — Machined Surface

Primary Zone

Thin shear zone along the shear plane. Strain rates: 10³–10⁵ s⁻¹. Material undergoes intense plastic shear — the majority of cutting energy is dissipated here as heat. Temperatures: 400–900 °C depending on Vc and material.

Secondary Zone

Friction between the underside of the chip and the rake face. High compressive stresses near the tool tip cause sticking contact (seizure zone); further up, sliding contact. Peak temperatures occur here — drives crater wear through diffusion.

Tertiary Zone

Rubbing/ploughing between the tool flank and the newly machined surface. Determines surface integrity — residual stresses, work hardening, and surface roughness. Driven by flank wear land growth over tool life.

Tool Wear Mechanisms & Measurement

Flank Wear (VB)

Abrasive wear on the clearance face. Measured as wear land width VB (mm). ISO 3685 criterion: VB = 0.3 mm (uniform), or VBmax = 0.6 mm. Primary cause of dimensional inaccuracy.

Taylor's Equation: Vc · Tⁿ = C

n = Taylor exponent (0.1–0.4 for HSS; 0.2–0.5 for carbide). Higher Vc → shorter tool life exponentially.

Crater Wear (KT, KB, KM)

Thermally-driven diffusion wear on rake face. Deepens the crater (KT = depth, KB = distance from edge, KM = max width). Critical for carbide tools in steel — mitigated by TiC/TiN coatings.

Other Wear Modes

Notch wear: at depth-of-cut line — oxidation + hard scale. BUE fracture: built-up edge periodically breaks away. Plastic deformation: at very high Vc + temperature, tool nose deforms. Chipping: micro-fracture from interrupted cuts.

Cutting Parameters — Sandvik Coromant

All material removal operations are controlled by three primary parameters. Understanding their interaction determines productivity, surface quality, and tool life.

Cutting Speed Vc (m/min)

Vc = π · Dm · n / 1000

Peripheral speed at the cutting edge. Dm = machined diameter (mm), n = spindle speed (rpm). Vc is the strongest driver of tool life (Taylor). Recommended starting values by material:

Feed per Revolution fn (mm/rev)

Ra ≈ fn² / (8 · rε) [mm]

rε = nose radius (mm). Feed dominates surface roughness. Increasing fn raises MRR and chip load — too high causes insert chipping. Typical: 0.1–0.5 mm/rev roughing; 0.05–0.15 finishing.

Depth of Cut ap (mm)

Radial engagement. Has the smallest effect on tool life of the three parameters. Minimum ap should be >1.5× the cutting edge radius to avoid rubbing instead of cutting. Roughing: 2–8 mm; finishing: 0.2–1 mm.

MRR = Vc · fn · ap [cm³/min] (× 1/1000 for unit match)

Chip Thickness & Engagement

Mean Chip Thickness hm

hm = fn · sin κr

κr = entering (lead) angle. At κr = 90°, hm = fn (full feed). Reducing κr thins the chip — distributes load over longer edge, allowing higher fn without overloading the insert.

Specific Cutting Force kc

kc = kc1 · hm⁻ᵐᶜ [N/mm²]

kc1 = specific force at hm=1 mm, mc = chip thickness exponent (~0.2–0.4). As chip thins (small hm), kc rises sharply — the "size effect." Relevant for finishing and micro-machining.

Cutting Force Components

Fc = kc · ap · hm

Tangential (main) cutting force. Radial (passive) force Fp ≈ 0.3–0.5 Fc; axial (feed) force Ff ≈ 0.1–0.3 Fc. Power = Fc × Vc / 60 000 (kW). Size cutting force components to prevent workpiece deflection.

Tool Geometry — Sandvik Coromant

Rake Angle γ (γo)

Positive rake (+10° to +20°): sharp, low forces, less heat — use for ductile materials (Al, soft steel, plastics). Risk of cutting edge weakness.
Negative rake (−6° to 0°): strong edge, handles interrupted cuts — use for hardened steel, cast iron, heavy roughing.

Clearance Angle α (αo)

Prevents flank face rubbing on machined surface. Typical 6–12°. Too small → rubbing & heat; too large → weak edge. Turning inserts: 7° standard. For hardened steel, use smaller clearance (5–7°) for edge strength.

Entering (Lead) Angle κr

Angle between cutting edge and feed direction. κr = 90°: all force in radial direction; κr = 45°: forces split equally radial/axial. Smaller κr → thinner chip → longer engaged edge → better tool life (but more vibration risk).

Nose Radius rε

Controls surface finish and edge strength. Large rε: stronger, better Ra but more radial force → vibration. Small rε: finer corners, less radial force. Sandvik recommendation: rε = fn × 2 to fn × 6 for stable cutting.

Grade Designation Logic (Sandvik GC series)

GC = Generation Coated. First digit: ISO group (1=P, 2=M, 3=K, 4=general). Middle digits: position in toughness-hardness scale (05=hardest/most wear-resistant → 40=toughest). Example: GC4315 = general-purpose P-steel grade, hard side of range.

Coating systems: CVD (TiCN+Al₂O₃+TiN) for high Vc steel/cast iron; PVD (TiAlN, TiSiN) for sharp edges, stainless, titanium, interrupted cuts; PCD for non-ferrous; CBN for hardened steel.
