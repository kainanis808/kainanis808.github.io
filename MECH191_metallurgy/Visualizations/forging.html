Forging – Design, Grain Flow & Process Comparison

Based on Forging Industry Association (FIA) guidelines and ASM International mechanical property data

FIA Closed-Die Forging Design Guidelines

Draft Angles

External surfaces: 5–7°
Internal surfaces / pockets: 7–10°
Enables die release without ejector damage

Fillet & Corner Radii

Fillet r ≥ 3 mm (inside corners)
Corner R ≥ 1.5 mm (outside corners)
Larger radii improve flow, reduce die wear

Parting Line Placement

Locate at maximum cross-section
Straight PL preferred; offset PL only when needed to minimize machining stock

Flash Land & Gutter

Land width: 3–5 × stock thickness
Gutter depth: 2–3× land thickness
Flash restricts flow to fill cavity, then exits to gutter

Web & Rib Proportions

Rib height : width ≤ 6 : 1
Web thickness ≥ 5 mm (steel); ≥ 3 mm (Al)
Thin webs require more forging load and increase die wear

Material Flow & Grain

Design so grain flows parallel to principal stress direction
Avoid abrupt section changes that cause turbulent flow and laps

Grain Flow Comparison

The same L-bracket cross-section produced by three different processes. Grain flow lines (streamlines) show how the crystallographic fiber orientation tracks through the part — continuous fiber parallel to the stress path is optimal.

Forged

Grain follows the part contour. Continuous fiber through every cross-section. No cut fibers at critical surfaces. Best fatigue and impact resistance.

Machined from Bar

Grain is cut at machined surfaces. Bar rolling gives straight grain — machining the L-shape severs fibers at every shoulder, exposing weak end-grain at stress risers.

Cast

Dendritic, non-directional grain. Solidification produces columnar dendrites growing toward the mold walls. No preferred fiber; isotropic but lower strength than wrought products.

Why grain flow matters: Fatigue cracks initiate and propagate most easily along grain boundaries and perpendicular to the grain direction. When grain is aligned with the load path (as in forging), cracks must cross the grain — the harder path. When grain is cut (machined bar) or random (casting), cracks find easy propagation routes along grain boundaries near the surface, reducing fatigue life and impact toughness by 30–60% relative to forging.

Process selection guidance:
  • Forging — best mechanical properties; preferred for safety-critical, high-stress, fatigue-loaded parts (connecting rods, crankshafts, aircraft structure, landing gear)
  • Investment casting — complex geometry, good surface finish; used when forging tooling cost is prohibitive or geometry cannot be forged
  • Sand casting — large parts, low volume; moderate properties; highest porosity risk
  • Machined bar — low volume, high precision; grain cut at surfaces; material waste can be severe for complex shapes

Closed Die Forging – Process Animation

Stage 0 – Billet Loading
Hot billet (orange, ~1200 °C for steel) placed on lower die. Billet is slightly oversized relative to part volume to ensure complete cavity fill.
Billet Preparation
Stock is cut to precise volume (±2%). Heated in a furnace to forging temperature (1150–1260 °C for carbon steel, 420–480 °C for aluminum alloys). Surface scale is minimized to avoid die wear.
Die Filling & Flash
As the ram descends, material flows outward to fill the die cavity. Flash forms in the narrow land gap, creating back-pressure that forces material into thin sections. Flash thickness is 2–4 mm.
Trimming
After forging, the part is placed in a trim die. A punch shears the flash at the parting line. For steel, trimming is done hot (900–1100 °C) or cold. Flash scrap is recycled.
