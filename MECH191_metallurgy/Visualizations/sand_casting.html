What Is Sand Casting?

Sand casting is the oldest and most widely used casting process. A pattern (typically wood, plastic, or metal) is pressed into a moist sand mixture to form a mold cavity. Molten metal is poured in, allowed to solidify, and the sand is broken away to release the part. It accommodates virtually any metal, nearly any size, and extremely complex geometry through the use of sand cores — making it the process of choice when tooling cost must be low or part runs are small.

Surface Finish

Sand casting produces the roughest surfaces of all casting processes. The typical range is 125–500 µin Ra (3.2–12.5 µm Ra). With careful pattern work, fine-grained molding sand, and mold washes a best-achievable of ~63 µin Ra (1.6 µm) is possible, but rare in production. Compare to die casting (~32–125 µin) or investment casting (~32–63 µin).

Dimensional Tolerance vs. Part Size

Tolerance in sand casting is not fixed — it scales with part size. The AFS Casting Tolerances Standard (CT system, ISO 8062) assigns a tolerance grade based on part length and process. Sand casting typically falls in CT11–CT14.

Shaded band shows the ±tolerance for a given largest dimension. Metal patterns and controlled molding conditions push toward the lower bound; hand-rammed green sand is near the upper bound.

Why Sand Casting Is Alloy-Agnostic

Because the mold is sacrificial (broken apart each cycle), there is no chemical interaction or temperature limit imposed by a permanent mold material. Sand is refractory to >1600 °C, so even high-melting-point alloys like carbon steel and nickel superalloys can be sand cast. Click an alloy family below to see details.
