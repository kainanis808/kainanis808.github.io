Why Does Casting Microstructure Look the Way It Does?

When a liquid metal cools below its melting point it doesn't freeze all at once. Instead, atoms attach themselves to tiny solid nuclei and build outward in preferred crystallographic directions — forming tree-like structures called dendrites (Greek for "tree"). The main trunk grows fastest along the easy-slip direction (e.g., ⟨100⟩ for cubic metals), then side branches called secondary arms sprout perpendicular to it, and so on.

Each dendrite cluster that grows from one nucleus becomes one grain. Grains are single crystals with a uniform orientation; neighboring grains have different orientations, separated by grain boundaries. The final cast piece is therefore a mosaic of randomly oriented grains — a polycrystalline microstructure.

Cooling rate controls everything. Rapid quenching gives little time for atoms to diffuse, so dendrite arms are fine and closely spaced — small dendrite arm spacing (DAS). Slow cooling allows long-range diffusion; arms coarsen into coarser, widely spaced structures. Fine DAS = finer grain size = stronger, tougher casting.

4 · Key Concepts

Nucleation

Solidification begins when atoms cluster into a stable embryo. In the mold, this usually happens on the mold wall (heterogeneous nucleation) rather than in the bulk liquid. Grain refiners (e.g., TiB₂ in aluminum) add more nuclei, producing a finer equiaxed grain structure.

Columnar vs. Equiaxed Grains

Near the mold wall, steep thermal gradients drive grains to grow long and columnar, pointing toward the mold center. If the superheat is low or grain refiners are added, equiaxed (blocky) grains form in the interior. The transition is called the columnar-to-equiaxed transition (CET).

Dendrite Arm Spacing (DAS)

DAS is the distance between secondary arms. It controls the scale of microsegregation and the distance solute must diffuse during homogenization heat treatment. Finer DAS → shorter diffusion paths → more uniform composition → better mechanical properties.

Microsegregation

Solute atoms (e.g., Cu in Al) are rejected at the solid–liquid interface as dendrites grow. The dendrite core solidifies first and is solute-lean; the interdendritic region solidifies last and is solute-rich. This chemical banding is reduced by homogenization annealing above the solvus.

Hot Tearing & Porosity

The last liquid in interdendritic channels is difficult to feed. Shrinkage and thermal stresses can tear the semi-solid network apart (hot tearing) or leave isolated pores. Designing a correct gating/riser system and controlling solidification direction (directional solidification) mitigate both defects.

Hall–Petch Strengthening

Grain boundaries block dislocation motion. Finer grains = more boundary area per unit volume = higher yield strength: σy = σ₀ + k·d−½. This is why rapid cooling (smaller grains, finer DAS) generally produces stronger castings.
