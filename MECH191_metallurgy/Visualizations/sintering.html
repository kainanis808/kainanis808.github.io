What Is Neck Formation?

When powder particles are heated, atoms at contact points migrate toward the shared boundary, growing a neck. The driving force is the reduction in total surface energy — two free surfaces are replaced by one grain boundary, which has lower energy. Neck size is described by the ratio x/R, where x is the neck radius and R is the particle radius. Early-stage sintering theory (Kuczynski, 1949) predicts (x/R)ⁿ = K·t, where n and K depend on the active diffusion mechanism and temperature.

Mass-Transport Mechanisms During Sintering

Sintering densification depends on which diffusion path atoms take. DoITPoMS identifies five mechanisms. Three grow the neck without densification (surface diffusion, evaporation–condensation, lattice diffusion from surface) and two cause true shrinkage by pulling particle centres together (lattice diffusion from grain boundary, grain-boundary diffusion). Pore Evolution & Closure

As sintering proceeds, pores change shape and connectivity. Initially pores form an interconnected channel network (open porosity). At ~8% porosity the channels pinch off into isolated spherical pores trapped inside grains or at grain boundaries. Final-stage sintering slowly shrinks these isolated pores — but grain growth can trap them inside grains where diffusion paths are longer, slowing densification.
