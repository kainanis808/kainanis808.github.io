The Three Principal Fracture Modes

The fracture surface records the crack initiation site, propagation direction, and local failure mode. Reading a fracture surface (fractography) identifies whether failure was ductile overload, brittle cleavage, or cyclic fatigue — and points to the origin and contributing factors. ASM Handbook Vol. 12 is the primary reference atlas for fractographic features.

Ductile Fracture
Fibrous zone — grey, rough centre; extensive plastic deformation before fracture
Shear lip — 45° angled rim; plane-stress fracture at free surface
Dimples (SEM) — equiaxed microvoids from microvoid coalescence (MVC)
Brittle Fracture
River marks — fine lines converging toward origin; steps between parallel cleavage planes
Cleavage facets — flat, bright, faceted; crack on {001} crystallographic planes
Chevron pattern — macroscopic herringbone pointing to origin; visible to naked eye
Fatigue Fracture
Initiation site — at surface stress concentration; point of crack nucleation
Beach marks — macroscopic concentric arcs; record crack arrest or load changes
Ratchet marks — radial steps between adjacent initiation sites
Striations (SEM) — 1 striation = 1 load cycle; spacing ∝ ΔK
Final fracture zone — rough overload; ductile dimples or cleavage

Ductile Fracture — Microvoid Coalescence (MVC)

Ductile fracture proceeds by nucleation, growth, and coalescence of microvoids at second-phase particles and inclusions. The process requires significant plastic work and produces the characteristic dimpled fracture surface seen in SEM. Higher inclusion content → shorter void spacing → lower fracture toughness.

Step 1 of 5

1 — Nucleation at Inclusions

Loading produces stress concentrations at second-phase particles and inclusions (MnS, Fe₃C, Al₂O₃). Decohesion occurs at the particle–matrix interface, or the particle itself fractures. A void forms around each particle when local stress exceeds interface cohesion strength.

Particles: MnS stringers, oxide inclusions, carbide particles
Interface decohesion or particle cracking initiates void
Higher particle fraction → shorter inter-void spacing → lower ductility

Brittle Fracture — Cleavage Mechanism

Cleavage fracture occurs when local tensile stress exceeds the cohesive strength of specific low-index crystallographic planes — {001} in BCC iron, {0001} in HCP metals. No plastic deformation occurs at the crack tip. River marks form when the crack steps between parallel cleavage planes at grain boundaries.

Step 1 of 4

1 — Polycrystalline Structure Under Tensile Load

A polycrystalline BCC steel under tensile loading. Each grain has a different crystallographic orientation. The {001} cleavage plane is oriented differently in each grain. Fracture initiates in the grain where the resolved tensile stress normal to the cleavage plane is highest, often at a stress concentration or grain boundary triple point.

Fatigue Fracture Surface — Multi-Scale Atlas

Fatigue fracture surfaces reveal different features at progressively higher magnification. Each scale provides different information about loading history, crack growth rate, and initiation conditions.

Macro View — Complete Fracture Face

The complete fracture face of a rotating-bending fatigue specimen. The initiation site is at the surface (lower-left), where a stress concentration started the crack. Beach marks radiate outward as concentric arcs. The final fracture zone (upper-right) is the rough overload region where the remaining cross-section was insufficient to carry the load.

Initiation site — at surface; often at a stress concentration
Beach marks — concentric arcs recording crack front at intervals
Ratchet marks — steps between adjacent initiation sites
Final fracture zone — rough overload; ductile dimples or cleavage

Striation Counting — Crack Growth Rate from Fracture Surface

At high SEM magnification, individual fatigue striations correspond to one load cycle each (Forsyth-Laird model). Measuring striation spacing gives crack growth rate da/dN directly from the fracture surface — enabling reconstruction of loading history in failure investigations.

Case Studies — Fracture Surface Reading in Failure Investigations

Case A — High-Cycle Fatigue (Shaft at Keyway)

Case A: Rotating Shaft — Fatigue at Keyway Corner

Component: 1040 steel shaft, 40mm dia, rotating bending, 2.3 × 10⁷ cycles
Stress concentration: Keyway with sharp corner (Kt ≈ 3.2)

Origin: Keyway corner — surface crack initiation at Kt = 3.2
Propagation: Fine beach marks (high-cycle); striation spacing 0.3–0.8 μm
Final fracture: Small overload zone (~15% of cross-section) — indicates low nominal stress, high-cycle regime
Corrective action: Add radius ≥1mm at keyway corner + shot peen to introduce compressive residual stress
Case B — Brittle Fracture (Pressure Vessel Nozzle)

Case B: Pressure Vessel Nozzle — Low-Temperature Brittle Fracture

Component: A516-70 nozzle weld, 25mm plate, ambient −15°C
Failure stress: Well below yield strength (<40% Fy) — single pressurisation event

Chevron pattern: Points to origin at weld toe HAZ — pre-existing HIC underbead crack
Cleavage facets: Bright, flat, crystalline — martensite in HAZ below NDT at −15°C
No shear lip: Fully brittle fracture — confirms temperature was below nil-ductility transition
Contributing factors: No preheat for CE 0.48 steel; HIC crack pre-existed; cold-temperature startup below NDT
