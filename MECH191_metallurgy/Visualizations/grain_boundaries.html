Grain Boundaries and Types

Grain boundaries are interfaces between two grains (crystallites) in a polycrystalline material. They form where grains of different crystallographic orientation meet, and they play a critical role in determining the mechanical, thermal, and electrical properties of metals.

Types of Grain Boundaries

Low-Angle Grain Boundaries (LAGB)

Low-angle grain boundaries occur when the misorientation angle between adjacent grains is less than 15°. They are composed of an array of dislocations and have relatively low energy. There are two subtypes:

  • Tilt Boundary: Formed by an array of edge dislocations. The rotation axis lies within the boundary plane.
  • Twist Boundary: Formed by an array of screw dislocations. The rotation axis is perpendicular to the boundary plane.

High-Angle Grain Boundaries (HAGB)

High-angle grain boundaries occur when the misorientation angle between adjacent grains is greater than 15°. They have higher energy than low-angle boundaries and are more effective at impeding dislocation motion, which contributes to grain boundary strengthening (Hall-Petch effect).

Special Grain Boundaries

  • Coincidence Site Lattice (CSL) Boundaries: Boundaries where a fraction of lattice sites are shared between the two grains. These have lower energy and higher mobility than general high-angle boundaries.
  • Twin Boundaries: A special type of CSL boundary where the two grains are mirror images of each other across the boundary plane. Common in FCC metals like copper and stainless steel.
