Grain Boundaries

Interfaces between crystallites and how their misorientation shapes material properties

Grain boundaries are interfaces where two grains of different crystallographic orientation meet. Their structure and energy depend on the misorientation angle between the grains, and they strongly influence mechanical, thermal, and electrical properties of polycrystalline metals.

Low-Angle Tilt Boundary

Misorientation < 15°. The grains are tilted relative to each other about an axis that lies in the boundary plane. The boundary is an array of evenly spaced edge dislocations (⊥). As the misorientation angle increases, the dislocations crowd closer together.

Low-Angle Twist Boundary

Also misorientation < 15°, but here one grain is twisted relative to the other about an axis perpendicular to the boundary plane. The boundary consists of a cross-grid of screw dislocations. Viewing the boundary face-on reveals the characteristic grid pattern.

High-Angle Grain Boundary

Misorientation > 15°. The two lattices are so different in orientation that dislocations can no longer be resolved individually — the boundary becomes a disordered, amorphous-like transition zone roughly 2–5 atoms wide. Higher energy makes it more effective at blocking dislocation motion (Hall–Petch strengthening).

Twin Boundary (Special / CSL)

A twin boundary is a special Coincidence Site Lattice (CSL) boundary where Grain B is a mirror image of Grain A across the boundary plane. Very low energy. Common in FCC metals (Cu, austenitic stainless steel). The atoms at the boundary itself sit on shared lattice sites, minimising strain.
