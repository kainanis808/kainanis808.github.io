What Is Powder Metallurgy?

Powder metallurgy (PM) is a net-shape or near-net-shape manufacturing process in which metal powders are compacted into a desired form and then sintered (heated below the melting point) to bond the particles into a solid part. Unlike casting, the material never becomes fully liquid — making PM uniquely suited for refractory metals, controlled porosity parts (self-lubricating bearings), and high-volume precision components. According to the Metal Powder Industries Federation (MPIF), PM can achieve material utilization above 97%, dimensional tolerances of ±0.05 mm, and production rates exceeding 1,500 parts/hour on automated presses.

Density Progression Through the PM Route

Each stage of PM increases part density. Starting from loose powder (55–65% theoretical density), compaction brings the green compact to 80–88%, and sintering further densifies to 85–95%. Secondary operations like repressing or HIP can reach near-full density.

MPIF Design Principles for PM Parts

PM parts are compacted along a single axis (the pressing direction). This fundamental constraint drives most design rules. Shapes that can be formed by a punch pressing straight down — and extracted straight up — are ideal. Features like undercuts, cross-holes, and threads perpendicular to the press axis require secondary operations or are simply impossible in green compaction. Green = preferred · Red = avoid

Sintering: The Science of Particle Bonding

Sintering is the thermal process by which powder particles bond without full melting. The driving force is the reduction in total surface energy — particles touch at small contact points called necks. As temperature rises, atoms diffuse across these contacts, growing the neck, reducing porosity, and increasing part density.
