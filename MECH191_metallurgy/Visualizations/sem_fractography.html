Dimple Morphology — Microvoid Coalescence (MVC) in SEM

Dimples are the SEM signature of ductile fracture via microvoid coalescence. Dimple geometry encodes the local stress state: equiaxed dimples indicate pure Mode I (tensile opening); elongated/parabolic dimples indicate shear or mixed-mode loading. Thermo Fisher Apreo SEM operates at 0.8–30 kV with SE and BSE detectors to resolve individual dimple walls and inclusions at centres.

Mode I — Equiaxed Dimples
Round, symmetric — equal diameter in all directions; formed under pure tensile opening stress
Inclusion/particle at dimple base — MnS, oxide, or carbide nucleation site
Wall ridges — shared walls between adjacent coalesced voids; height ∝ void growth ratio
Mode III — Elongated/Parabolic Dimples
Parabolic profile — elongated along shear direction; open end points toward crack propagation
Matched pair rule — opposing fracture surfaces show dimples pointing in opposite directions
Aspect ratio (L/W > 2) — higher ratio indicates larger shear component
Mixed Mode — Tear Dimples
Irregular, flattened — combination of I + III; seen at shear lips and Mode II crack fronts
Depth gradient — shallow near shear lip transition; deeper in plane-strain core
Roughness parameter Ra (SEM stereo) — higher for equiaxed, lower for shallow tear dimples

Dimple Size, Depth & Material Correlations

Dimple diameter and depth reflect void growth ratio (VGR = Rf/R₀) and are quantifiable with Thermo Fisher Avizo 3D reconstruction from stereo-SEM pairs. Cleaner steels (lower inclusion content) produce fewer, larger dimples; dirty steels produce dense fine dimples.

Thermo Fisher Apreo combines SE/BSE imaging with Oxford Instruments EDS and EBSD for correlative fractography. EDS identifies inclusion chemistry (MnS, Al₂O₃, TiN) at dimple bases; EBSD maps crystallographic misorientation at dimple walls revealing local plastic deformation.

Cleavage Fracture — SEM Features & Identification

Cleavage fracture produces flat, crystallographically-controlled surfaces on specific low-index planes ({001} BCC, {0001} HCP). The key SEM features — river marks, feather markings, tongue marks, and cleavage steps — allow origin identification and stress-state reconstruction. Thermo Fisher Phenom XL desktop SEM resolves cleavage facets at 15–30× to 50,000×.

Feature 1 of 5

River Marks

River marks are fine parallel lines on a cleavage facet that converge in the direction opposite to crack propagation — following river marks "downstream" leads to the origin grain. They form when the crack front crosses a low-angle twist boundary between adjacent parallel cleavage planes, creating a step that appears as a line in SEM. Step height = offset between cleavage planes ≈ 1–100 nm.

Convergence direction — rivers flow toward origin; diverge away
Step height — 1–100 nm; visible in BSE at >5,000×
TFS tip: Tilt specimen 45° for best river mark contrast; use 5–10 kV

Thermo Fisher Apreo S LoVac; 5 kV SE2; BCC ferritic steel

Cleavage Feature Gallery — All Five Key Signatures

Feather markings — fine herringbone pattern branching from central spine; propagation → spine tip; common in low-carbon steel at low T
Chevron pattern — macroscale herringbone; apex points to origin; entire grain may show single chevron
Tongue marks — tongue-shaped depressions formed when crack front intersects a mechanical twin; seen in high-rate impact fractures and HCP metals
Grain boundary step — cleavage plane misorientation across grain boundary produces a step; river marks form to accommodate misfit

Cleavage vs. Intergranular Fracture — SEM Discrimination

Both cleavage and intergranular fracture produce bright, flat surfaces at low magnification — SEM is required to discriminate. Cleavage shows river marks within a grain facet; intergranular fracture follows grain boundaries producing a "rock candy" faceted topology without intra-facet river marks.

Fatigue Striations — SEM Identification & Crack Growth Rate

Fatigue striations are the highest-resolution fracture evidence available without TEM. In the Forsyth–Laird model, each striation corresponds to one load cycle: the crack tip blunts on loading and re-sharpens on unloading, advancing by one striation spacing per cycle. Striation spacing = da/dN and varies from ~10 nm (near threshold) to ~10 μm (high ΔK). Thermo Fisher Apreo FEG-SEM at 50,000–100,000× resolves near-threshold striations in steel and aluminum.

Near-Threshold Striations — Fine Spacing (ΔK ≈ 5 MPa√m)

Near the fatigue threshold ΔKth, striation spacing is 10–100 nm — at or near the TEM resolution limit for SEM. Thermo Fisher Apreo at 100 kV FEG with immersion lens mode achieves 1–2 nm resolution, resolving individual striations. The fracture surface appears nearly flat with very fine, closely-spaced parallel ridges perpendicular to the local crack propagation direction. Secondary cracks are absent.

Striation spacing ≈ 10–100 nm; measure ≥20 striations and average for da/dN
Crack direction — perpendicular to striations; striations are parallel ridges
No secondary cracks — low ΔK; Stage II planar propagation
TFS setting: 5 kV SE; immersion mode; beam deceleration for surface contrast

Source: Forsyth–Laird model; TFS Apreo SEM application note AN0052

Striation Counting Protocol — da/dN from Fracture Surface

Striation counting is an ASTM E1823-defined measurement technique. The procedure requires calibrated SEM magnification (NIST traceable), a minimum of 20 striation measurements per location, and multiple locations per crack growth increment. Results are compared to Paris-law data (da/dN vs. ΔK) to validate or reconstruct loading history.

Step-by-Step Counting Procedure

1. Locate region — known distance from initiation site; measure with vernier stage encoder
2. Identify striations — parallel ridges ⊥ to crack growth; must be distinguishable from machining marks or surface steps
3. Draw intercept line — draw a line ∥ to crack growth direction; count intersections with striations over known length L
4. Calculate spacing — s = L / N; repeat at 3+ orientations; report mean ± 1σ
5. Paris law check — compare s to da/dN = C(ΔK)^m; estimate ΔK at that crack length
