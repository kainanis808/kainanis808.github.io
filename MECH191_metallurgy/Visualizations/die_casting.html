Die Casting

Hot & cold chamber processes, NADCA design guidelines, and alloy selection

Data sources: North American Die Casting Association (NADCA) Product Standards & Engineering · NADCA Design Guidelines (8th ed.)

What Is Die Casting?

Die casting forces molten metal into a hardened steel die (tool) under high pressure (7–200 MPa). The metal fills the cavity in milliseconds, solidifies rapidly under pressure, and is ejected by pins. It yields the tightest tolerances and smoothest surfaces of any casting process — at the cost of high tooling investment and limitation to lower-melting alloys. Two variants exist: hot chamber (injection mechanism submerged in molten metal — used for Zn, Mg, Pb) and cold chamber (metal is ladled separately — used for Al, Cu, Mg).

Surface Finish vs. Other Casting Processes

NADCA standard surface finish for die casting is 32–125 µin Ra (0.8–3.2 µm) as-cast, with best achievable ~16 µin Ra (0.4 µm) on polished die faces. This is 4–10× smoother than sand casting.

NADCA Dimensional Tolerance Chart

NADCA "basic" tolerance = ±0.001 in per inch of dimension for aluminum; additional ±0.001 in across the parting line or any moving die component. Die casting tolerances tighten further with precision CNC die machining and controlled shot parameters.
