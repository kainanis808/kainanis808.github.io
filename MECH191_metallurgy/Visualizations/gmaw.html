Transition Current: The boundary between short-circuit and spray transfer depends on wire composition and diameter, and shielding gas. For 0.045 in ER70S-6 in Ar/CO₂ (C25), transition occurs near 220–250 A. Below this, globular or short-circuit mode dominates even with high voltage. Pulsed power sources circumvent this by driving brief peak pulses above transition current while keeping the average current below it — enabling spray-like droplet detachment at lower average heat inputs.

Lincoln Electric GMAW Welding Guide pp. 14–18; Miller Electric Pulsed MIG Welding Application Guide; AWS C5.6:2003 §4.1.

Droplet Transfer Mechanism — Animated Detail

Force Balance on a Droplet

Kim & Eagar (1993) Weld. J. 72(7) 269-s – 278-s; Rhee & Kannatey-Asibu (1992) Weld. J. 71(11) 381-s – 386-s. Electromagnetic (Lorentz) pinch and gravity drive detachment; surface tension resists it. In spray mode, electromagnetic pinch dominates, producing fine axial droplets.

Short-Circuit Cycle Phases

Lincoln Electric GMAW Welding Guide; Modenesi & de Abreu (1999) cycle-time analysis. Typical cycle: arc phase (wire melts) → short phase (contact, surface tension pulls) → arc reignition. Inductance setting controls di/dt at reignition to limit spatter.

Shielding Gas Selection

Effect of CO₂ Content on Transfer Mode

Increasing CO₂ raises the transition current and oxidizing potential. Above ~20% CO₂, spray transfer requires very high currents and produces more spatter. Pure CO₂ is limited to short-circuit or globular.
