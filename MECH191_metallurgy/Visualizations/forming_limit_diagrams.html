How to Read an FLD

Axes:
X-axis: Minor strain ε₂ — strain in the direction perpendicular to the major strain (in the plane of the sheet). Negative = compression; positive = stretch.

Y-axis: Major strain ε₁ — largest principal strain in the plane of the sheet (always ≥ 0 in sheet forming). Strain is engineering (true) strain: ε = ln(l/l₀).
Forming Limit Curve (FLC):
The FLC is the boundary between safe (no neck/fracture) and failure. Points below the FLC: safe. Points above: through-thickness necking → fracture.

FLC₀ = FLC value at plane strain (ε₂ = 0) — the minimum of the curve and the critical limit for most stamping operations. A useful empirical estimate:
FLC₀ ≈ (23.3 + 360·t) · n / 0.21
where t = sheet thickness (mm), n = strain hardening exponent.
Wrinkling zone (bottom-left, ε₂ < −ε₁/2):
When the compressive minor strain exceeds half the major strain, the sheet buckles out of plane (wrinkles). Wrinkles are controlled by blank holder force — too little BHF → wrinkling; too much BHF → tearing.
Failure modes summary:
• Tearing/fracture — strain point above FLC. Reduce blank holder force, increase lubrication, change material, redesign geometry.
• Wrinkling — strain below the wrinkling limit. Increase blank holder force.
• Safe — strain below FLC and above wrinkling limit. Aim for a safety margin of 10–15% below FLC.

Effect of n-value on FLC:
Higher strain hardening exponent n → higher FLC₀ and steeper FLC. Materials with high n (304 SS, brass) distribute strain more evenly, delaying localized necking. Low-n materials (DP600, AA6061) have flatter FLC curves and tighter forming windows.

Effect of r̄ (Lankford coefficient / normal anisotropy):
High r̄ means the material resists thinning relative to in-plane flow. IF steel (r̄ ≈ 1.8) is the benchmark deep-drawing material — the high r̄ allows large cup draws without tearing through the base.

Strain Paths for Common Forming Operations

Deep Drawing

Blank is drawn into a cylindrical cup. Flange is in compression (ε₂ < 0), wall is under uniaxial-like tension. Strain path: ε₂ ≈ −ε₁/2 (uniaxial tension path). Cup bottom: close to balanced biaxial. Failure: tearing at cup radius; wrinkling in flange.

Stretch Forming

Sheet stretched over a punch with all edges clamped. Plane strain to biaxial stretch (ε₂ > 0). Passes through the most critical (lowest FLC) region at plane strain. Highest risk of tearing at punch nose.

Hole Expansion

A pre-punched hole is expanded by a conical punch. Strain state: uniaxial tension in hoop direction, zero in radial (plane stress at hole edge). Strain path along left branch of FLD. Sensitive to edge quality (shear damage from punching).

Ironing

Cup wall is thinned by drawing through a die with controlled clearance < wall thickness. Large compressive strain through thickness, tensile in draw direction. Effectively ε₂ ≈ −ε₁. Far left of FLD; friction and lubrication are critical.

Equi-biaxial Stretch

Hydraulic bulge or dome stretch: ε₁ = ε₂ (equal biaxial). Strain path goes up the right branch of FLD at 45°. High FLC on right branch — but if thickness is not uniform, necking can initiate at thinner zones at lower strains than the FLC predicts.

Bending

Pure bending: outer surface in tension, inner in compression. Strain state: plane strain (ε₂ ≈ 0) at surface. FLC is most critical at plane strain minimum. Minimum bend radius = t·(50/r-1) using n-value correlation. Springback significant for HSS.

Strain path dependence: The FLC is path-dependent — a material that follows the deep draw path (left branch) can sustain higher major strains than one that follows the plane strain path. Pre-straining in one direction reduces the available FLC in another (Bauschinger effect and texture evolution). Simulation tools (AutoForm, PAM-STAMP) track incremental strain paths for complex stampings.

How to use FLD data in practice:
1. Simulate first: Run sheet metal simulation (FEM) to predict strain distribution. Extract major/minor strain at each element.
2. Plot on FLD: Overlay simulation strain points on the FLD for the chosen material and thickness.
3. Apply safety margin: Target all strains to be >10% below the FLC (shift FLC down by 10% for the "safety FLC").
4. Identify failure zones: Points above the FLC → tearing risk → redesign die geometry, change blank shape, add draw beads, adjust BHF.
5. Check for wrinkling: Points in ε₂ < −ε₁/2 region → increase blank holder force or add bead restraint.
