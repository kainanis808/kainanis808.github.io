What Is Investment Casting?

Investment casting (lost-wax casting) is one of the oldest known metalworking processes — traced to 3000 BC in ancient Egypt and China. A wax replica of the desired part is coated in multiple layers of ceramic slurry and coarse sand (the "investment") to form a shell mold. The wax is then melted or flash-fired out, leaving a hollow ceramic cavity that exactly replicates the wax surface. Molten metal is poured in, the shell is broken away, and the casting is finished.

The result is the most dimensionally accurate, smoothest, and geometrically complex casting achievable — with zero draft required, internal undercuts possible, and surface finishes approaching machined quality. The tradeoff is process time (1–3 weeks per batch) and higher per-part cost.

Process Steps (ICI Sequence)

1

Wax Injection

Wax (or plastic) injected under pressure into precision metal dies at 60–80 °C to form exact replicas of the finished part.

2

Pattern Assembly

Individual wax patterns are welded (heat or solvent) to a central wax sprue to form a "tree" — typically 10–100 parts per cluster.

3

Shell Building

Tree dipped in ceramic slurry, drained, coated in refractory stucco, and dried. Repeated 7–12 times over 3–10 days. First 2–3 coats use fine zircon for surface finish; later coats use coarser material for strength.

4

Dewaxing

Wax removed in a flash-fire autoclave (~350 °C steam) or direct-flame oven. The shell expands slightly before the wax, preventing cracking — a key Hitchiner innovation.

5

Shell Burnout

Empty shell fired at 900–1100 °C to burn residual wax, sinter the ceramic, and pre-heat the mold for pour. Eliminates gas-generating organics.

6

Metal Pour

Molten metal poured into the hot shell (gravity, vacuum, or counter-gravity). Hot shell improves fluidity and reduces thermal shock on thin sections.

7

Shell Removal

Ceramic broken away by vibration, water blasting, or chemical leaching (for complex internal passages). Individual parts visible on tree.

8

Cutoff & Finishing

Parts cut from tree with abrasive wheel. Gate stubs ground flush. NDT inspection (FPI, X-ray), heat treatment, machining of critical features if required.

Surface Finish vs. Other Casting Processes

Investment casting produces the finest as-cast surface of all casting processes. ICI standard finish is 32–125 µin Ra (0.8–3.2 µm); best achievable with polished dies is ~16 µin Ra (0.4 µm).

ICI Dimensional Tolerance Chart

ICI Classes 1–3: Class 1 = general commercial; Class 2 = precision (most common); Class 3 = close tolerance (special dies, controlled conditions). Tolerance applies to individual dimensions within one die half; across-parting-line and core dimensions carry an additional allowance.

ICI Casting Design Principles

Investment casting design freedom far exceeds other casting processes: zero draft, free undercuts, thin walls, small holes, and complex internal passages are all achievable. Why Investment Casting Dominates Superalloy & Titanium Processing

Because the mold is ceramic and sacrificial, investment casting imposes no die-material temperature limit. This makes it the only practical near-net-shape route for nickel superalloys (turbine blades), titanium alloys (aerospace structural parts), and cobalt alloys (surgical implants).
