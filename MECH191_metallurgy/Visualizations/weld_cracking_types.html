Lincoln Electric — Weld Discontinuity Reference

The Three Principal Weld Cracking Mechanisms

Weld cracking can occur during solidification (hot cracking), after cooling (cold cracking), or through the base metal thickness under through-thickness weld stress (lamellar tearing). Each has a distinct mechanism, location, temperature range, and set of causes. Correct identification drives the right prevention strategy.

Hot Cracking — Solidification
Solidification crack — centerline of weld; forms as dendrites solidify and inter-dendritic liquid film tears under shrinkage stress
HAZ liquation crack — at fusion line; grain boundary liquation in HAZ during high heat input
Temperature range — occurs at solidification temp (~1400°C for carbon steel); crack is open at high temperature
Cold Cracking — HIC / Hydrogen-Induced
Underbead crack — in HAZ parallel to fusion line; most common HIC location in susceptible microstructure
Toe crack — initiates at weld toe stress concentration; propagates into plate under residual stress
Delayed fracture — occurs <150°C, often hours or days after welding; requires susceptible microstructure + H + stress simultaneously
Lamellar Tearing — Z-Direction
Stepped crack — staircase morphology below fusion line; terraces along MnS stringers, risers through ferrite
MnS inclusions — elongated parallel to rolling direction; low through-thickness ductility (%RAZ)
Through-thickness stress — weld shrinkage forces act in Z-direction (through plate); worst in T-joints and corner joints

AWS — Weld Defect Visual Comparison Reference

Six Principal Weld Crack Types — Location & Morphology

AWS D1.1, AWS B4.0, and the AWS Welding Inspection Handbook categorise weld cracks by location and orientation. Understanding where each crack appears in a cross-section — and how it looks — guides both non-destructive testing method selection and root cause analysis.

Centreline Crack

Cross-section view. Crack splits weld bead down the centre. Hot cracking mechanism. High S/P; concave bead; high restraint.

Crater Crack

Plan view (top). Star crack at weld end crater. Hot cracking; insufficient crater fill at arc stop. Prevent: backfill crater before stopping arc.

Transverse Crack

Longitudinal section (side view). Crack perpendicular to weld axis. Hot or cold; high restraint transverse to weld; high-strength weld metal.

Underbead Crack

Cross-section view. HAZ crack parallel to fusion line. Cold cracking (HIC); requires hydrogen + susceptible martensite + tensile stress. Most common HIC form.

Toe Crack

Cross-section view. Initiates at weld toe stress concentration (Kt ≈ 2–4). Cold cracking; propagates diagonally into plate under residual stress. Surface-breaking — detectable by MT/PT.

Root Crack

Cross-section view. At weld root — highest restraint location and often smallest cross-section. Cold or hot. Groove welds: insufficient root opening or penetration. Fillet welds: lack of fusion at root.

TWI — The Welding Institute — Lamellar Tearing Guide

Lamellar Tearing — Mechanism, Stepped Morphology & Inclusion Role

TWI's research on lamellar tearing established the role of elongated MnS inclusions as the primary material factor. Weld shrinkage loads act through the plate thickness (Z-direction). Where inclusions provide planes of weakness parallel to the plate surface, cracks propagate along inclusions (terraces) and jump between bands (risers), creating the characteristic stepped or staircase fracture path below the HAZ.

Three Conditions for Lamellar Tearing (TWI)

Through-thickness stress — weld shrinkage loading acting in Z-direction; worst in T-joints, corner joints, cruciform joints where the plate is loaded through its thickness
Low Z-direction ductility — %RAZ <20% (measured by Z-test, BS 6443 / ISO 7778); caused by elongated MnS, oxide, or silicate inclusions parallel to plate rolling surface
Location below HAZ — crack initiates just below the fusion line in the base metal; HAZ thermal cycle reduces inclusion–matrix bond strength further

Fractographic Features (TWI / ASM Vol. 11)

Terrace — flat, elongated fracture surface parallel to plate surface; corresponds to crack propagation along one MnS stringer band; woody or fibrous texture in SEM
Riser — short, steep fracture segment connecting two terraces; ductile dimple fracture through the ferritic matrix between stringer bands
MnS inclusions — identified by SEM/EDS; grey, elongated ellipsoidal morphology in unetched cross-section; decohesion at MnS–ferrite interface initiates terrace
