CNC Turning — Sandvik Coromant & Haas

Sandvik — Primary Formulas

Vc = π · D · n / 1000 [m/min]
n = Vc · 1000 / (π · D) [rpm]
vf = fn · n [mm/min]
MRR = ap · fn · Vc [cm³/min ÷1000]
Pc = Fc · Vc / (60 000 · η) [kW]

η = machine efficiency (~0.80). Fc = kc · ap · fn. Use Pc to check against machine spindle power.

Haas — Machine Operation Tips

Tool offsets (G41/G42): Always set geometry offset (wear offset = 0 at start). Measure tool with probe or edge-find — enter actual Z and X values. Wrong offset = crash or scrap.
Work coordinate: G54 is standard first work offset. Set Z0 at part face. G28 (machine home) before tool change — never skip.
First-part caution: Run at 50% feed and speed override until proven. Single-block mode (OB key on Haas) steps one block at a time — use for first article.
Chip control: Chip breaker geometry on insert selection matters as much as grade. Break chips before they wrap around the part or spindle. Use coolant at 60–80 PSI directed at cutting zone.
Facing passes: Always face from OD to center (positive X to X0). This ensures chip exits — facing center-out causes built-up chip.

Insert Shape & Application Reference

Insert Grade Logic (ISO P/M/K/N/S/H)

First digit = ISO group. Higher number = tougher (more cobalt, coarser grain). Use harder grades (P10, K10) at high Vc; use tougher grades (P40) for interrupted cuts, scale, or unstable setups.

Chip Breaker Selection

Finishing (F): Sharp edge, positive rake — use at low fn (0.05–0.15 mm/rev). Medium (M): General purpose 0.10–0.30. Roughing (R): Negative chamfer, handles heavy DOC and hard scale. Match chip breaker geometry to fn range shown in manufacturer chart.

Haas — Insert Indexing

Index or replace inserts at the first sign of wear marks visible to the naked eye — don't wait for surface finish to degrade. Track insert life per part, not by time. Use Haas tool life management (T offset page) to preset index count alerts.

CNC Milling — Sandvik Coromant & Haas

Sandvik — Milling Formulas

Vc = π · Dc · n / 1000 [m/min]
n = Vc · 1000 / (π · Dc) [rpm]
vf = fz · z · n [mm/min] (table feed)
MRR = ae · ap · vf / 1000 [cm³/min]
hm = fz · √(ae / Dc) (chip thinning, ae < Dc/2)

hm = mean chip thickness. When ae < Dc/2, real chip is thinner than fz — compensate by increasing fz to maintain recommended hm. Haas: always program vf (mm/min), not fz directly.

fz_adj = fz_rec · √(Dc / ae) (for ae < Dc/2)

Toolpath Strategies

Climb vs. Conventional Milling

Climb milling (chip thick-to-thin): cutter rotation same direction as feed. Chip is deposited behind the cutter — better surface finish, lower cutting temperature, less work hardening. Use for all finishing; standard for most operations on CNC.

Conventional milling (chip thin-to-thick): cutter rotation opposite to feed. Rubbing at chip entry — more heat, work hardening, BUE risk. Use only when backlash is a concern (rare on modern CNC).

Trochoidal (High-Efficiency) Milling

Small radial engagement (5–15% Dc) with full axial depth of cut. Cutter follows circular arc paths — keeps chip load consistent, prevents heat buildup, allows much higher feed rates. Ideal for hardened or difficult materials where full-width slotting would overheat the tool.

Software: HSMWorks, Mastercam "Dynamic Mill", Fusion 360 "Adaptive Clearing", Hypermill Maxx. Haas: use CAM-generated trochoidal G-code — do not program manually.

Haas — Milling Setup Tips

Tool runout: Check with DTI in spindle. >0.01 mm runout doubles effective chip load variation — degrades tool life and surface finish.
G54 work offset: Use Haas probe (if equipped) or edge-finder. Set X0, Y0 at part datum; Z0 at top of stock.
Pecking: For deep slots, use incremental ap (peck milling) to clear chips — program Z increments in the CAM or use canned cycle G73/G83 adapted for milling.
Cutter comp (G41/G42): Apply on finish passes only. Lead in/out with arc moves — never engage cutter comp in a straight plunge.

Corner Radius vs. Sharp Corner

Corner radius (bull-nose end mill) significantly extends tool life vs sharp corners — stress concentration at a sharp corner limits depth of cut. Helical Solutions recommends: use the largest corner radius that part geometry allows. Typical: 0.2–2 mm radius on general-purpose end mills.

For inside corners: the minimum radius achievable = tool radius. Always design with fillet ≥ tool radius + 10% clearance.

Surface Finish — Sandvik & Haas

Turning Surface Roughness

Ra (μm) ≈ fn² / (8 · rε) × 1000

Theoretical Ra for a single-point turning tool with nose radius rε. Actual Ra is 1.5–2× theoretical due to vibration, BUE, and chip re-cutting. Reducing fn by half reduces Ra by 4×.

Milling Surface Roughness

Ra (μm) ≈ ae² / (8 · R) × 1000 (ball end)
Ra (μm) ≈ fz² / (8 · rε) × 1000 (flat end, step-over)

For a ball-end mill, ae (step-over) and ball radius R determine the scallop height. For a flat end mill with corner radius rε, it is the feed per tooth fz that controls Ra in the feed direction.

Factors Affecting Actual Surface Finish

Vibration & Chatter

Chatter produces characteristic waviness (chatter marks) perpendicular to feed. Caused by regenerative vibration — the tool cuts a wavy surface left by the previous pass.

Fixes (Haas/Sandvik):

  • Reduce ap or ae (less cutting force)
  • Reduce Vc in 10% steps until stable
  • Shorten tool stickout (most effective — rigidity ∝ 1/L³)
  • Use anti-vibration toolholder (Sandvik Silent Tools)
  • Change spindle speed ±15% (shift frequency off resonance)
  • Climb mill instead of conventional
  • Add damping mass to workpiece fixture

Built-Up Edge (BUE)

Work material cold-welds to rake face at low-to-medium speeds. Periodically fractures and tears the machined surface — produces poor Ra and irregular finish.

Solutions: Increase Vc past the BUE speed (material-dependent: typically >150 m/min for steel, >250 m/min for aluminum). Use cutting fluid, increase rake angle, use coated insert (TiN reduces adhesion).

Haas — Finishing Pass Strategy

Leave stock for finish: Rough to 0.2–0.5 mm stock, then finish. Prevents heat distortion from affecting final dimension.
Coolant mode: Flood coolant for steel/stainless. Air blast for cast iron (coolant causes thermal shock to insert). Through-spindle for deep bores.
Spindle warm-up: Run spindle 5–10 min at operating speed before taking critical dimension cuts — thermal growth of spindle otherwise shifts Z by 5–30 μm.
Wiper inserts: Sandvik Wiper geometry has a secondary flat behind the nose radius — allows 2× feed rate for same Ra. Use for finishing passes where MRR matters.

Difficult Materials — Harvey Tool & Helical Solutions

Difficult-to-machine materials require systematic parameter reduction and process changes. The chart below shows Vc and fn as a percentage of equivalent steel baseline (AISI 1045, Vc=200 m/min turning).

Stainless Steel (304, 316, 17-4)

ISO MWork-hardens severely

Turning (Sandvik GC2035): Vc 120–200 m/min. Maintain positive chip contact — never dwell or allow rubbing. fn min 0.08 mm/rev to cut under the work-hardened layer. Flood coolant essential.

Milling (Helical Solutions): Use 4-flute end mill with sharp AlTiN coating. Climb mill only. Trochoidal preferred at ae=8–12% Dc. fz: maintain hm ≥ 0.04 mm — too light a cut rubs instead of cuts. Vc 80–150 m/min.

Harvey Tool tip: For 17-4 PH (H900/H1025): treat as alloy steel in H1025 condition. In H900, reduce parameters an additional 20%.

Titanium (Ti-6Al-4V, Grade 5)

ISO SLow thermal conductivity

The key rule: Keep Vc LOW, keep feed HIGH. Heat cannot escape through the workpiece (κ ≈ 7 W/m·K vs steel 50) — all heat goes into the tool. Low Vc = low heat generation rate.

Turning: Vc 50–80 m/min. fn 0.10–0.20 mm/rev. Use sharp uncoated or PVD-TiAlN inserts. Never stop feed while spindle runs — BUE and weld instantly.

Milling (Helical Solutions): 3–4 flute, 38–40° helix, large core diameter. ae = 5–10% Dc, full ap (trochoidal). fz maintain 0.03–0.06 mm. Flood coolant at high pressure (70+ PSI) — directed precisely at cutting edge.

Inconel 718 & Nickel Superalloys

ISO SHardest to machine

Turning: Two regimes — (1) Carbide at low Vc (30–60 m/min), fn 0.05–0.15 mm/rev — flood coolant, sharp positive geometry. (2) Ceramic at high Vc (200–400 m/min) — no coolant (thermal shock), use SiAlON or whisker-reinforced Al₂O₃. Interrupted cuts: carbide only.

Milling (Harvey Tool): Maximum rigidity — minimize stickout, use shrink-fit or hydraulic chuck. Vc 35–55 m/min. Trochoidal essential. Through-spindle coolant at 1000+ PSI preferred. Index inserts/replace end mills frequently — catastrophic failure occurs fast with worn tools.

Critical: Never stop the cutter in contact with Inconel. Always retract before stopping spindle — the tool will weld to the part.

Aluminum (6061, 7075, 2024)

ISO NHigh speed, sticky chips

Turning: Vc 500–2000 m/min. fn up to 0.5 mm/rev. Use uncoated carbide or PCD (polycrystalline diamond). Positive rake (+15° to +20°). Chip control: use chipbreaker geometry to prevent long stringy chips wrapping around spindle.

Milling (Helical Solutions): 2–3 flute end mill with large chip gullets and polished flutes (prevents aluminum adhesion). Vc 600–3000 m/min depending on machine. High helix angle (40–45°). Mist coolant or flood.

Harvey Tool tip (7075): 7075-T6 is significantly harder than 6061 — use parameters closer to steel. Avoid sharp corners that stress the material and cause tear-out.

Hardened Steel (45–65 HRC)

ISO H

Turning: CBN inserts (CB7015/CB7025) or Al₂O₃ ceramics. No coolant — thermal shock cracks inserts. Vc 80–200 m/min depending on hardness. Very light DOC (ap 0.1–0.5 mm). Rigid setup mandatory — BN inserts chip instantly under vibration.

Milling (Harvey Tool): Use AlTiN-coated ball-end or bull-nose end mills (no sharp corners). Climb mill only. ae 5–10% Dc, ap 0.1–0.5 mm, Vc 80–180 m/min for carbide. Trochoidal with constant chip load. Finish to Ra 0.4–0.8 μm to approach grinding quality.

Helical Solutions — Universal Rules

1. Rigidity first. Reduce tool stickout before reducing parameters. A 2× reduction in overhang = 8× increase in stiffness.

2. Never rub. Always maintain minimum chip load (hm ≥ 0.02–0.04 mm). Rubbing generates heat without cutting — accelerates wear instantly.

3. Trochoidal by default for difficult materials. Constant chip load + chip evacuation > conventional slotting in all difficult materials.

4. Replace proactively. In difficult materials, worn tools fail catastrophically. Budget for aggressive tool change intervals (track by part, not time).

5. Coating matters. TiAlN for heat resistance (steel, stainless); AlTiN for extreme heat (Ti, Inconel); diamond-like carbon (DLC) for aluminum adhesion; TiB₂ for silicon aluminum.
