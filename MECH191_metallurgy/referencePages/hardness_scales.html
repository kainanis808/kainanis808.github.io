Hardness Testing Scales

Rockwell · Brinell · Vickers — methods, scales, and cross-reference

Hardness is a material's resistance to permanent surface deformation. It is one of the most commonly measured mechanical properties because it is fast, inexpensive, and non-destructive (leaving only a small indentation). The three dominant industrial scales — Rockwell, Brinell, and Vickers — each use a different indenter geometry and measurement principle, making them suited to different materials and applications.

Indenter Types

Each method uses a standardized indenter pressed into the surface under a controlled load. The indenter geometry determines what you measure — depth of penetration or the size of the residual impression.

💎
Brale (Diamond Cone)
120° sphero-conical diamond tip. Used in Rockwell scales A, C, D. Suited for hard steels and carbides.
⚪
Steel / Carbide Ball
1/16″ (1.588 mm) ball for Rockwell B/F/G. 10 mm ball for Brinell. Suited for softer metals.
🔷
Vickers Pyramid
Square-based diamond pyramid, 136° included face angle. Leaves a square indentation — diagonal length is measured optically.
🔹
Knoop Pyramid
Elongated diamond pyramid. Used for microhardness of thin coatings or brittle materials. Elongated impression reduces cracking risk.

Rockwell Hardness (HR)

Rockwell measures the depth of penetration under a major load relative to a baseline set by a minor (preload) force. The result is read directly from the machine dial — no optical measurement is needed, making it the fastest and most common shop-floor test.

Rockwell Number (all scales)
HR = N − (h / s)
N = scale constant  |  h = permanent depth increase (mm) after removing major load  |  s = scale factor (0.002 mm/division)

In practice: a minor load of 10 kgf sets the zero reference, the major load is applied and removed, and the machine reads the residual depth as a hardness number. Higher number = shallower indent = harder material.

HRC is the most commonly referenced scale for steels. Carburized gears, tool steels, and heat-treated shafts are almost always reported in HRC. HRB covers soft steels and non-ferrous alloys that would be too soft for HRC (indenter would bottom out).

Superficial Rockwell scales (15N, 30N, 45N) use a 3 kgf minor load instead of 10 kgf and are intended for thin sections and coatings where standard loads would punch through.

Brinell Hardness (HB)

Brinell uses a large hardened steel or tungsten carbide ball pressed into the surface under a specified load. The diameter of the resulting circular indentation is measured optically with a calibrated microscope, and hardness is calculated from the load and contact area. Because the indentation is large, Brinell averages over a bigger volume of material — making it ideal for coarse-grained materials like cast iron and forgings.

Brinell Hardness Number (HB)
HB = 2F / [ π D ( D − √(D² − d²) ) ]
F = applied load (kgf)  |  D = ball diameter (mm)  |  d = indentation diameter (mm)

The standard test uses a 10 mm ball. Load is chosen based on expected hardness — harder materials need higher loads to produce a measurable indent. The ratio F/D² should be kept constant to maintain geometric similarity.

Standard Brinell Test Conditions

Material Class Load F (kgf) F/D² Ratio Ball Diameter D (mm) Typical HB Range
Steel, cast iron 3000 30 10 100–650
Copper & copper alloys 500 5 10 35–200
Aluminum & alloys 500 5 10 20–150
Lead, tin, soft metals 25–62.5 1 5 or 10 2–30

Upper limit ~650 HB — above this, the ball deforms and results are unreliable. Use Rockwell or Vickers for very hard materials.

Brinell is widely used for castings and forgings because its large indentation averages out local microstructural variation (e.g., graphite flakes in grey cast iron). It is also commonly used for incoming material inspection of steel bar stock.

Brinell ↔ Tensile Strength Approximation (Steel)

For carbon and low-alloy steels, ultimate tensile strength (UTS) correlates approximately with Brinell hardness:

Empirical approximation — steels only
UTS (MPa) ≈ 3.45 × HB     |     UTS (psi) ≈ 500 × HB

This approximation works reasonably well for HB 100–400. Do not use for stainless steels, non-ferrous alloys, or very high-hardness steels.

Vickers Hardness (HV)

Vickers uses a square-based diamond pyramid (136° included angle between opposite faces) pressed into the surface. The test leaves a small square indentation; both diagonals are measured optically and averaged. Because the pyramid geometry is self-similar at all scales, the Vickers scale is continuous — one scale covers everything from pure lead to diamond-like coatings without switching indenters or scales.

Vickers Hardness Number (HV)
HV = 1.854 × F / d²
F = applied load (kgf)  |  d = mean diagonal of indentation (mm)

Load Ranges

Category Load Range (gf) Designation Example Typical Use
Macro Vickers 1 – 120 kgf HV 10, HV 30 Bulk hardness of metals, large cross-sections
Low-force Vickers 200 – 1000 gf HV 0.2, HV 1 Case-hardening depth profiles, weld HAZ mapping
Microhardness 1 – 200 gf HV 0.01, HV 0.1 Individual grains, thin coatings, PVD/CVD films

Load must be reported with the result (e.g., "650 HV 10" means 650 HV at a 10 kgf load). Values obtained at different loads may not be identical — always specify the load.

Vickers is the preferred scale for case depth measurement. A series of indentations at increasing depths from the surface reveals the hardness gradient through a carburized or nitrided case, down into the soft core. This is not practical with Rockwell due to the larger indentation footprint.

Choosing the Right Scale

Situation Recommended Scale Reason
Hardened steel part (shafts, gears, dies) HRC Fast, no optical measurement, industry standard for hard steels
Soft steel, copper, aluminum HRB HRC indenter would bottom out; HRB uses a ball at lower load
Casting or forging (coarse grain) HB Large indent averages over microstructural variation
Carburized / nitrided case depth profile HV Small indent allows measurements at known depths from surface
Thin coating or PVD film HV (microhardness) Ultra-low loads (<100 gf) avoid substrate influence
Very hard material (carbide, ceramic) HRA or HV HRB/HRC balls deform; diamond indenters are needed
Brittle material or thin cross-section Knoop (HK) Elongated impression reduces cracking and allows shallow depths
Research / universal comparison HV Single continuous scale; no scale-switch artifacts for cross-material comparison
