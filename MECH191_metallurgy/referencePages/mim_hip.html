1  Metal Injection Moulding (MIM) — Process Overview

Metal Injection Moulding combines the geometric freedom of plastic injection moulding with the material properties of wrought or PM metals. The process is capable of producing complex, three-dimensional net-shape or near-net-shape parts in quantities of thousands to millions per year — at wall thicknesses and internal features that are impractical by conventional machining or die casting. Typical part weight range is 0.1 g to ~300 g.

Fine metal powder (particle size <20 µm, often <10 µm for best sintered density) is blended with a thermoplastic or wax-based binder system at 40–45 vol% metal. The resulting feedstock is pelletized and fed into a conventional injection moulding machine. After moulding, the binder is removed in a debinding step, leaving a porous "brown part" that is then sintered to near-full density. The overall linear shrinkage is 15–20%, which must be accounted for in tooling design.

Why finer powder? MIM uses powder significantly finer than conventional die-compaction PM (<20 µm vs. typically 60–150 µm). The high surface area increases sintering driving force (surface energy minimization), enabling near-full density at sintering temperatures that would be insufficient for coarser powder. The trade-off is higher powder cost and greater sensitivity to oxygen pick-up during handling.

Dimensional control: Because sintering shrinkage (15–20% linear) must be uniform and predictable, feedstock formulation and powder loading are tightly controlled to ±0.2 vol%. Density gradients in the moulded green part caused by non-uniform flow or weld lines translate directly into differential shrinkage and warpage after sintering.

2  Hot Isostatic Pressing (HIP) — Process Overview

Hot Isostatic Pressing subjects a part or powder compact to simultaneously high temperature and high isostatic gas pressure, applied uniformly in all directions through an inert gas medium (typically argon). The combination of temperature-activated diffusion and pressure-driven pore closure eliminates residual porosity that sintering alone cannot remove, producing components at or near theoretical density (>99.5%).

HIP was developed in the 1950s at Battelle Memorial Institute for diffusion bonding nuclear fuel rods, and has since become a critical manufacturing step for aerospace castings, powder metallurgy products, and MIM parts requiring maximum mechanical performance. Bodycote is the world's largest provider of HIP services, operating HIP vessels across North America, Europe, and Asia.

Why argon? Argon is the standard HIP pressure medium because it is chemically inert with essentially all metals and ceramics, and has a molecular size too large to diffuse into closed pores under typical HIP conditions. If the atmosphere penetrated pores, those pores would be filled with pressurized gas after cooling and could not be eliminated — a phenomenon known as repressurization. Argon also has a high density at operating pressure (~600 kg/m³ at 150 MPa, 1150 °C), enabling efficient pressure transmission.
Open porosity cannot be HIP'd: HIP only closes sealed, closed porosity. Any pore connected to the surface by an open channel will be pressurized by the argon and remain open. For this reason, MIM parts must be fully sintered to >92% density (closing all open pores) before HIP will be effective. Parts with surface-connected porosity must be encapsulated (in glass or metal) before HIP.

3  Before & After Density Data — Sintering vs. Sintering + HIP

HIP consistently elevates the relative density of MIM and PM parts from the typical as-sintered range of 95–98% to >99.5% of theoretical. This 1.5–4.5 percentage-point gain in density translates non-linearly into large improvements in fatigue strength (closed pores are crack initiation sites), elongation, and impact toughness. Key takeaway: HIP reliably delivers mechanical properties that approach or match wrought equivalents, especially for fatigue-critical applications. The largest gains are in elongation and fatigue limit — both governed by pore-initiated cracking. Strength (UTS, YS) gains are typically 5–15%, while ductility and fatigue life improvements can be 30–100%.

4  Bodycote Case Studies — MIM & HIP Applications

Bodycote operates HIP vessels ranging from laboratory scale (300 mm diameter × 600 mm height) to large production units (1650 mm diameter × 3000 mm height), supporting a broad range of part sizes across multiple industry sectors. The following case studies illustrate the application of HIP to MIM and cast components across aerospace, medical, automotive, and oil & gas sectors.

Aerospace

Turbine Blade & Vane Castings — Nickel Superalloy HIP

Investment-cast IN 718 and Waspaloy turbine vanes contain micro-shrinkage porosity from solidification. HIP at 1175 °C / 150 MPa / 4 h (per AMS 2786 / AMS 2774) eliminates subsurface porosity, qualifying cast parts to the same stress-rupture and fatigue life requirements as forgings at significantly lower cost.

MIM Surgical Instrument Jaws — 17-4 PH Stainless Steel

Minimally invasive surgical instrument jaws are produced by MIM from 17-4 PH SS feedstock, achieving complex jaw geometries impossible to machine economically. Oil & Gas

Duplex Stainless Steel Valve Bodies — Casting + HIP

Subsea valve bodies and manifold components in 2205 duplex stainless steel are sand or investment cast, then HIP'd at 1100 °C / 140 MPa / 3 h to eliminate shrinkage porosity before pressure testing to NACE MR0175 sour-service requirements. HIP eliminates porosity-related leak paths and eliminates ~30% of hydrostatic test failures vs. non-HIP'd castings.

Defence / Aerospace

PM Titanium Structural Brackets — Direct HIP Consolidation

Near-net-shape titanium brackets for airframe applications are produced by filling a titanium or steel canister with pre-alloyed Ti-6Al-4V powder, then HIP consolidating at 920 °C / 100 MPa / 3 h. The consolidated billet is machined to final dimensions. This PM-HIP route eliminates forging die cost for low-volume parts and produces a fully isotropic, 100% dense microstructure with no preferred texture from forging.

HIP + Sinter-HIP (Sinter-HIP): Some advanced furnace configurations combine sintering and HIP in a single thermal cycle — the part is sintered under low argon pressure (<0.1 MPa), then at the end of the sintering hold, the vessel is pressurized to full HIP pressure (100–200 MPa) for a dwell period before cooling. Sinter-HIP reduces total cycle time and thermal exposure compared to separate sintering and HIP operations, particularly for cemented carbides and ceramic-matrix composites.
