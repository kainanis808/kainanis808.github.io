Impact & Fatigue Testing

Charpy · Izod · Ductile-Brittle Transition Temperature · S-N Curve

Impact and fatigue testing reveal how materials behave under dynamic and cyclic loading — failure modes that static tensile tests cannot predict. Impact tests quantify a material's toughness (energy absorbed before fracture under a sudden blow), while fatigue tests characterize the stress levels at which repeated cyclic loading causes eventual fracture — often at stresses far below the yield strength.

Impact Testing — Overview

Impact tests measure the energy absorbed when a notched specimen is fractured by a swinging pendulum hammer. The height difference of the pendulum before and after the strike gives the energy consumed — a direct measure of toughness under dynamic, high-strain-rate conditions. The notch artificially concentrates stress, ensuring fracture at a defined location and making the test sensitive to material embrittlement.

Energy absorbed (both Charpy and Izod)
E = mg(h₀ − h₁)
m = hammer mass (kg)  |  g = 9.81 m/s²  |  h₀ = initial release height (m)  |  h₁ = height reached after fracture (m)

The result is reported in joules (J) (SI) or foot-pounds (ft·lb) (imperial). Higher energy = tougher material. The test is especially sensitive to embrittlement caused by low temperature, hydrogen, radiation damage, or certain heat treatments.

C
Charpy Test
  • Specimen supported at both ends (simply supported beam)
  • Hammer strikes the face opposite the notch
  • Specimen: 10 × 10 × 55 mm, 2 mm deep V-notch
  • Standard: ASTM E23 / ISO 148
  • Most common industrial impact test for metals
  • Used for steel qualification and DBTT characterization
I
Izod Test
  • Specimen clamped at one end (cantilever beam)
  • Hammer strikes the notch face (notch faces the hammer)
  • Specimen: 10 × 10 × 75 mm; notch 28 mm from clamped end
  • Standard: ASTM E23 / ISO 180
  • Common for plastics; less common for metals in modern practice
  • Low-temperature testing is more difficult than Charpy

Charpy Impact Test

A standardized notched bar is placed horizontally across two anvil supports, and a pendulum hammer is released from a fixed height. The hammer strikes the center of the specimen on the side opposite the notch. After fracture the height the pendulum reaches on the far side is recorded, and absorbed energy is calculated from the height difference.

Fracture Surface Appearance

Ductile Fracture
  • Dull, fibrous, grey appearance
  • Significant plastic deformation; visible shear lips at edges
  • High absorbed energy
  • Fracture surface inclined ~45° (shear mode)
  • Occurs at temperatures above the DBTT
Brittle Fracture
  • Bright, crystalline, shiny appearance (cleavage facets)
  • Little or no visible plastic deformation
  • Low absorbed energy
  • Flat fracture surface perpendicular to loading axis
  • Occurs at temperatures below the DBTT
Percent shear fracture (%SF) is often reported alongside absorbed energy. It estimates the fraction of the fracture surface that appears ductile (fibrous). 100%SF = fully ductile; 0%SF = fully brittle. For structural steels, minimum %SF is often specified in design codes (e.g., ≥ 70% for pipeline steels at the design temperature).

Izod Impact Test

In the Izod test the specimen is clamped vertically at its base, acting as a cantilever beam. The notch faces the incoming hammer, which strikes 22 mm above the notch on the free end of the specimen. Because the specimen is clamped rather than simply supported, the stress state differs from Charpy — results from the two methods cannot be directly compared.

Results from Charpy and Izod tests are not interchangeable. Always specify which test method was used when reporting impact energy values.

Ductile-Brittle Transition Temperature (DBTT)

Many metals — particularly BCC (body-centered cubic) metals such as carbon steels and ferritic stainless steels — exhibit a dramatic drop in impact energy over a relatively narrow temperature range called the ductile-brittle transition. Above this range the material fractures by ductile mechanisms and absorbs large amounts of energy (upper shelf). Below it fracture is dominated by cleavage and the absorbed energy drops to a low plateau (lower shelf). The temperature at the midpoint of this transition is the DBTT.

Fig. 1 — Charpy absorbed energy vs. temperature for a typical BCC steel. The DBTT is conventionally defined at the midpoint energy between the upper and lower shelf values.
Why does BCC exhibit a DBTT? In BCC metals, the Peierls-Nabarro stress (lattice friction on dislocations) rises steeply with decreasing temperature, and the number of active slip systems is limited. Below the DBTT, dislocations cannot move fast enough to relieve stress, so fracture proceeds by cleavage along {100} planes — a low-energy, brittle mechanism. FCC metals have many more active slip systems and lower lattice friction, so they remain ductile even at cryogenic temperatures.

Engineering significance: The Liberty Ships in WWII and the RMS Titanic are famous examples of catastrophic brittle fracture linked to operating below the DBTT of the hull steel. Cold ocean temperatures pushed the material below its transition temperature, enabling cleavage fracture to propagate from small notches or defects at stress levels well below the yield strength.

Upper Shelf vs. Lower Shelf Energy

The impact energy vs. temperature curve for BCC steels has two horizontal plateaus connected by an S-shaped transition. These plateaus define the upper and lower shelf.

Upper Shelf Energy (USE)
  • Energy absorbed at high temperatures (above DBTT)
  • Fracture dominated by ductile mechanisms — microvoid coalescence (dimple fracture)
  • High absorbed energy; often >100 J for structural steels
  • Fracture surface appears dull, fibrous, grey
  • Visible shear lips at specimen edges
  • Represents the material's maximum available toughness
Lower Shelf Energy (LSE)
  • Energy absorbed at low temperatures (below DBTT)
  • Fracture dominated by cleavage — brittle transgranular fracture along {100} planes
  • Low absorbed energy; may be as low as 2–7 J
  • Fracture surface appears bright, crystalline, mirror-like
  • No shear lips; flat fracture plane
  • Represents the worst-case toughness for that material
Design codes and minimum energy requirements: Structural steel specifications (e.g., ASTM A709 for bridges) specify a minimum Charpy energy at a specified test temperature. For example, A709 Grade 50W Zones 2 and 3 requires ≥ 27 J (20 ft·lb) at −18°C (0°F). This ensures the material is operating in or above its transition zone — not on the lower shelf — at service temperatures.

Fatigue and the S-N Curve

Fatigue is the progressive, localized structural damage that occurs when a material is subjected to cyclic (repeated) loading. Failure can occur at stresses far below the static yield strength after a sufficient number of cycles — making fatigue the cause of the majority of mechanical failures in engineering practice (rotating shafts, connecting rods, aircraft structures, bridges, biomedical implants).

Fatigue failure occurs in three distinct stages:

①
Stage I — Crack Initiation

A microcrack nucleates at a stress concentration — typically a surface defect, machining mark, inclusion, or geometric discontinuity (notch, hole, fillet, keyway). The crack grows slowly along persistent slip bands oriented ~45° to the loading axis.

②
Stage II — Crack Propagation

The crack reorients to grow perpendicular to the maximum tensile stress. Each load cycle advances the crack by a small increment, leaving beach marks (fatigue striations) on the fracture surface — one striation per cycle. Growth rate is described by Paris' Law: da/dN = C(ΔK)m.

③
Stage III — Final Fracture

When the remaining cross-section can no longer support the peak load, sudden final fracture occurs — either ductile overload (dimples) or brittle cleavage depending on material and temperature. The final fracture zone shows a rough, irregular surface distinctly different from the fatigue region.

The S-N Curve (Wöhler Curve)

An S-N curve is generated by testing multiple identical specimens at different stress amplitudes and recording the cycles to failure. The x-axis is plotted on a log scale. Each data point represents one specimen; scatter is significant, so multiple specimens per stress level are required for statistical reliability.

Fig. 2 — S-N curves for steel (solid line) and aluminum (dashed line). Steel exhibits a true endurance limit Sₑ — a stress below which fatigue failure does not occur regardless of the number of cycles. Aluminum shows no endurance limit; fatigue strength must be reported at a specified life (typically 10⁷ cycles).
Cyclic stress parameters
Sₐ = (Sₘₐₓ − Sₘᵢₙ) / 2     Sₘ = (Sₘₐₓ + Sₘᵢₙ) / 2     R = Sₘᵢₙ / Sₘₐₓ
Sₐ = stress amplitude  |  Sₘ = mean stress  |  R = stress ratio
R = −1 → fully reversed (Sₘ = 0, zero mean stress)  |  R = 0 → pulsating tension (min = 0)  |  R = +1 → static load (no fatigue)

Endurance Limit vs. Fatigue Strength

A key distinction exists between materials that have a true endurance limit and those that do not:

Endurance Limit (Sₑ) — Ferrous Metals
  • Steels exhibit a horizontal plateau in the S-N curve
  • Below Sₑ the material can endure infinite cycles without failure
  • Approximation for polished, unnotched steel specimens:
    Sₑ ≈ 0.5 × UTS  for UTS < ~1400 MPa
  • Above ~1400 MPa UTS, Sₑ no longer scales with UTS (inclusions dominate)
  • Sₑ is a laboratory value — real components require knockdown factors for surface finish, size, notches, and reliability
Fatigue Strength (Sₑ) — Non-Ferrous Metals
  • Aluminum, copper, and most non-ferrous alloys show no endurance limit — the S-N curve keeps declining
  • Fatigue strength must be reported at a specified life (typically 10⁷ or 5×10⁸ cycles)
  • Example: 6061-T6 aluminum ≈ 96 MPa at 5×10⁸ cycles (~31% of UTS)
  • Designs must account for expected service life — infinite-life design is not valid

Endurance limit knockdown factors: The textbook Sₑ value assumes a mirror-polished, unnotched laboratory specimen under fully reversed bending (R = −1). Real components require correction factors for: surface finish (rough machining can reduce Sₑ by 40–60%), stress concentrations (Kₑ), component size, environmental effects, and reliability. A heavily notched steel shaft operating in a corrosive environment may have an effective endurance limit only 15–25% of the material's laboratory Sₑ.
