ASM International — Handbook Vol. 11: Failure Analysis and Prevention

The ASM Seven-Step Failure Analysis Process

ASM Handbook Vol. 11 defines a systematic seven-step process for failure analysis. Each step must be completed before advancing, and findings at later steps often require revisiting earlier ones. The process is iterative, not strictly linear.

1
Background Information Collection

Gather design drawings, material specifications, service history, operating conditions, maintenance records, and operator accounts. Establish the component's function, load history, environment, and any prior repairs or modifications.

  • Design drawings and tolerances
  • Material certification records (mill certs, heat treat records)
  • Service life and load cycle history
  • Environmental exposure (temperature, corrosives, humidity)
  • Prior maintenance, modifications, or known defects
2
Visual and Macroscopic Examination

Examine the component without sectioning. Document all visible damage — deformation, corrosion, wear, fracture surface topography, and fracture plane orientation relative to the applied load. Use low-power stereo microscopy (1–50×).

  • Fracture surface: flat/fibrous/chevron/beach marks
  • Deformation: necking, bending, torsion evidence
  • Corrosion: location, type (pitting, SCC, galvanic)
  • Origin identification: convergence of river marks / ratchet marks
3
Non-Destructive Testing (NDT)

Apply NDT before any sectioning to detect secondary cracks, subsurface defects, or additional damage not visible to the naked eye. Results guide where to section.

  • Dye penetrant (PT) — surface-open cracks
  • Magnetic particle (MT) — near-surface cracks in ferromagnetics
  • Radiography (RT) — internal voids, inclusions, shrinkage
  • Ultrasonic (UT) — subsurface cracks, delaminations
4
Mechanical Testing

Test material from the failed component against specification requirements. Select locations away from the fracture to avoid heat-affected or deformed material.

  • Tensile testing (UTS, yield, elongation, reduction of area)
  • Hardness surveys (macro and micro — Vickers, Rockwell)
  • Charpy impact (ductile-to-brittle transition)
  • Fracture toughness (KIC) if applicable
5
Metallurgical and Chemical Analysis

Section, mount, polish, and etch specimens from key locations. Verify chemistry, microstructure, heat treatment condition, and detect any manufacturing or material defects.

  • Optical metallography: grain size, phase identification, inclusion content
  • SEM / EDS: fractographic features + elemental analysis
  • OES or wet chemistry: alloy composition verification
  • EPMA: local composition at initiation site
6
Fracture Mechanics Assessment

Apply linear-elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) to verify that the observed crack size, geometry, and material properties are consistent with the proposed failure mode. Check whether the critical crack size at fracture matches the fracture surface evidence.

  • Calculate critical crack size: ac = (1/π)(KIC/σ·F)²
  • Estimate da/dN from striation spacing (fatigue)
  • Assess crack growth life from initial defect to ac
  • Compare calculated vs observed final fracture zone size
7
Root Cause Determination & Report

Synthesise all findings into a root cause (primary), contributing causes (secondary), and recommendations. All conclusions must be supported by physical evidence — not assumed. The report must distinguish between what is known, what is inferred, and what remains uncertain.

  • Root cause: single initiating condition without which failure would not occur
  • Contributing factors: conditions that accelerated or enabled failure
  • Corrective actions: design, material, process, or maintenance changes
  • Preventive actions: broader system-level improvements

Industry Case Studies — ASM Failure Analysis

Aerospace Turbine Blade Fatigue — High-Cycle Thermal-Mechanical

Component: Ni-based superalloy compressor blade, stage 3, 8,200 hr service. Failure: High-cycle fatigue (HCF) initiated at a film cooling hole edge, propagated across 70% of chord before liberation.

Fractographic evidence: Fine striations at ~0.05 μm spacing near origin (very high ΔK regime); multiple ratchet marks at cooling hole edge. Final fracture zone shows intergranular fracture consistent with hot corrosion weakening of grain boundaries.
Root cause: Stress concentration at cooling hole (Kt ≈ 2.8) combined with vibratory stress from compressor surge event. Hot corrosion (Type I, 850°C) reduced grain boundary strength. Corrective action: Film cooling hole radius increased; inlet filtration improved; compressor surge protection re-calibrated.
Automotive Crankshaft Fatigue — Torsional Resonance at Fillet Radius

Component: Forged 4340 steel crankshaft, 6-cylinder diesel engine, 180,000 km. Failure: Fatigue fracture at journal fillet radius; single initiation site at a machining mark.

Fractographic evidence: Beach marks occupying 85% of cross-section (low nominal stress, long crack growth period). Single ratchet mark at fillet. Striations at 0.3–1.2 μm spacing indicate torsional cyclic loading. Final fracture zone (15%) shows ductile dimples — adequate material toughness.
Root cause: Machining mark at fillet (Rz 12 μm vs specified Rz 4 μm) acted as stress concentration under torsional resonance. Torsional vibration damper had failed 30,000 km prior. Corrective action: Surface finish specification tightened; periodic damper inspection interval added.
Civil / Structural Bridge Eyebar Brittle Fracture — Silver Bridge Collapse (1967)

Component: High-strength steel eyebar, main suspension chain, Silver Bridge, OH/WV, USA. Failure: Brittle fracture of one eyebar triggered progressive collapse; 46 fatalities.

Fractographic evidence: Corrosion-fatigue crack initiated at fretting pit at eyebar pin contact. Crack propagated by stress corrosion cracking (SCC) through high-strength steel (UTS ~950 MPa) in river environment. Final fracture: cleavage, no shear lip — temperature was 4°C, below NDT.
Root cause: Design provided zero redundancy — single-pin eyebar chain with no alternative load path. Material susceptible to SCC. Absence of inspection access prevented detection of growing crack (est. 2.5 mm deep at fracture). Legacy: Led to bridge inspection standards and fracture-critical member (FCM) designation in AASHTO.
Pressure Equipment Ammonia Storage Tank SCC — Polythionic Acid Environment

Component: 304 stainless steel ammonia storage vessel, chemical plant, 12 yr service. Failure: SCC resulting in leakage during shutdown; no fatalities but $4M damage.

Fractographic evidence: Intergranular SCC from inner surface. Sensitized grain boundaries (carbide precipitation at 550–750°C during welding) provided short-circuit diffusion paths. Polythionic acid formed from H₂S + SO₂ + H₂O during shutdown and plant washout.
Root cause: Welding created sensitized HAZ without post-weld solution anneal. Polythionic acid environment formed only during shutdown — not anticipated in original design. Corrective action: Post-weld anneal or use low-carbon grade (304L) for all future vessels; nitrogen purge during shutdowns; shutdown procedure revised.
Manufacturing Carbide Tool Insert Chipping — Thermal Shock + Adhesive Wear

Component: WC-Co cemented carbide insert, interrupted turning of 4140 steel, 300 m/min. Failure: Edge chipping after 45 seconds cutting time.

Fractographic evidence: Comb cracks (thermal fatigue) on rake face from cyclic heating/cooling. Adhesive wear (built-up edge, BUE) on flank at low cutting speed. Chipping initiated from comb crack intersection with edge.
Root cause: Interrupted cut produced thermal cycling; intermittent coolant application worsened thermal shock. Grade too brittle (low Co binder). Corrective action: Higher Co content grade (tougher); dry cutting or continuous flood coolant — not intermittent.

NTSB — National Transportation Safety Board (USA)

NTSB Investigation Structure and Phases

The NTSB investigates accidents in aviation, rail, highway, marine, and pipeline transportation. All NTSB investigations focus on safety improvement — not fault assignment or liability. Published reports are public and freely available at ntsb.gov.

Phase 1 — Go-Team Deployment & Scene Documentation
Investigators arrive within hours of notification. The primary task is scene documentation before any evidence is disturbed: total-station mapping, photography, wreckage distribution diagrams, witness interviews. For aviation, the flight data recorder (FDR) and cockpit voice recorder (CVR) are secured immediately.
  • Wreckage documentation: GPS-mapped, photographed, catalogued
  • Chain of custody established for all physical evidence
  • Witness statements taken; flight/voyage records secured
  • Weather data, ATC recordings, radar tracks obtained
Phase 2 — Factual Investigation (Working Groups)
The investigation is divided into working groups, each led by an NTSB investigator with participation from parties (manufacturers, operators, unions, regulatory agencies). Working groups cover: structures, powerplant, systems, operations, human performance, meteorology, survival factors.
  • Structures group: wreckage reconstruction, fracture analysis, load analysis
  • Powerplant group: engine teardown, component inspection, borescope
  • Systems group: flight control, hydraulics, avionics data
  • Human performance: crew history, fatigue, training, decision-making
Phase 3 — Laboratory Analysis
Fracture surfaces, fasteners, components, and materials are analysed at NTSB's Washington D.C. laboratory or contracted labs. Fractographic techniques mirror those in ASM Vol. 11/12. Independent metallurgical experts may be called.
  • SEM / EDS fractography: fracture mode, crack origin, crack growth direction
  • Fatigue life estimation from striation spacing
  • Material verification: chemistry, hardness, microstructure vs. spec
  • NDT of similar components still in service (fleet-wide action)
Phase 4 — Probable Cause Determination & Safety Recommendations
The NTSB Board meets to vote on the probable cause and contributing factors. Safety recommendations (addressed to FAA, NHTSA, DOT, manufacturers, etc.) are issued as separate letters — these are binding in the sense that recipients must formally respond but are not legal mandates.
  • Probable cause: the most direct causal chain leading to the accident
  • Contributing factors: conditions that influenced the probable cause
  • Safety recommendations: specific, actionable, addressed to named recipients
  • Most-Wanted List: recurring systemic safety issues tracked publicly
AAIB — Air Accidents Investigation Branch (UK)

AAIB Investigation Structure

The AAIB (UK Department for Transport) investigates civil aviation accidents and serious incidents in the UK. Like the NTSB, AAIB investigations are safety-focused, not fault-based. Reports are published in full at gov.uk/aaib-reports. The AAIB operates under ICAO Annex 13 standards and EU Regulation 996/2010.

Selected Material Failure Findings from Published Reports
NTSB — Aviation Aloha Airlines Flight 243 (1988) — Fuselage Fatigue / MSD

NTSB Report AAR-89/03. 737-200 suffered explosive decompression at 24,000 ft; 18-foot section of fuselage separated. Material finding: Multiple-site damage (MSD) — simultaneous fatigue cracks at multiple fastener holes in the lap joint. Each crack individually subcritical, but their link-up produced a critical flaw length much faster than single-crack growth models predicted.

Metallurgical key findings: Disbonding of adhesive bond between fuselage skin layers (moisture-induced) removed the bond's crack-arrest function. Crevice corrosion at the lap joint accelerated crack initiation at each fastener hole. Fatigue striations confirmed high-cycle fatigue; 89,680 flight cycles on the airframe.
Legacy: Led to USAF and FAA aging aircraft programs; lap-joint inspection requirements; damage-tolerance analysis mandated for high-cycle aircraft.
AAIB — Rail (RAIB) Hatfield Rail Crash (2000) — Rolling Contact Fatigue / Gauge Corner Cracking

HSE / HMRI report. High-speed derailment caused by rail fracture from rolling contact fatigue (RCF) — specifically gauge corner cracking (GCC). Four fatalities. The rail had been identified as needing replacement 21 months earlier but was not changed.

Metallurgical key findings: RCF cracks initiated at gauge corner under wheel contact stress (hertzian contact). Cracks grew at ~35° below surface, branching transversely. Rail fragmented into multiple pieces — 300+ fragments recovered. Hardness of failed rail matched specification; failure was RCF-mode, not material defect.
Legacy: Led to mandatory RCF inspection programs, grinding schedules, and improved track maintenance management systems in UK. Railtrack company restructured.
NTSB — Pipeline Pacific Gas & Electric San Bruno Pipeline Explosion (2010)

NTSB Report PAR-11/01. Natural gas pipeline rupture and fire; 8 fatalities, 58 homes destroyed. Material finding: Seam weld defect in 1956-vintage ERW pipe acted as the flaw. Defect had existed since manufacture; pipeline records were incorrect about pipe specification and seam type.

Metallurgical key findings: ERW seam weld had a cold weld zone (lack of fusion) ~72mm long × 3mm deep — a manufacturing defect from 1956. Pressure cycle fatigue grew the defect over 54 years. Final fracture: brittle, through-thickness rupture propagating ~51m along the seam. No hydrostatic test had been performed at sufficient pressure to detect the defect.
Legacy: PHMSA rules requiring pipeline operators to verify records, perform pressure tests on untested pipe segments, and replace or verify pre-1970 ERW pipe.

Key Terminology in Accident Investigation Reports

TermDefinition in Report Context
Probable CauseThe primary causal chain established by evidence. Not a legal finding of fault. NTSB uses "The probable cause of this accident was…"; AAIB uses "Causal Factor."
Contributing FactorA condition that influenced the accident but was not the primary cause. Multiple contributing factors are common.
Safety RecommendationSpecific, actionable recommendation addressed to a named authority. Graded: Open-Acceptable, Open-Unacceptable, Closed-Acceptable, Closed-Unacceptable.
Factual ReportUninterpreted data compiled by each working group. Published separately from the analytical report in NTSB docket.
Fracture-Critical Member (FCM)A structural member whose single failure would cause collapse — no redundancy. Requires special inspection protocols (AASHTO/FAA terminology).
Damage ToleranceDesign philosophy ensuring a structure can sustain a specific defect size and still carry limit load until next inspection.
Multiple-Site Damage (MSD)Simultaneous fatigue cracks at multiple locations (e.g., fastener holes) that reduce the critical crack length for link-up fracture.
Initiating EventThe first event in the accident sequence — often distinguished from the root cause (the underlying condition that allowed the initiating event to occur).
TWI — The Welding Institute — Failure Analysis Methodology Guide

TWI Systematic Failure Investigation — Overview

TWI's failure analysis methodology is used for engineering components across welded structures, pressure equipment, pipelines, and rotating machinery. The approach prioritises evidence preservation and sequential examination so that later destructive tests do not destroy evidence needed for earlier conclusions. Each stage produces a written record before proceeding.

Critical Rule: Never perform destructive examination (sectioning, hardness indents, chemical etch) before completing all non-destructive examination. Evidence lost by premature sectioning cannot be recovered. Document everything at each stage before proceeding.

TWI Examination Sequence — Flowchart

Stage 1 — Background Information & Evidence Preservation

Preservation of the failed component in its post-failure state is the first and most critical step. Any cleaning, handling damage, or premature sectioning permanently destroys evidence. TWI's protocol requires written authorisation before any component is touched beyond initial documentation.

Evidence Preservation Checklist

  • Photograph the component in-situ before removal
  • Bag and label all fracture fragments — do not re-assemble mating surfaces
  • Seal fracture faces in dry nitrogen or desiccant bag to prevent further oxidation
  • Do NOT clean fracture surfaces — corrosion products contain chemical evidence
  • Do NOT wire-brush, grit-blast, or apply penetrant to fracture surface
  • Mark the orientation of every piece relative to installation direction
  • Collect any debris, wear particles, or fluid in the area
  • Record temperature, humidity, and environment at time of failure if possible
  • Preserve lubricant samples and any process fluids present
  • Secure any mating components or counterfaces (gears, bearings, seals)

Background Information Collection Checklist

  • Design drawings, material specification, and tolerances
  • Mill certificates and heat treatment records
  • Welding procedure specifications (WPS) and welder qualification records
  • Manufacturing inspection records and NDT reports
  • Service history: total life, load cycles, overhauls, repairs
  • Operating conditions at time of failure (load, speed, temperature, pressure)
  • Maintenance log entries — any recent repairs or modifications
  • Similar failures on the same or sister components
  • Environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, corrosive chemicals)
  • Operator accounts and any known pre-failure symptoms

Stage 2 — Visual & Macroscopic Examination

The first examination of the component uses only the unaided eye and low-power stereo microscope (up to 50×). This stage establishes the overall failure geometry, fracture mode, and origin location before any magnification or destructive work.

Stage 4 — Stress Analysis Considerations

Before destructive work, TWI recommends estimating the stress state at the failure location to determine whether the observed failure mode is consistent with service loads.

Fracture mechanics check: Calculate the critical crack size ac = (KIC/σ·F)²/π. If the initial defect size (from NDT or fractographic estimation) is > ac, failure was inevitable at that load. If ac is large (cm-scale), fatigue crack growth over extended time is implicated.
  • FEA or analytical stress at failure location vs. nominal design stress
  • Stress concentration factors (Kt) at origin geometry
  • Residual stress from welding or manufacturing (may add or subtract from service stress)
  • Cyclic stress range and R-ratio for fatigue life estimation
  • Threshold K (Kth) for fatigue crack propagation

Stage 5 & 6 — Destructive Examination

Irreversible: Plan the entire sectioning strategy before making the first cut. Identify and mark all regions of interest. Cut specimens in a sequence that preserves the most critical evidence longest. Photograph each cross-section immediately after cutting.

Metallurgical Examination — Sequence of Specimen Preparation

A
Fracture Surface Examination (Before Sectioning)

Examine fracture surface in SEM before any sectioning or etching. Record: fracture mode (dimples/cleavage/striations), crack origin location, crack growth direction, evidence of corrosion or contamination. EDS at initiation site to identify any embrittling species.

B
Transverse Section Through Origin

Section perpendicular to the fracture face through the initiation site. Mount, polish, examine unetched (inclusion content, second-phase particles, voids) then etched (microstructure, grain size, phase identification, HAZ extent if welded).

C
Longitudinal Section

Provides grain flow direction (wrought products), decarburisation depth, surface treatment depth (case depth, plating, coatings), and through-thickness microstructural gradients.

D
Chemistry Verification

OES (optical emission spectrometry) on a ground surface remote from the failure — verifies alloy composition against certification. EDS in SEM identifies inclusions at origin site. EPMA for quantitative local chemistry if segregation or contamination is suspected.

Stage 7 — Root Cause Synthesis

TWI uses a structured cause-and-effect analysis to move from observations to root cause. All proposed causes must be consistent with ALL physical evidence. Any single piece of evidence that contradicts a proposed root cause disqualifies that hypothesis.

Hypothesis Testing Rule (TWI): For each proposed root cause, list the physical evidence that supports it AND the evidence that would be expected if this were the cause but is absent. If expected evidence is absent, the hypothesis is weakened or eliminated.

Common Analysis Pitfalls (TWI)

  • Confirmation bias: Seeking evidence to support a pre-formed conclusion; ignoring contradictory evidence
  • Single-cause thinking: Most failures have multiple causes; stopping at the first plausible explanation
  • Blaming the operator: Human error is rarely a root cause — it is usually a symptom of a system deficiency
  • Missing latent causes: Design or management system failures that enabled the immediate cause to occur

Stage 8 — Report Writing

The TWI failure analysis report must be self-contained, evidence-based, and written for a technically competent reader who was not present during the investigation. Conclusions must never go beyond what the evidence supports. Uncertainty must be explicitly stated.

1. Executive Summary (1 page)
Component identity; failure date and consequence; root cause statement (one sentence); key recommendations. Sufficient for a non-technical manager to understand the outcome.
2. Background and Component Description
Drawing references; material specification; function; service history; operating conditions; maintenance records. Photographs of the component in service condition if available.
3. Visual and Macroscopic Examination
Annotated photographs of all significant features. Description of fracture surface, deformation, corrosion. Origin location (with scale reference). Fracture mode hypothesis at this stage.
4. NDT Results
Method used; conditions; findings with locations. Photographs of indications. Interpretation of NDT findings (secondary cracks, subsurface defects).
5. Mechanical Testing Results
All results tabulated with specification requirements alongside. Pass/fail against specification. Hardness survey maps. Charpy transition curves if applicable.
6. Metallurgical and Chemical Analysis
SEM fractographs annotated with scale bars; EDS spectra; optical micrographs of microstructure with etching conditions; chemistry table vs. specification; grain size measurements.
7. Stress and Fracture Mechanics Analysis
Calculated service stress; stress concentration factor at origin; critical crack size calculation; consistency check between calculated ac and observed final fracture zone size.
8. Discussion and Root Cause
Synthesis of all evidence. Fracture sequence narrative. Root cause and contributing factors stated explicitly. Any alternative hypotheses considered and eliminated (with reason). Uncertainty statements where evidence is incomplete.
9. Conclusions
Numbered list. Each conclusion must be directly supported by evidence cited in sections 3–7. No new information in the conclusions section.
10. Recommendations
Corrective actions (fix the specific failure) and preventive actions (address the system condition that allowed it). Each recommendation is specific, actionable, and addressed to a named responsible party. Prioritised by urgency and safety criticality.
TWI Report Quality Check: Before issuing, verify — (1) every conclusion is supported by cited evidence; (2) the root cause eliminates all other hypotheses; (3) recommendations address both the immediate cause and the underlying system condition; (4) the report has been reviewed by an independent engineer not involved in the investigation.
