Metal Transfer Modes in GMAW

The manner in which molten metal transfers from the wire electrode to the weld pool determines arc stability, spatter level, position capability, and penetration profile. Transfer mode is primarily controlled by current (wire feed speed) and voltage, and is also influenced by shielding gas composition and wire diameter.

Short-Circuit Transfer (GMAW-S)
How it works: Wire physically contacts the pool 20–200 times/sec. Current spikes, surface tension pulls droplet into pool, arc re-ignites.

Current: 50–175 A
Voltage: 15–22 V
Wire: 0.023–0.035 in
Gas: CO₂ or C25 (75Ar/25CO₂)
Positions: All positions
Ideal for: Sheet metal <3/16 in, root passes, out-of-position
Caution: Risk of cold lap / lack of fusion on thick material
Globular Transfer
How it works: Droplets larger than wire diameter fall into pool under gravity irregularly. Not axially directed.

Current: 150–250 A
Voltage: 22–28 V
Wire: 0.035–0.045 in
Gas: CO₂ or low-Ar blends (<80% Ar)
Positions: Flat & horizontal only
Ideal for: Generally avoided — occurs as transition with CO₂ or when too low for spray
Characteristics: High spatter, irregular bead
Spray Transfer
How it works: Above transition current, droplets smaller than wire diameter transfer axially in a fine stream — no short circuits.

Current: 200–500 A (above transition)
Voltage: 26–36 V
Wire: 0.035–0.062 in
Gas: ≥80% Ar required
Positions: Flat & horizontal only (gravity-dependent pool)
Ideal for: High deposition on thick plate, structural, heavy fabrication
Characteristics: Smooth arc, very low spatter, deep penetration
Pulsed Spray (GMAW-P)
How it works: Power source alternates between high peak current (one droplet/pulse) and low background current. One droplet per pulse at 30–400 Hz.

Peak current: 350–450 A
Background current: 50–100 A
Avg. current: 100–250 A
Gas: ≥75% Ar
Positions: All positions
Ideal for: Thin-to-medium sheet, stainless, aluminum, any position at spray-like quality
Requires: Pulsed-capable inverter power source

Carbon Steel Wire Electrodes (AWS A5.18)

AWS A5.18 classifies carbon steel GMAW wire as ER70S-X, where 70 = minimum tensile strength in ksi, S = solid wire, and the suffix number indicates chemistry grade. ER70S-6 is the most commonly used all-purpose wire in North America.

ER70S-7 70 Med–High (0.50–0.80%) Very High (1.50–2.00%) Higher Mn CO₂, C25 Higher CVN toughness than S-6; structural, pressure vessel; where impact properties matter
Why does Si matter? Silicon acts as a deoxidizer and slag former. Higher Si (ER70S-6) compensates for oxidizing shielding gases (CO₂) and dirty base metal by removing oxygen from the weld pool, preventing porosity. However, excess Si forms glassy silica islands on the bead surface that must be removed between passes in multi-pass welds.

Stainless Steel Wire Electrodes (AWS A5.9)

Stainless GMAW wire is classified by weld metal composition. L-grade (low carbon, ≤0.03% C) is preferred to minimize carbide precipitation (sensitization) in the HAZ of austenitic stainless steels.

AWS Class Base Metal Match Carbon Ni Cr Mo Shielding Gas Application
ER308L 304, 304L ≤0.03% 9–11% 19–21% — 98Ar/2O₂ or tri-mix Food/bev, dairy, general SS fabrication, tanks
ER308LSi 304, 304L ≤0.03% 9–11% 19–21% — 98Ar/2O₂ or tri-mix Same as 308L but higher Si for better wetting/less spatter in spray
ER309L Dissimilar metals ≤0.03% 12–14% 23–25% — 98Ar/2O₂ or tri-mix 304/316 to carbon steel; overlay/cladding buffer layers; 309 base metal
ER316L 316, 316L ≤0.03% 11–14% 18–20% 2–3% 98Ar/2O₂ or tri-mix Marine, chemical process, pharmaceutical (Mo improves pitting resistance)
ER347 347, 321 ≤0.08% 9–11% 19–21% — 98Ar/2O₂ High-temperature service; Nb-stabilized, resists sensitization at 800–1500°F
L-grade rule: Always use L-grade (308L, 316L, 309L) for service temperatures above 800°F or for any multi-pass weld where repeated heat cycles could sensitize the HAZ. Standard grade (308, 316) may be used for single-pass welds on clean base metal not subject to corrosive service.

Aluminum Wire Electrodes (AWS A5.10)

Aluminum GMAW uses two main filler alloy families. Choice depends on base metal alloy, required strength, and whether parts will be anodized after welding.

ER4043 Al-Si (5% Si) 25 1xxx, 3xxx, 6xxx, some 5xxx 100% Ar Excellent flow/wetting, low crack sensitivity, silvery-gray anodized finish — good for 6061. Lower strength than 5356.
ER4047 Al-Si (12% Si) 24 6xxx, brazing 100% Ar Lowest melting range, high fluidity — automotive brazing/welding, heat exchangers. Narrow fusion zone.

Never use ER5356 for service above 150°F — the Mg content can cause stress-corrosion cracking in long-term hot/wet environments.

Stainless Steel Shielding Gas Selection (Airgas / Linde)

Stainless steel is highly sensitive to oxidation — CO₂ content must be kept very low (<5%) to prevent carbide precipitation (sensitization) and maintain corrosion resistance. Oxygen additions (<2%) improve wetting and bead shape without significantly affecting chemistry.

Gas Mixture Composition Transfer Mode Arc Characteristics Bead Appearance Best For
98Ar/2O₂ 98% Ar, 2% O₂ Spray only Very stable; low spatter; excellent arc directionality Very flat, bright, smooth; minimal oxidation tint Primary choice for SS spray; flat & horizontal; food/dairy/pharmaceutical
Tri-mix (HeAr-CO₂) 90% He / 7.5% Ar / 2.5% CO₂ Spray or Pulsed Hotter arc; faster travel speeds; excellent fusion Bright, low spatter; good appearance All-position SS; thick plate; high-production; pipe welding
97.5Ar/2.5CO₂ 97.5% Ar, 2.5% CO₂ Spray, Pulsed, SC Stable arc; slightly more fluid puddle than 98Ar/2O₂ Good; slight darkening more than O₂ mix General SS fabrication; less oxidation concern than higher CO₂
100% Ar 100% Argon SC / Pulsed Erratic in spray mode; less stable; no oxidizing component Good on thin SS; erratic bead with spray Pulsed process on thin SS when O₂/CO₂ must be avoided
Sensitization caution: CO₂ in shielding gas reacts with the weld pool and HAZ. At elevated temperatures, Cr reacts with C to form Cr₂₃C₆ at grain boundaries — depleting chromium and destroying corrosion resistance (sensitization). Keep CO₂ ≤2.5% for austenitic SS (308L, 316L). Use 98Ar/2O₂ whenever possible. Never use C25 or 100% CO₂ on stainless steel.

Setting GMAW Parameters — Miller Electric Approach

Miller Electric's "golden rule" for GMAW setup: Wire Feed Speed (WFS) controls amperage (and thus penetration); Voltage controls arc length and bead width. Start with the WFS for the desired current, then tune voltage for bead shape.

Step-by-Step Setup (Miller Electric MIG Guide)
  1. Select wire diameter based on material thickness (see Wire Selection tab)
  2. Set shielding gas and flow rate (25–35 cfh for indoor; 35–50 cfh if any air movement)
  3. Set WFS to target amperage — verify with tip chart or synergic table
  4. Calculate starting voltage: V ≈ 0.04 × A + 16 (±2 V adjustment from there)
  5. Set CTWD (contact-tip-to-work distance)
  6. Run a test bead; adjust voltage ±1V steps for arc sound and bead shape
  7. Check for proper fusion at toes; adjust travel speed last
Starting Voltage = 0.04 × (Amperage) + 16 [± 2 V]
Example: 150 A → V = 0.04(150) + 16 = 22 V starting point. Adjust for wire diameter, gas, and bead shape.

Lack of fusion (LOF) Short-circuit on thick material (cold weld); amperage too low; CTWD too long; travel speed too fast; wrong joint prep angle Increase WFS/amperage; switch to spray or pulsed on thicker material; reduce CTWD; slow travel; open joint bevel to ≥30° included angle
Undercut Voltage too high; amperage too high; travel speed too fast; wrong gun angle; arc not dwelling at toe Reduce voltage 1–2V; reduce WFS; slow travel speed; adjust work angle to direct arc to toe; use slight weave pause at toes
Overlap / cold lap Travel too slow; voltage too high; puddle flowing ahead of arc; wrong gun angle Increase travel speed; reduce voltage; adjust gun angle; use drag technique to keep arc ahead of puddle
Convex / ropy bead Voltage too low; CTWD too short; travel too fast; wrong gas Increase voltage 1–2V; adjust CTWD; slow down; verify correct gas blend
Burn-through (thin sheet) Amperage too high; travel too slow; wire diameter too large; poor fit-up (gap too wide) Reduce WFS; increase travel speed; drop to smaller wire diameter (0.023 or 0.030); tighten fit-up; use pulsed transfer
Silica islands on bead (CS) Normal with ER70S-6 + CO₂; Si oxidizes and rises to surface as glassy inclusions Wire brush between passes (multi-pass); switch to ER70S-3 if Si islands are excessive; or switch to lower-CO₂ gas
Distortion Heat concentrated in one area; excessive passes; no preheat sequence planning Use backstep or skip welding sequence; clamp/tack frequently; reduce heat input; balance welds on both sides of joint
Gas-Specific Issues (Airgas)
SymptomGas-Related CauseSolution
Porosity on Al Moisture in Ar; CO₂ or CO₂-blend used on Al; base metal not cleaned of Al oxide Use dry 100% Ar (dewpoint ≤–40°F); never use CO₂ or CO₂ blends on Al; clean with acetone + SS brush immediately before welding
Sensitization on SS (corrosion failure) CO₂ >3% in shielding gas for austenitic SS; high heat input multi-pass Switch to 98Ar/2O₂ or tri-mix; use L-grade wire (308L, 316L); control interpass temp ≤350°F
Spray not achievable (high Ar gas on hand) Too much CO₂ (must be <20% for spray) Switch to ≥80% Ar blend; use C10 or 98Ar/2O₂; or switch to pulsed GMAW
Hydrogen porosity (HIC risk) Moisture in shielding gas, wire, or on base metal (coatings, rust, oil) Store wire sealed; clean base metal; verify gas dewpoint; avoid welding on wet/cold material without preheat
