Arc Welding Processes — TWI Overview

TWI defines arc welding as a group of processes that use an electric arc as the heat source to melt and join metals. The four primary arc welding processes used in fabrication and structural work are SMAW, GMAW, GTAW, and FCAW. Each uses a different approach to arc protection, electrode type, and positional capability.

SMAW

Shielded Metal Arc Welding (Stick)

An arc is struck between a flux-coated consumable electrode and the base metal. The flux coating decomposes in the arc to produce shielding gas and slag, protecting the weld pool from atmospheric contamination. No external shielding gas cylinder is required.

How it works: Current flows through the electrode core, generating an arc that melts both the electrode tip and the base metal. Molten flux floats to the surface and solidifies as slag, which must be chipped away after each pass.

Applications

  • Structural steel — bridges, buildings, pressure vessels
  • Pipeline field girth welds (cellulosic electrodes)
  • Repair and maintenance welding
  • Outdoor / wind-exposed sites where gas shielding is impractical

DCEP (most); AC or DCEN for specific grades

GMAW / MIG

Gas Metal Arc Welding (MIG/MAG)

A continuously fed solid wire electrode is melted by an arc and the weld pool is shielded by an externally supplied gas or gas mixture. The semi-automatic nature of continuous wire feeding gives higher productivity than SMAW and eliminates slag removal (except on FCAW).

How it works: Wire is fed from a spool through a gun and contact tip where electrical contact is made. Shielding gas flows concentrically around the wire through the nozzle. Metal transfer occurs as short-circuit, globular, spray, or pulsed spray depending on parameters.

Applications

  • Sheet metal fabrication and automotive
  • Structural steel in shop environments
  • Aluminum and stainless steel fabrication
  • Robotic and automated welding lines
Polarity
DCEP (constant voltage)

All (short-circuit); F & H preferred for spray

GTAW / TIG

Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (TIG)

An arc is maintained between a non-consumable tungsten electrode and the base metal. Filler metal is added separately by hand or mechanically. The process produces the highest quality, cleanest welds of any arc process — with no spatter and minimal distortion when properly applied.

How it works: The tungsten electrode carries current without melting. Argon shielding gas flows from the ceramic cup around the electrode. The welder manually dips filler rod into the leading edge of the pool. Current may be AC (aluminum) or DCEN (steel, stainless).

Applications

  • Root passes in pipe welding (food, pharma, power)
  • Thin-gauge stainless and aluminum sheet
  • Aerospace and nuclear components
  • Exotic alloys — titanium, Inconel, Hastelloy
Polarity
DCEN (steel/SS); AC (aluminum)
Positions
All positions
Shielding
Argon (most); He or Ar/He for deeper penetration
Deposition rate
Low (0.5–2 lb/hr)
Skill level
Very high — two-handed coordination
Materials
All weldable metals; especially stainless, Al, Ti, Ni
FCAW

Flux-Cored Arc Welding

Similar to GMAW in setup, but uses a tubular wire with a flux core instead of a solid wire. The flux provides additional shielding and slag-forming compounds. FCAW-G uses supplemental shielding gas; FCAW-S (self-shielded) relies on the flux core alone — allowing outdoor use in wind.

How it works: The tubular wire is fed continuously like GMAW. The flux inside the wire decomposes in the arc, generating shielding gas and slag. Higher deposition rates than GMAW solid wire are achieved, making FCAW the preferred process for heavy structural fabrication.

Applications

  • Heavy structural fabrication — bridges, offshore platforms
  • Shipbuilding (high deposition, out-of-position capability)
  • Construction field welding (FCAW-S in wind)
  • Hardfacing and wear-resistant overlays
Polarity
DCEP (FCAW-G); DCEN (most FCAW-S)
Positions
All (with T-1 / T-8 type wires)
Shielding
75Ar/25CO₂ or CO₂ (FCAW-G); none (FCAW-S)
Deposition rate
High (8–25 lb/hr)
Skill level
Medium — slag removal required every pass
Materials
Carbon steel and low-alloy steel primarily

GTAW — Parameter Guidance (TWI)

GTAW uses constant current (CC). Amperage is set on the machine; the welder controls heat input by arc length and travel speed. Tungsten selection and preparation are critical for arc stability and weld quality.

Application Case Studies (ESAB)

The following case studies reflect the type of application scenarios and outcomes documented in ESAB's process and productivity guides. They illustrate how process and consumable selection decisions translate to real-world fabrication outcomes.

Offshore Jacket Structure — API 2Y-50 Steel
SMAW Root + FCAW-G Fill/Cap

Challenge: Welding tubular nodes on an offshore jacket in API 2Y-50 high-strength steel. AWS D1.1 Annex K structural requirements. CVN toughness required at −20°F. Field erection with wind exposure.

Solution: E7010-P1 SMAW cellulosic electrodes for root pass (penetration and vertical-down speed); E81T1-Ni1M H4 FCAW-G fill and cap passes with 75Ar/25CO₂. Preheat 150°F minimum (CE = 0.44). Interpass maximum 400°F monitored by contact pyrometer.

Process controls: Low-hydrogen filler throughout; heat input within 40–65 kJ/in; PWHT not required due to H4 hydrogen control and preheat protocol. NDT: 100% UT on primary nodes.

Result: 40% productivity gain over all-SMAW approach; all welds met AWS D1.1 and CVN toughness requirements at −20°F.
Pharmaceutical Process Piping — 316L Stainless
GTAW Full Penetration

Challenge: ASME B31.3 process piping in 2" and 4" schedule 10 316L stainless. ASME BPE surface finish requirement: Ra ≤ 32 µin. ID. Zero contamination tolerance — welds will be in contact with API pharmaceutical product.

Solution: GTAW autogenous root pass with ID argon back purge (O₂ < 20 ppm, verified by in-line monitor). ER316L filler for fill passes. Electropolish to Ra ≤ 20 µin after welding. 100% visual and endoscopic ID inspection per ASME BPE SF-1 criteria.

Process controls: Ceriated tungsten (EWCe-2) 3/32"; 80–140A DCEN; 15–20 CFH argon; cup size #8; purge maintained until weld cools below 300°C. Filler rod wiped with acetone-dampened cloth immediately before use.

Result: Zero weld rework on 340 spools. All welds passed visual, PMI, and borescope inspection. Passed FDA process validation audit.
Highway Bridge Fabrication — A709 Grade 50W Steel
GMAW Tack + SAW Fill

Challenge: Fabricating girder flanges (up to 3" thick) for a highway bridge per AWS D1.5 Bridge Welding Code. High deposition rates required for economical production; CVN impact testing at +40°F required on all weld metal.

Solution: ER70S-6 GMAW tack welds and short assembly passes; F7A6-EM13K SAW flux-electrode combination for primary flange-to-web fillet welds (flat position). Preheat 125°F minimum; interpass maximum 400°F.

Process controls: SAW at 600–900A, 30–36V; travel speed 18–24 in/min; single or tandem wire. All SAW procedures qualified per AWS D1.5 with CVN testing. Flux dried at 300°F for 2 hr before use.

Result: 3.2× deposition rate increase versus SMAW. All PQRs passed CVN at +40°F. Girder delivery schedule reduced by 6 weeks.
Aluminum Marine Superstructure — 5083-H116
Pulsed GMAW (GMAW-P)

Challenge: Welding 6mm and 10mm 5083-H116 aluminum plate for a fast ferry superstructure. Distortion control critical — thin plate in long panel sections. Burn-through risk on 6mm; conventional spray transfer impractical out-of-position.

Solution: Pulsed GMAW (GMAW-P) with ER5356 wire (0.035") and 100% argon at 40–50 CFH. Pulsed mode allows spray-quality transfer at reduced average current, enabling out-of-position welding without burn-through. ER4043 used for internal web-to-plate T-joints prone to hot cracking.

Process controls: Pulse parameters: peak current 300A, background 50A, pulse frequency 120 Hz; average current 130–180A. Gun angle: 5–10° push (forehand). Interpass cleaning: stainless steel wire brush (aluminum-dedicated).

Result: Burn-through eliminated on 6mm plate. Distortion reduced 35% vs. conventional GMAW. All welds passed DNV-GL Class survey visual and UT inspection.
