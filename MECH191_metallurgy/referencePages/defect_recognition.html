Defect Recognition

Weld Discontinuities  ·  Microstructural Defects  ·  NDT Signatures

Recognizing defects — knowing what they look like, where they form, what causes them, and how each NDT method reveals them — is a foundational skill for welding inspectors, quality engineers, and metallurgists. This reference is organized around the defect libraries from The Welding Institute (TWI), the Lincoln Electric weld defect library, and microstructural defect examples from the ASM Micrograph Center. Defects are classified by type: planar (crack-like, most structurally severe), volumetric (three-dimensional voids or inclusions), and geometric (profile and surface defects). Microstructural defects in base metal and HAZ are covered separately in Section 4.

Defect Classification Overview

Not all discontinuities are equally serious. Code acceptance criteria reflect this hierarchy: planar defects are universally rejectable at any length because their sharp tips concentrate stress and propagate as cracks; volumetric defects are evaluated against size and distribution limits; geometric defects are assessed by their effect on cross-sectional area and stress concentration at the weld toe.

1  — Planar / Crack-Like Defects Always Reject

Hot Cracking  (Solidification Cracking)
aka: Centerline crack, hot tear, solidification crack — TWI designation: Type C-s
Critical Weld Metal Often visible at cap
Description & Appearance

Forms during the final stages of weld pool solidification when columnar grains growing from opposite fusion walls meet at the centerline. Low-melting-point liquid films (sulfides, phosphides, silicates) become trapped between grains and tear under solidification shrinkage stress before they can solidify. The crack runs longitudinally along the weld centerline, often breaking the cap surface. Branching patterns and hot-torn, oxidized fracture surfaces are characteristic (TWI fractographic signature). Crater cracks are a localized form that appear as star-shaped cracks at the weld end.

Risk increases when the weld bead width-to-depth ratio is less than ~1.0 (finger bead), concentrating harmful segregants at the centerline.

Root Causes
  • High S, P content — low Mn/S ratio (<20) allows MnS to be replaced by harmful FeS films
  • High heat input → coarse columnar grains with thin liquid films
  • Concave (finger) bead profile — bead width < bead depth
  • High restraint across the joint
  • Austenitic stainless with low ferrite number (<3 FN)
  • Abrupt arc extinction without crater fill

Prevention
  • Keep Mn/S ≥ 20 (verify on MTR)
  • Maintain convex bead profile; limit bead depth
  • Use crater fill; backstep at weld ends
  • Reduce heat input; lower restraint where possible
  • For SS: maintain FN 4–8; use ER308L, ER316L
NDT Detection
VT
Excellent — centerline crack visible at cap surface; oxidized appearance
RT
Excellent — dark linear / branching at weld centerline; may be wavy or branching
UT
Fair — reflects well if oriented perpendicular to beam; may be missed if parallel
MT
Excellent for surface-breaking; wet fluorescent MT preferred for fine cracks
PT
Excellent for surface cracks; use on austenitic SS where MT is impractical
Cold Cracking  (Hydrogen-Induced Cracking / HAC)
aka: Underbead crack, toe crack, delayed cracking, HACC, HIC — TWI designation: Type C-h
Critical HAZ Weld Metal
Description & Appearance

Unlike hot cracks, cold cracks form after the weld has cooled — sometimes minutes, sometimes hours or even days later. Three conditions must simultaneously be present (the "hydrogen cracking triangle"): (1) diffusible hydrogen in the weld/HAZ, (2) a susceptible microstructure (untempered martensite or high-hardness HAZ), and (3) tensile residual stress. Remove any one condition and cracking cannot occur.

Underbead cracks run parallel to the fusion line in the CGHAZ. Toe cracks initiate at weld toe stress concentrations and propagate into the base metal. Transverse weld metal cracks can also form in high-strength weld deposits. Cold cracks have tight, smooth fracture faces (no oxidation) — distinguishing them from hot cracks on fractography.

Root Causes
  • Moisture on electrode coating, flux, or base metal surface
  • High CE steel (CE > 0.45) → martensitic or high-hardness HAZ
  • Inadequate preheat or interpass temperature → fast cooling, hydrogen doesn't diffuse out
  • High restraint → high residual tensile stress
  • Cellulosic (high-hydrogen) electrodes on restrained joints

Prevention
  • Use low-hydrogen electrodes (H4 or H8 classification)
  • Store and redry electrodes per manufacturer (typically 300–350°C / 1 hr)
  • Apply and maintain preheat (see CE table)
  • PWHT immediately after welding if high restraint
  • Hydrogen bake-out: hold at 200–300°C for 1–4 hr after welding
NDT Detection
VT
Poor — underbead cracks are subsurface; only toe cracks may be visible
RT
Often missed — tight crack faces give low contrast; thin dark line if favorably oriented
UT
Excellent — planar reflector gives strong signal; angle beam UT standard method for HAZ cracks
MT
Excellent for surface/near-surface toe cracks; inspect 24–48 h after welding (delayed cracking)
PT
Fair — detects surface cracks; MT preferred on ferromagnetic steels

Key: UT inspection of HAZ for cold cracks should be performed after the joint has fully cooled (min. 24–48 h for high-restraint joints on high CE steels).

Lamellar Tearing
aka: Step cracking, through-thickness cracking — occurs in BASE METAL, not the weld
Critical Base Metal (beneath HAZ)
Description & Appearance

Lamellar tearing is a base metal failure — it occurs in the plate beneath the HAZ, not in the weld or HAZ itself. The "staircase" or terraced fracture appearance (terraces parallel to the plate surface connected by shear steps) is diagnostic. It is driven by through-thickness (short-transverse) tensile stresses from weld shrinkage pulling on a plate with low through-thickness ductility caused by elongated MnS inclusions aligned parallel to the plate surface by hot-rolling.

Most susceptible joint types: T-joints, corner joints, and cruciform joints where weld shrinkage acts perpendicular to the plate rolling direction (through-thickness loading).

TWI research: Z-test (ASTM A770) measures short-transverse reduction of area (RA%); RA < 15% is susceptible; RA > 35% (Z35 steel) provides good resistance.

Root Causes
  • Elongated MnS inclusions — stringers reduce short-transverse ductility
  • Joint geometry requiring through-thickness loading (T, corner, cruciform)
  • High restraint + high heat input → large shrinkage force
  • No Z-direction ductility specified on the purchase order

Prevention
  • Specify Z-grade plate (ASTM A770; EN 10164 Z15/Z25/Z35) for through-thickness loading
  • Butter (overlay) the plate face before assembly to allow strain to distribute
  • Redesign joint to reduce through-thickness restraint
  • Use lower-strength, higher-ductility filler metal
  • Reduce heat input; use smaller passes
NDT Detection
VT
Poor — subsurface; only surface-breaking steps visible (late stage)
RT
Poor — terraces are parallel to radiation beam; very low contrast
UT
Excellent — primary detection method; terraces and shear steps give multiple strong reflections beneath the weld
MT
Fair — only if tearing breaks the plate surface; detects surface steps
Reheat Cracking  (Stress Relief Cracking)
aka: SR cracking, PWHT cracking, stress relaxation cracking — TWI research area: Cr-Mo-V steels, Grade 91
Critical Coarse-Grained HAZ
Description & Appearance

Reheat cracking forms in the coarse-grained HAZ (CGHAZ) during post-weld heat treatment or during high-temperature service. It is driven by creep — the CGHAZ must relax residual stresses at elevated temperature, but if the grain interior is strengthened by carbide precipitation (from alloying elements Cr, Mo, V, Nb) and the grain boundaries are weaker, creep deformation concentrates at grain boundaries and intergranular cracks form.

Characteristic location: CGHAZ immediately adjacent to the fusion line. Fracture is intergranular (follows prior austenite grain boundaries) — distinguishable from cold cracks (transgranular) by metallographic examination. Particularly prevalent in Cr-Mo steels (P4, P5), Grade 91 (9Cr-1Mo-V, P5B), and some low-alloy structural steels with V additions.

Root Causes
  • Alloying elements (Cr, Mo, V, Nb, Ti) that precipitate carbides and strengthen grain interiors during PWHT
  • Coarse prior austenite grain size in CGHAZ (from high peak HAZ temperature)
  • High weld toe stress concentration (sharp weld profile)
  • Rapid heating to PWHT temperature — insufficient time for stress relief at boundaries
  • Residual stress exceeding creep strength of the grain boundary

Prevention
  • Dress weld toes to reduce stress concentration before PWHT
  • Control heat input to minimize CGHAZ width
  • Temper bead technique (for in-service repairs without PWHT)
  • Select steels/filler metals with low reheat crack susceptibility (low V+Nb+Ti)
  • Slow heat-up rate to PWHT temperature to allow early stress relaxation
NDT Detection
VT
Poor — subsurface; only detectable if weld toe cracks reach surface
RT
Poor — intergranular cracks often too fine and tortuous for good RT contrast
UT
Fair — detect after PWHT using angle beam UT focused on CGHAZ region
MT
Excellent — inspect immediately after PWHT cool-down; wet fluorescent MT recommended
PT
Excellent — use on austenitic or high-alloy materials post-PWHT
Lack of Fusion  (LOF)
aka: Cold lap (surface), sidewall LOF, interbead LOF — TWI designation: Imperfection type 401–403
Critical Fusion Line Inter-bead
Description & Appearance

Lack of fusion is a planar discontinuity where the weld metal fails to bond to the base metal (sidewall LOF) or to a previously deposited pass (interbead LOF). The weld metal is present, but there is no metallurgical bond across the interface. On cross-section (macro), LOF appears as a sharp crack-like interface with the weld metal lying against but not fused to the base metal.

Sidewall LOF is most common in narrow-groove joints, GMAW (especially short-circuit transfer), and when arc energy is directed away from the groove walls. Interbead LOF occurs when inadequate heat or poor technique prevents remelting the previous pass.

Root Causes
  • Insufficient amperage or heat input for the joint thickness and base metal
  • Arc directed at the weld pool rather than the groove wall
  • Excessive travel speed — pool solidifies before fusion boundary forms
  • Contamination (mill scale, oxide, moisture) on groove walls
  • Too wide a weld pool that "bridges" the groove without fusing walls
  • Short-circuit GMAW (low spatter mode — low heat input)

Prevention
  • Correct amperage and travel speed per WPS
  • Direct the arc at the groove wall, not the pool
  • Preheat to raise base metal temperature
  • Clean groove walls thoroughly before welding
  • Use spray or pulse GMAW instead of short-circuit for structural welds
NDT Detection
VT
Poor — internal; cold lap (surface LOF) may be visible at weld toe as a rolled edge
RT
Fair — thin dark line at fusion boundary; often missed if planar interface is parallel to radiation beam (LOF parallel to beam = low contrast)
UT
Excellent — planar reflector gives strong, sharp signal; preferred method; angle beam UT clearly locates sidewall LOF depth
MT
Poor for internal LOF; only detects cold lap that breaks the surface
Lack of Penetration  (Incomplete Joint Penetration — IJP / LOP)
aka: Incomplete root fusion, root pass defect — Lincoln Electric: one of the most common root defects
Critical (in CJP welds) Root
Description & Appearance

In a complete joint penetration (CJP) weld, LOP means the root of the joint was not fully fused — weld metal does not extend through the full joint thickness. A planar gap or unfused interface remains at the root, typically appearing as a continuous linear defect along the weld centerline at the root level.

LOP differs from LOF: LOP is specifically at the root of the joint; LOF can occur anywhere along the sidewall or between passes.

In partial joint penetration (PJP) welds, insufficient throat dimension is evaluated differently — the specified throat size must be achieved, but the root is not required to fully penetrate.

Root Causes
  • Insufficient heat input on root pass
  • Root gap too small to allow electrode access
  • Root land (face) too large
  • Wrong process parameters: travel speed too fast, amperage too low
  • Electrode too large for the root pass
  • Lack of back-gouging before the back-weld on double-sided joints

Prevention
  • Correct root gap and root face dimensions per WPS joint detail
  • Keyhole technique for GTAW root passes
  • Back-gouge to sound metal before depositing back-weld
  • RT or UT of root pass before filling — repair before burying the defect
  • Use backing (ceramic, steel, flux) to support root pass
NDT Detection
VT
Poor — internal; visible only if root is accessible and unfused gap is open
RT
Excellent — dark continuous linear indication at weld centerline; most reliable RT indication; easily recognized
UT
Excellent — strong signal from root reflector; TOFD and phased array provide precise sizing
MT
Not applicable for internal root defect

2  — Volumetric Defects

Porosity  (Gas Porosity)
Types: Scattered / Isolated (IP), Cluster (CP), Wormhole / Piping, Crater Pipe, Surface Porosity
High Weld Metal
Description & Appearance by Type

Scattered / Isolated Porosity: Individual spherical or slightly elongated gas pockets distributed randomly through the weld metal. Formed when shielding gas, moisture decomposition products (H, O, N), or base metal contamination outgasses from the pool faster than the weld can solidify around the bubbles.

Cluster Porosity: Multiple pores in close proximity — typically from a burst of contamination (restart porosity, loss of shielding). More harmful than scattered porosity at the same total area because of pore interaction.

Wormhole / Piping Porosity: Elongated, tubular pores oriented perpendicular or at an angle to the weld surface — caused by continuous gas evolution from a persistent contamination source (trapped moisture in joint, rusted plate). Appear as elongated dark spots on RT, often in a herringbone pattern.

Crater Pipe: A cone-shaped void that forms at the weld crater when the arc is broken abruptly — solidification shrinkage draws liquid metal inward creating a pipe. Highly rejectable as it combines a void with the shape of a stress concentrator.

Root Causes (by source gas)
  • Hydrogen: Moisture on electrode coating or base metal; wet flux; damp shielding gas lines; condensation in cold conditions
  • Nitrogen: Loss of shielding gas coverage (wind, too long an arc, incorrect flow rate)
  • Carbon monoxide: High oxygen in weld pool reacting with carbon; insufficient deoxidants in filler
  • Other: Organic contamination (grease, paint, primer); galvanizing zinc; mill scale

Prevention
  • Dry and store electrodes per manufacturer (LH electrodes: 300–350°C)
  • Clean base metal within 2″ of weld: grind, degrease, brush
  • Correct shielding gas flow (15–25 CFH typical for GMAW); use wind screens outdoors
  • Correct arc length (not too long — extends shielding coverage)
  • Use correct polarity; verify deoxidant filler selection for rusty plate
  • Crater fill at weld end
NDT Detection
VT
Fair — surface-breaking porosity visible; internal porosity not detectable by VT
RT
Excellent — dark rounded spots; spherical pores are the most recognizable RT indication. Wormholes appear as elongated dark streaks. Easily quantified by count and diameter.
UT
Fair — spherical reflectors scatter energy; pores < 1.5 mm often missed; cluster porosity may register as increased scatter
MT
Fair — surface porosity shows as leakage field; subsurface porosity near-surface may be detected with high-sensitivity wet MT
PT
Excellent for surface-breaking pores — pulls penetrant out of open pores clearly

RT is the preferred method for porosity quantification. Acceptance criteria (D1.1 Fig. 6.1; ASME Sec. VIII Appendix 4) evaluate maximum individual pore size, aggregate area per length, and spacing rules.

Slag Inclusions
Includes: Flux inclusions (SMAW, SAW, FCAW), oxide inclusions, elongated slag lines — Lincoln Electric: most common in SMAW multipass
High Weld Metal
Description & Appearance

Non-metallic material (flux slag from electrode coating or submerged arc flux) trapped inside the solidified weld metal. Slag forms naturally on top of the weld pool during flux-shielded processes (SMAW, SAW, FCAW-S) and must float to the surface and be completely removed between passes. Trapped slag is irregular in shape — elongated along the weld axis if trapped in a groove, or globular if trapped in a pocket created by an undercut or rolled bead edge.

Elongated slag inclusions (ESI) are particularly harmful because their length makes them behave as planar defects at certain orientations, giving them higher stress intensity than their cross-sectional area alone would suggest.

Root Causes
  • Inadequate or incomplete slag removal between passes — including slag buried at the toe of a pass
  • Incorrect electrode angle allowing slag to roll ahead of the arc
  • Undercut or groove geometry trapping slag before it can float out
  • Excessive weld pool width that allows cool edges to trap slag
  • Restarting the arc on unremoved slag

Prevention
  • Remove all slag completely with chipping hammer and wire brush between every pass
  • Use correct electrode angle (10–15° drag for SMAW)
  • Maintain convex bead profile; avoid sharp bead-to-bead valleys
  • Grind out slag traps at toes before depositing the next pass
  • Use higher-fluidity slag systems (EXX18 low-hydrogen vs. E6013)
NDT Detection
VT
Poor — internal; surface slag visible but must be removed anyway before VT acceptance
RT
Excellent — dark irregular / elongated shapes; less uniform than porosity; often seen as worm-like dark lines with irregular edges. Tungsten inclusions: bright (white) spots — opposite contrast to all other inclusions.
UT
Fair to good — irregular shape gives variable reflectivity; large elongated slag may show strong signal; small globular slag often missed
MT
Not effective — internal inclusions do not cause leakage flux to surface unless very near-surface

3  — Geometric / Profile Defects

Geometric defects are visible by VT and measured with weld gauges. They are caused primarily by incorrect welding parameters or technique. While less immediately dangerous than planar defects, undercut and overlap are particularly significant in cyclic loading because they act as stress concentration notches at the weld toe — the primary fatigue crack initiation site.

4  — Microstructural Defects ASM Micrograph Center

Microstructural defects are identified by optical or electron microscopy of polished and etched cross-sections. They are not detected by conventional weld NDT; their presence is inferred from material specification compliance (chemistry, mechanical testing, heat treatment records) and confirmed by metallographic examination. The ASM Micrograph Center provides reference micrographs for each condition below; the descriptions here follow ASM metallographic conventions.

Defect Location Appearance (Micrograph) Causes & Effects Detection / Confirmation Prevention / Remedy
Inclusion Defects
MnS Inclusions / Sulfide Stringers Base metal (throughout; elongated parallel to rolling direction) Longitudinal section (nital or picral etch): dark grey elongated streaks ("stringers") at 50–200×. Transverse section: small grey ellipses or dots uniformly distributed. Naturally present in all steel; content controlled by S spec limit. High S (>0.035%) produces coarse stringers. Effect: reduced short-transverse (through-thickness) ductility and fatigue life; lamellar tearing susceptibility; stress corrosion cracking initiation sites. Metallographic cross-section — longitudinal orientation shows stringer morphology most clearly. ASTM A295 / E45 for inclusion rating. UT from the plate face can detect stringer planes as a "grass" pattern. Specify Ca-treated steel (Ca deoxidation converts stringers to globular inclusions); specify Z-grade for through-thickness loading; control S ≤ 0.010% for critical applications.
Oxide Inclusions Weld metal and HAZ Nital etch, 100–500×: small dark spherical or irregular particles; often at grain boundaries or associated with fine acicular ferrite nucleation sites. GTAW welds may contain larger isolated oxide particles if shielding is inadequate. Form from dissolved oxygen in the weld pool reacting with deoxidants or base metal. Fine, well-distributed oxides are beneficial (nucleation sites for acicular ferrite which improves toughness); coarse oxide clusters reduce ductility. Metallographic section + SEM/EDS for compositional identification; ASTM E45 inclusion chart for severity rating. Adequate deoxidant in filler metal (Si, Mn, Ti, Al); correct shielding gas coverage; control oxygen potential of weld pool.
HAZ Microstructural Defects
Untempered Martensite in HAZ Coarse-grained HAZ (CGHAZ) — immediately adjacent to the fusion line in carbon and alloy steels 2% nital etch, 200–500×: white or very lightly etched acicular (needle-like or lath) structure against a darker background; no resolved internal structure (untempered). Microhardness HV >350 in CGHAZ is diagnostic. Cause: rapid cooling from above Ac3 in a hardenable steel (CE >0.35). Effect: very high hardness (HRC 40–65); low toughness; hydrogen cracking susceptibility — HAC initiates preferentially in untempered martensite. Microhardness traverse from fusion line across HAZ into base metal (ASTM E384). Metallographic section + nital etch. D1.1 Annex I limits HAZ hardness to HV10 325 max for most structural applications. Preheat to slow the cooling rate (reduce martensite fraction); PWHT to temper existing martensite; use lower CE base metal; use temper bead technique for repair without PWHT.
Widmanstätten Ferrite Weld metal and overheated HAZ — grain interiors and prior austenite grain boundaries Nital etch, 50–200×: plate-like or "feather" ferrite growing from prior austenite grain boundaries into grain interiors at ~45° angles; "basket-weave" pattern when plates from multiple boundaries intersect. Characteristic low-magnification (<50×) macro-appearance. Cause: slow cooling through the ferrite transformation range combined with a coarse prior austenite grain size (from high heat input or overheating above ~1100°C). Effect: lower toughness than equiaxed ferrite + pearlite microstructure; raises ductile-to-brittle transition temperature (DBTT); reduced CVN energy. Metallographic cross-section (transverse to weld), nital etch, optical microscopy at 50–100×. CVN impact testing reveals toughness reduction compared to normalized base metal. Control heat input per WPS; grain-refining alloy additions (Nb, V, Ti) restrict austenite grain growth; normalize weld metal or HAZ if required by specification (e.g., some ASME pressure vessel requirements).
Stainless Steel & Corrosion-Related Defects
Sensitization (Intergranular Cr Carbide Precipitation) HAZ of austenitic stainless steel — occurs 425–870°C exposure range ("sensitization range") Oxalic acid etch (ASTM A262 Practice A), 200×: grain boundaries decorated with dark Cr23C6 carbide films; "ditched" grain boundary structure in severely sensitized material. In practice, a "step" structure at low mag indicates partial sensitization; "ditch" indicates full sensitization. Cause: C diffuses to grain boundaries and precipitates as Cr23C6, depleting Cr below ~12% in adjacent zones — making those zones susceptible to intergranular corrosion in oxidizing acid environments. Weld HAZ always passes through sensitization range during cooling. Effect: susceptibility to intergranular corrosion (IGA), stress corrosion cracking (IGSCC in BWR environments), and sensitization-assisted cracking. ASTM A262 Practice A (oxalic acid screen) and Practice E (Huey test — 65% HNO3) for corrosion rate confirmation. Electrochemical potentiodynamic reactivation (EPR) is a quantitative laboratory method. Use L-grade (304L, 316L) with C ≤ 0.03%; use stabilized grades (321-Ti, 347-Nb); rapid cooling through sensitization range; solution anneal + water quench to redissolve carbides; cannot be reversed by PWHT below solution anneal temperature.
Surface & Through-Thickness Defects
Decarburization Surface layer of base metal (or weld cap) — extends inward from 0.1 to several mm depending on exposure Nital or picral etch, 50–200×: white (ferritic) surface layer versus darker microstructure (pearlite + ferrite, or martensite) in the unaffected interior. Layer thickness measured by image analysis or hardness traverse. Cause: oxidizing atmosphere (air, CO2, steam) at elevated temperature reacts with surface carbon; carbon diffuses out leaving a low-carbon (ferritic) surface. Common in hot-rolling, forging, and heat treatment without protective atmosphere. Effect: soft surface with reduced fatigue and wear resistance; reduces case depth after subsequent carburizing; reduces surface hardness by HRC 10–20 in severe cases. Metallographic cross-section; nital etch; measure decarburized layer depth. Microhardness traverse from surface confirms hardness gradient. ASTM E1077 for decarburization depth measurement. Controlled atmosphere heat treatment (N2, endothermic gas, vacuum); salt bath; remove decarburized layer by machining; minimize over-heating during hot working.
Hydrogen Flakes / Fisheyes Base metal — internal; visible on fracture surfaces or polished cross-sections of thick sections (forgings, heavy plate) Fracture surface: bright, smooth-faceted circular or elliptical areas ("fish eyes") surrounded by fibrous ductile fracture. Cross-section: small cracks or voids centered on inclusions or prior grain boundaries. Optical microscopy at fracture: intergranular flat facets. Cause: hydrogen dissolved during steelmaking (from moisture in scrap/fluxes) precipitates into voids during slow cooling of heavy sections; internal pressure from molecular H2 causes internal cracking. Most prevalent in large forgings and thick plate. Effect: internal cracking that significantly reduces through-thickness tensile ductility and toughness; potential catastrophic fracture initiation sites. Ultrasonic testing during mill inspection (ASTM A388 for forgings). Visible on tensile fracture surfaces (fish-eye appearance). Etched macro-section reveals cracking pattern. Vacuum degassing during steelmaking; slow controlled cooling after forging or rolling; hydrogen-removal anneal (hold at 200–250°C for extended time before cooling); specification of low-H content steel (e.g., via ASTM A435 UT requirements for pressure vessel plate).
Banding (Microstructural Segregation Banding) Base metal — throughout rolled plate and bar; oriented parallel to rolling direction Nital etch, 25–100×: alternating light bands (ferrite-rich, low Mn/Cr) and dark bands (pearlite-rich or martensite-rich, high Mn/Cr) parallel to the rolling direction. Severity rated per ASTM E1268 (banding severity rating 0–5). Cause: solidification microsegregation of Mn, Cr, Mo, and other substitutional alloying elements in the as-cast structure; hot rolling elongates the segregated regions into bands. Effect: anisotropic properties (reduced through-thickness toughness); promotes lamellar tearing along ferrite bands; hardenability varies between bands causing mixed microstructures after heat treatment; banded martensite in quench-hardened parts. Metallographic cross-section perpendicular to rolling direction; nital etch; ASTM E1268 for banding severity rating. Hardness traverse across bands reveals local property variation. Homogenization anneal at high temperature (rarely practical); select low-segregation heats; normalize twice to partially redistribute; specify maximum banding severity per ASTM E1268 on critical purchase orders.
Grain Boundary Oxidation (Overheating / Burning) Base metal surface and sub-surface — result of excessive hot-working temperature Nital etch, 100–500×: dark, oxidized (squiggly or decorated) grain boundaries; in severe "burning," grain boundary liquation visible as voids or rounded features at grain corners and along boundaries. Grains are coarse and irregular. Cause: exposure above the steel's solidus temperature range during forging or rolling — grain boundaries melt (liquate) and oxidize. Even if solidified, the oxide films remain as irreversible grain boundary damage. Effect: severely reduced ductility and impact toughness; grain boundary fracture in service; cannot be reversed by heat treatment — material must be scrapped. Metallographic section; nital etch; look for rounded grain boundary voids and oxide decoration at high magnification (500×). No conventional NDT method reliably detects burning; sampling and metallographic examination required for suspect lots. Strict furnace temperature control; thermocouple calibration; defined soak time limits; material rejected by inspection — no recovery possible. Prevention is the only option.

Micrograph reference basis: ASM Handbook Vol. 9 (Metallography and Microstructures), ASM Micrograph Center digital database, ASTM E3 (metallographic specimen preparation), and ASTM E407 (microetching). Etchant concentrations: 2% nital (2 mL HNO3 in 98 mL ethanol) for carbon and alloy steels; 10% oxalic acid electrolytic etch for austenitic stainless steel (ASTM A262 Practice A).

RT vs. UT for planar defects: RT is excellent for volumetric flaws (porosity, slag) and for root conditions (LOP). It frequently misses planar flaws (LOF, cracks) when the defect plane is not perpendicular to the radiation beam — a classic failure mode. UT using angle beam probes is specifically designed to interrogate planar flaws at the fusion line and root, making it the preferred method for detecting LOF and HAC cracks in thick plate. When both methods are required by the code, they are complementary, not redundant.
