Corrosion Protection Selection Guide

NACE International  ·  ASM International Vol. 13  ·  ISO 12944 (Coatings)  ·  NACE SP0169 / SP0572 (Cathodic Protection)

NACE International · ISO 12944 · ASM International Vol. 13A · SSPC (Coatings)

Five primary categories of corrosion protection. Most real-world systems combine two or more (e.g. coating + cathodic protection for buried pipelines). Understanding the mechanism of each allows effective pairing and failure analysis.

① Protective Coatings

Mechanism: Physical barrier separating metal from electrolyte; some coating types add electrochemical (sacrificial) or inhibitive chemistry at the interface.

Organic Coatings (paint systems)

  • Primers: zinc-rich epoxy (sacrificial), wash primer (adhesion), inhibitive (chromate-free alternatives)
  • Midcoats: epoxy, vinyl ester — barrier build
  • Topcoats: polyurethane (UV, wear), silicone-acrylate (high-temp)
  • ISO 12944 classifies exposure: C1 (very low) to CX (extreme marine/offshore)

Metallic & Inorganic Coatings

  • Hot-dip galvanizing — Zn sacrificial, 50–150 µm, structural steel
  • Thermal spray — Zn, Al, ZnAl (metallizing), offshore, bridges
  • Anodizing — Al₂O₃ on aluminium, 5–25 µm; no galvanic benefit
  • Electroplating — Cr, Ni, Cd, Zn; decorative and functional
  • Conversion coatings — phosphate, chromate (restricted), zirconate; primer adhesion base

Best for: Uniform corrosion, atmospheric corrosion, general structural steel. Requires surface preparation (SSPC/NACE SP-6 to SP-5) — coating adhesion is the limiting factor.

Limitations: Holidays, mechanical damage, and disbondment remove protection. Cannot alone address crevice or pitting in immersion without CP backup. Temperature limits (epoxy ~120 °C, thermoplastic linings up to 200 °C).

② Cathodic Protection (CP)

Mechanism: Shifts the metal potential below its corrosion potential (E_corr) into the immune or passive range on the Pourbaix diagram. Corrosion requires anodic current; CP supplies cathodic current so the metal becomes the cathode and oxidation is suppressed.

Sacrificial Anode CP (SACP / Galvanic)

  • More active metal (Zn, Al, Mg alloy) attached to structure
  • Anode corrodes preferentially; electrons flow to protected structure
  • No external power; self-regulating; limited driving voltage (Zn: ~0.25 V in seawater)
  • Applications: ship hulls, offshore jacket legs, buried tank bottoms, jetties, heat exchangers
  • Anode materials: Zn alloy (NACE TM0190), Al alloy (offshore, seawater), Mg alloy (fresh water, soil)

Impressed Current CP (ICCP)

  • External DC power supply drives current from inert anode (MMO/Ti, graphite, high-Si cast iron) to structure
  • Higher driving voltage; adjustable output; covers large structures
  • Applications: buried pipelines, storage tank floors, offshore platforms, reinforced concrete (rebar CP)
  • Criterion: steel in soil/seawater protected at −850 mV vs. CSE (NACE SP0169); −800 mV vs. Ag/AgCl/seawater (offshore)
  • Risk: hydrogen embrittlement on high-strength steel if over-protected (<−1100 mV CSE)

Best for: Buried and submerged structures (pipelines, tank bottoms, ship hulls, offshore). Most effective combined with high-quality coating (coating reduces current demand; CP protects holidays).

③ Material Selection & Upgrading

Mechanism: Inherently resistant material eliminates the thermodynamic or kinetic driving force for corrosion. No maintenance of a protection system required.

Alloy Upgrading

  • 304 → 316L → 2205 → 2507 (increasing PREN and SCC resistance)
  • Carbon steel → Cor-Ten weathering steel (atmospheric)
  • Low-alloy steel → 9Cr-1Mo or 316L (H₂S sour service)
  • NACE MR0175 / ISO 15156 defines H₂S-resistant alloys by hardness and heat treatment

Non-Metallic Materials

  • HDPE, CPVC, GRP/FRP, PTFE for piping
  • Rubber lining, thermoplastic lining for tanks
  • Ceramic tiles for abrasion-corrosion
  • Carbon-fibre composites for lightweight structural

Clad & Weld Overlay

  • Carbon steel base + 316L / Alloy 625 / titanium cladding
  • Weld overlay (buttering) on nozzles and flanges
  • Explosive bonding for large flat sheets
  • Economical for large vessels where solid alloy is cost-prohibitive

Best for: Pitting, crevice, SCC, intergranular — where coatings are impractical (internal surfaces, high-temperature, abrasive flow). First choice when operating life > 20 years or maintenance access is limited.

④ Corrosion Inhibitors

Mechanism: Chemicals added to the environment at low concentration (ppm level) that adsorb on the metal surface or react with the electrolyte to slow anodic oxidation, cathodic reduction, or both.

Anodic Inhibitors

  • Chromates, molybdates, orthophosphates, silicates, nitrites
  • Stabilise passive film; reduce anodic dissolution rate
  • Risk: if under-dosed, accelerate pitting (dangerous if concentration falls)
  • Applications: cooling water, recirculating systems

Cathodic Inhibitors

  • Zinc salts, polyphosphates, arsenates (restricted), Ca/Mg compounds
  • Form insoluble deposits at cathodic sites; impede O₂/H⁺ reduction
  • Safer than anodic (under-dosing not catastrophic)
  • Applications: water treatment, closed cooling loops

Mixed / Film-Forming

  • Amines, imidazolines, thiourea derivatives, quaternary ammonium compounds
  • Adsorb on metal surface (organic film), block both reactions
  • Oil & gas pipelines, oil-well acidising (HCl), downhole injection
  • Volatile corrosion inhibitors (VCI) — amines in packaging, enclosed spaces

Best for: Closed systems, process streams, cooling water, oil & gas production where continuous dosing is practical. Not suitable for open systems or potable water (toxicity).

Limitations: Require continuous monitoring and dosing; effectiveness degrades with flow velocity (erosion removes film); regulatory limits on certain compounds (chromates, arsenates).

⑤ Design Changes

Mechanism: Eliminate or mitigate the geometric and environmental conditions that enable specific corrosion forms — particularly crevice, galvanic, erosion-corrosion, and SCC — without modifying the material or adding chemicals.

  • Eliminate crevices: continuous welds instead of lap joints; seal gaps; use gaskets with minimum contact area; butt weld instead of socket weld
  • Prevent galvanic coupling: insulate dissimilar metals (dielectric flanges, sleeves, washers); keep area ratio favourable (large anode, small cathode); break electrical continuity
  • Reduce stress: post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) to reduce residual stress and lower SCC susceptibility; avoid stress concentrations (notches, sharp transitions)
  • Improve drainage: slope surfaces to prevent ponding; drain dead-legs; avoid horizontal pipe sections where solids settle
  • Control flow velocity: keep velocity above silt deposition (to prevent underdeposit corrosion) but below erosion threshold; avoid sudden geometry changes that cause turbulence
  • Access for maintenance: design for inspection, cleaning, and recoating; include CP test points; install corrosion monitoring coupons

Best for: Crevice, galvanic, erosion-corrosion, SCC prevention at the design stage. Most cost-effective intervention — design changes at concept cost pennies per kg of structure; retrofitting costs orders of magnitude more.

Combined protection principle: Defence-in-depth stacking is always more reliable than a single method. The industry standard for buried pipelines — high-build coating + ICCP — demonstrates this: the coating reduces current demand by 98 %, and the CP protects coating holidays that would otherwise become corrosion sites. Neither alone is adequate for a 50-year design life.

Step-by-Step Protection Selection Protocol

Step Question / Action Key Factors to Evaluate Outcome / Next Step
1 Identify corrosion type(s) present or anticipated Visual inspection, failure analysis (ASM Vol. 11), service history, environment chemistry (Cl⁻, pH, O₂, temperature, H₂S/CO₂) Determine primary corrosion form (uniform, pitting, galvanic, crevice, SCC, intergranular, erosion-corrosion). More than one may be present.
2 Is the corrosion type addressable by design changes? Check for crevice geometry (flanges, gaskets, lap welds), dissimilar metal contact, flow-induced erosion zones, residual stress concentrations If yes: implement design fix first (lowest lifecycle cost). Redesign eliminates the root cause; other methods only manage it.
3 Is material upgrade technically feasible and cost-effective? PREN requirement (use Cl⁻ vs. temperature map), SCC susceptibility, H₂S sour service (NACE MR0175), wall thickness budget, lead time If yes: upgrade alloy or switch to non-metallic. Material selection is the most durable long-term solution for pitting, crevice, SCC, and intergranular.
4 Is the surface accessible for coating application and maintenance? Internal vs. external, access frequency, operating temperature range, mechanical wear, surface prep feasibility (blast cleaning standard) If accessible: specify coating system (ISO 12944 category C3–CX); select primer/topcoat based on environment; establish recoat interval.
5 Is the structure buried, immersed, or in splash zone? Soil resistivity (Ω·cm), seawater depth, current distribution feasibility, presence of AC/stray current, access for anode inspection If buried/immersed: add CP (SACP or ICCP) to complement coating. Calculate current demand (NACE SP0169 / SP0572). Design for −850 mV CSE criterion (steel).
6 Is the system closed and can chemical dosing be controlled? Flow rate, retention time, water chemistry, regulatory constraints (potable water, discharge limits), monitoring capability If closed/controlled: inhibitor program (cooling water treatment, pipeline injection). Establish dosing rate, monitoring frequency (coupon, LPR, ER probe).
7 Combine methods for defence-in-depth Identify residual risk from each single method; pair complementary strategies (coating + CP, material upgrade + design change, inhibitor + coating) Document protection system in corrosion management plan; set inspection intervals; specify corrosion allowance if residual uniform corrosion is accepted.
