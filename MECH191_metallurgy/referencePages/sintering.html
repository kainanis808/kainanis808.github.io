1  Time-Temperature Profiles

A production sintering cycle passes through three functional zones. The preheat / burn-off zone (typically 400–600 °C) volatilizes die-wall lubricants added during compaction. The high-heat zone holds the compact at sintering temperature for sufficient time to grow inter-particle necks and achieve target density. The controlled-cooling zone sets the final microstructure and phase content (e.g., martensite in heat-treatable iron PM alloys).

Two reference curves are shown below. The standard cycle at 1120 °C (2050 °F) covers the majority of structural iron and iron-copper PM parts. The high-performance cycle at 1260 °C (2300 °F) increases diffusion rates, reduces residual porosity, and is used for high-strength PM steels, stainless steel, and certain hard metals.

2  Sintering Atmosphere Specifications

The sintering atmosphere serves three functions: (1) protection — prevents oxidation of compact and furnace fixtures; (2) reduction — reduces surface oxides on powder particles to expose clean metal for diffusion bonding; (3) carbon potential control — for iron-carbon alloys, the CO/CO₂ ratio in endothermic gas sets the carbon activity and prevents decarburization or carburization.

Atmosphere selection is driven by the oxide stability of the alloy system (Ellingham diagram), the required dew point, furnace hardware compatibility, and cost.

Dew Point and Oxide Reduction

The dew point of an atmosphere sets its effective water-vapor partial pressure (PH₂O). Combined with the H₂ content (PH₂), this determines the equilibrium oxygen partial pressure via the water-gas equilibrium. For iron PM, a dew point below −10 °C is generally required at 1120 °C to maintain net oxide reduction. Chromium-bearing alloys (SS, low-alloy PM steels with >1% Cr) require dew points below −40 °C to reduce Cr₂O₃.

Critical for stainless steel: Sintering SS-316L in endothermic gas or poorly dried N₂–H₂ can re-oxidize Cr at grain boundaries, causing sensitization. Use hydrogen or vacuum with a dew point <−40 °C and verify with a calibrated dew-point meter at the hot zone, not just at the furnace inlet.

Atmosphere zone control: In a continuous mesh-belt furnace the atmosphere composition and flow rate are typically different in each zone. Rich endothermic or high-H₂ gas is used in the high-heat zone; a lighter N₂ curtain flow at the entrance and exit prevents air infiltration without creating a flammable gas plume at the door.

3  Microstructural Evolution During Sintering

Sintering proceeds through three classical stages first described by Coble (1961) and extensively documented in ASM Handbook Vol. 7. The key parameter tracked in each stage is relative density (ρ / ρtheoretical) and the geometry of residual porosity. Grain growth and pore pinning: In the final stage, grain boundaries and pores interact by the Zener pinning mechanism. Pores on grain boundaries slow grain growth; pores trapped inside grains are cut off from boundary diffusion paths and essentially become permanent defects. This is why over-sintering (excessive time or temperature) often reduces final mechanical properties despite achieving slightly higher density — coarse grain size offsets the density benefit.
Photomicrograph interpretation tip: When examining optical or SEM sections of sintered PM parts, distinguish pores (black, irregular to rounded features) from graphite inclusions (dark, lamellar), martensite (needle-like, high-C areas near pores), and bainite (feathery). Etching with 2% Nital reveals grain boundaries and carbon gradients. Always note the magnification scale bar — pore sizes in initial-stage compacts (10–50 µm) are an order of magnitude larger than final-stage pores (<5 µm).
