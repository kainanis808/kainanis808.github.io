The Three-Condition Model

Hydrogen-induced cracking (HIC) — also called cold cracking, hydrogen-assisted cracking (HAC), or delayed cracking — requires three conditions to coexist simultaneously. Eliminating any one of the three prevents cracking entirely. This is the foundation of every HIC control strategy in every major welding code.

1 — Susceptible Microstructure
Hard, brittle HAZ microstructures — primarily martensite and lower bainite — formed when high-CE steels cool rapidly after welding. Dislocation density is high; fracture toughness is low. The critical hardness threshold is 350 HV (referenced by AWS D1.1, EN 1011-2, AS/NZS 1554). Controlled via steel composition (CE) and cooling rate (preheat).
2 — Diffusible Hydrogen
Atomic hydrogen absorbed from moisture in flux, wire contamination, or surface grease during welding. Dissolves in the weld pool at high temperature (~30 mL/100g at 1600°C) but is nearly insoluble in solid steel (<1 mL/100g at room temp). Excess hydrogen remains "trapped" and diffuses to stress concentration sites. Controlled via low-hydrogen consumables, drying, and preheat.
3 — Tensile Stress
Residual stresses from weld thermal contraction routinely approach yield strength in constrained joints. Stress concentrations at weld toes and roots amplify local stress by Kt ≈ 2–5. Applied loads add to residual stress. Controlled via joint design, weld sequence, PWHT, and post-weld stress relief.
Key insight: HIC is a threshold phenomenon. Below a critical hydrogen content, or below a critical stress level, cracking will not occur even in susceptible microstructures. Preheat, low-hydrogen electrodes, and PWHT each act to keep one or more conditions below its threshold.

Condition 1 — Susceptible HAZ Microstructure

The coarse-grained HAZ (CGHAZ) immediately adjacent to the fusion line is most susceptible. At peak temperatures above 1100°C, prior austenite grains coarsen significantly. On cooling, the transformation product — benign ferrite/bainite or brittle martensite — depends on CE and cooling rate.

Condition 2 — Diffusible Hydrogen

At weld pool temperatures, moisture dissociates — H₂O → 2H⁺ + O²⁻ — and atomic hydrogen dissolves in molten steel. On solidification, solubility drops sharply and excess hydrogen remains as diffusible HD, migrating toward stress concentration sites and microstructural traps (prior austenite grain boundaries, martensite lath interfaces).

Post-weld hydrogen bake-out: Holding the weldment at 150–250°C for 2–4 hours immediately after welding drives out ~80–90% of diffusible hydrogen before the joint cools below 100°C. This is recognised in EN 1011-2 Annex D and AS/NZS 1554.1 as an alternative to preheat for moderate-CE steels (CE 0.40–0.50).

Condition 3 — Tensile Stress

Welding always introduces residual tensile stresses due to thermal contraction. In highly restrained joints, residual stresses reach or exceed yield strength. Stress concentrations at weld toes (Kt ≈ 2–5) amplify local stress substantially.

Stress Sources

  • Thermal contraction: Weld metal shrinks on cooling; surrounding cold base metal resists, creating biaxial or triaxial tensile stress
  • Transformation volume change: Martensitic transformation expands the lattice by ~4%; this interacts with contraction to produce complex stress states
  • Joint geometry: Weld toes and root notches concentrate stress; undercut and sharp profiles increase Kt further
  • Restraint: Fully restrained repair welds or thick flange welds accumulate residual stress approaching Fy
Delayed cracking timing: HIC typically initiates 24–72 hours after welding as hydrogen diffuses to critical sites and the joint temperature drops below 150°C. NDT performed immediately after welding will miss delayed cracks. For CE >0.45, inspect no earlier than 48 hours; for CE >0.55 or Q&T steels, 72 hours minimum (EN 1011-2 Annex A).

TWI Case Studies

The following cases are representative of published TWI investigation scenarios and illustrate how the three-condition model manifests in real fabrication environments.

Case 1 — Structural Repair Weld: All Three Conditions Present

Material: ASTM A514 (T-1) Q&T steel, 50mm plate, CE ≈ 0.54 (Group III/IV boundary)
Situation: Emergency repair weld on an excavator boom arm under time pressure. No preheat applied. E7018 electrodes from a container left open for three days in a humid shop.
Finding: Toe cracks detected by MPI 36 hours after welding. Cross-section confirmed lath martensite in CGHAZ, hardness 440–480 HV.
Root cause: Susceptible microstructure (CE 0.54, rapid cooling → martensite); elevated hydrogen (~15 mL/100g estimated, open electrode storage); high restraint (repair weld in stiff structure).
Corrective action: Minimum preheat 150°C per AWS D1.1 Table 4.4 (Group IV, 38–65mm). E7018-H4R from sealed hermetically-packed container stored in quiver at 120°C. 48-hour NDT hold imposed.

Case 2 — Pipeline Girth Weld: Cellulosic Root Pass in Humid Conditions

Material: API 5L X70, 12mm wall, CE ≈ 0.42 (acceptable CE per pipeline standard)
Situation: Offshore pipeline girth weld completed at 95% relative humidity. SMAW E6010 cellulosic root pass followed by E7018-H8 fill and cap (properly stored).
Finding: Underbead crack below root pass detected by automated UT 24 hours after welding.
Root cause: Despite acceptable CE, cellulosic E6010 introduced 30–40 mL/100g HD — well above the NLH threshold. Fast-cooled root pass HAZ (no preheat, thin section) and the hydrogen load were sufficient. Fill passes with H8 did not affect the already-cracked root HAZ.
Corrective action: GTAW root pass required for all girth welds (H<4 mL/100g). Alternatively, 50°C preheat with moisture protection where E6010 root is required by specification; NDT extended to 48 hours.

Case 3 — Structural Steel: Nominal CE Used Instead of Actual

Material: EN S355JR structural steel, nominal max CE = 0.45 per EN 10025 spec; actual heat cert CE = 0.49
Situation: Preheat calculated as 50°C using nominal CE. Welding stopped overnight at 5°C ambient. Work resumed next morning without re-heating joint.
Finding: Transverse weld metal cracks found by RT 48 hours after welding.
Root cause: Actual CE (0.49) placed the weld in a higher preheat requirement band than the nominal CE (0.45). The 5°C overnight ambient temperature further accelerated cooling rate and drove hydrogen into the joint during the interruption. Real required preheat was 100°C minimum.
Lesson: Always use actual heat certificate chemistry. Account for ambient temperature per AWS D1.1 Clause 4.2.4 or EN 1011-2 temperature correction factor. Never restart a cooled weld without re-applying preheat.

Common thread in TWI investigations: HIC is almost always preventable. The most frequent failure mode is not the code being wrong — it is the code being applied to incorrect inputs: nominal instead of actual CE, assumed instead of verified electrode hydrogen content, or inadequate inspection delay.

Carbon Equivalent — What It Measures

Carbon equivalent (CE) collapses a steel's full chemistry into a single hardenability index. Carbon contributes most to martensite formation; other alloying elements contribute proportionally. Two formulas are in widespread use:

IIW Formula (AWS D1.1, EN 1011-2)
CEIIW = C + Mn/6 + (Cr+Mo+V)/5 + (Ni+Cu)/15 Best for medium-to-high alloy steels (CE >0.35). Reference: IIW Doc. IX-535-67 (Dearden & O'Neill).

Manganese contributes ~1/6 per wt% relative to carbon. Cr/Mo/V are strong carbide formers grouped together. Ni/Cu are austenite stabilisers.

Pcm Formula (JIS, Pipeline Steels)
Pcm = C + Si/30 + (Mn+Cu+Cr)/20 + Ni/60 + Mo/15 + V/10 + 5B Better for modern low-carbon HSLA steels (C <0.12%, CE <0.35). Reference: Ito & Bessyo 1968.

The boron term (×5) captures the strong hardenability contribution of even trace boron additions (typical B = 10–30 ppm). Use for API 5L X52–X80 grades.

Choosing the right formula: Use CEIIW for structural steels (A36, A572, A588, A514) per AWS D1.1. Use Pcm for pipeline steels (API 5L) or any steel with C <0.12% where CEIIW may underestimate cracking risk.

AWS filler metal specifications (A5.1, A5.18, A5.20, A5.28, A5.36) require that electrodes and wires tested per AWS A4.3 achieve the hydrogen level of their designator. The measured diffusible hydrogen per 100g of deposited weld metal must not exceed the limit.

Worked Example — ASTM A588 Grade A (Weathering Steel, 40mm Plate)

A588 Grade A is a commonly welded HSLA weathering steel used in bridges and exposed structural applications. Its copper, chromium, nickel, and vanadium additions push CE well above the nominal structural steel expectation. Default calculator values above are pre-loaded with this steel's typical ladle chemistry.

Step-by-Step: A588-A, 40mm plate, E7018-H8 SMAW electrode, ambient 15°C

1

CE = 0.516 → falls in 0.51–0.60 range → Group III

Hydrogen adjustment — E7018-H8:

If E7018-H4R were used: Lincoln Electric allows −25°C reduction → 125°C minimum.

Most common mistake: Using the nominal ASTM A588 max CE = 0.45 (from the grade specification) instead of the actual heat certificate CE = 0.516. This places the weld in Group II (107°C) instead of Group III (150°C) — a 43°C underestimate that creates real HIC risk on a 40mm plate. Always use actual heat certificate chemistry, not specification maximums.

EN 1011-2 — Analytical Preheat Formula (Annex B)

EN 1011-2 Annex B calculates minimum preheat temperature using a formula that accounts for CE, hydrogen content, heat input, and combined joint thickness. This allows preheat to be reduced when high heat input or an extra-low-hydrogen process is used — something AWS D1.1 Table 4.4 cannot capture.

Tp = 697 × CEIIW + 160 × tanh(d/35) + 62 × HD0.35 + (53 × CEIIW − 32) × Q − 328 d = combined thickness, mm  |  HD = diffusible hydrogen, mL/100g (EN ISO 3690)  |  Q = arc energy, kJ/mm  |  Result in °C.   Ref: EN 1011-2:2001 Annex B (CEN/TS method).

The four terms:

  • 697 × CE: Composition term — linear in CE; hardenability drives the baseline preheat requirement
  • 160 × tanh(d/35): Thickness term — saturates at large d; characteristic thickness constant = 35mm; combined thickness d = sum of plate thicknesses meeting at joint
  • 62 × HD0.35: Hydrogen term — diminishing-returns relationship at high HD
  • (53 × CE − 32) × Q − 328: Heat input correction — negative (subtractive) when Q is large; higher heat input → slower cooling → lower required preheat; the CE-dependent coefficient captures that high-CE steels benefit less from increased heat input
Example calculation: CE = 0.45, d = 50mm, HD = 8 mL/100g (H8), Q = 1.5 kJ/mm (typical SMAW pass):

Tp = 697×0.45 + 160×tanh(50/35) + 62×80.35 + (53×0.45 − 32)×1.5 − 328
= 313.7 + 160×0.878 + 62×2.14 + (23.85 − 32)×1.5 − 328
= 313.7 + 140.5 + 132.7 − 12.2 − 328
= 247°C → round up to 250°C minimum preheat

Other International Standards — Key HIC Provisions

ISO 15614-1:2017 — Welding Procedure Qualification (WPQT)

ISO 15614-1 requires Vickers hardness testing of the HAZ during procedure qualification. A limit of 350 HV must be demonstrated. If exceeded, the fabricator must either revise preheat (higher), revise heat input (higher), or demonstrate via CTOD/CVN fracture toughness testing that the harder HAZ is still fracture-safe — which requires engineering approval and a full toughness test programme.

BS 7910:2019 — Fitness-for-Service Assessment

BS 7910 is not a fabrication code but a fracture mechanics assessment standard — it determines whether a discovered hydrogen crack can remain in service or must be repaired. Key provisions relevant to HIC:

  • Level 1 (simplified): Flaw interaction rules + reference stress; gives a quick conservative acceptance decision for discovered cracks without full fracture mechanics
  • Level 2 (normal): Full KI / CTOD analysis; requires known HAZ fracture toughness — critical for Q&T steels where material characterisation is essential
  • Hydrogen embrittlement factor reduces effective fracture toughness for assessment while hydrogen is still present — mandates that FFS assessment be performed post-PWHT or after confirmed outgassing

AS/NZS 1554.1:2014 — Australian/NZ Structural Welding

AS/NZS 1554.1 mirrors AWS D1.1 closely but adds several improvements for HIC prevention:

  • H-level columns in preheat table: Table 3.1 has separate columns for H5, H10, and H15 (mL/100g per EN ISO 3690) allowing preheat reduction when an H5 process is certified
  • Dehydrogenation heat treatment: Clause 5.4.4 explicitly allows 150–200°C for 2–4 hr immediately post-weld as alternative to preheat for CE 0.40–0.50 steels
  • Inspection timing: Clause 6.7.1 — NDT not permitted less than 48 hr after welding for CE >0.40 or Q&T steels welded under the standard

API 1104:2021 — Pipeline Welding

API 1104 uses Pcm (not CEIIW) for modern X52–X80 pipeline steels. Section 5.3 requires preheat maintenance until at least two fill passes are complete over a cellulosic root pass. For sour service pipelines, API Spec 5L Annex H applies — HIC-resistant grades require CE ≤ 0.43 and HAZ hardness ≤ 250 HV (versus the structural welding threshold of 350 HV).

Sour Service (H₂S environments): Sulfide stress cracking (SSC) and hydrogen-induced cracking (HIC) in sour service are governed by NACE MR0175 / ISO 15156, not by structural welding codes. The embrittlement mechanism is identical but the hydrogen source is electrochemical (H₂S corrosion rather than welding). The HAZ hardness limit is 250 HV (stricter than the 350 HV welding threshold), and the material qualification procedure is entirely separate from WPQT under D1.1 or EN 1011-2.
