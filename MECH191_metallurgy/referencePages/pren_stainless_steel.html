What Is PREN?

The Pitting Resistance Equivalent Number (PREN) is an empirically derived index that ranks stainless steels and nickel alloys by their resistance to pitting and crevice corrosion in chloride-containing environments. It is calculated from the mass percentages of the three elements that most strongly influence passive-film stability: chromium (Cr), molybdenum (Mo), and nitrogen (N).

PREN is not an absolute guarantee of performance — it must always be considered alongside temperature, chloride concentration, pH, and surface finish. However it is the industry-standard first-pass screening tool for alloy selection.

Standard Formulas

PREN = %Cr + 3.3 × %Mo + 16 × %N
PRENW = %Cr + 3.3 × (%Mo + 0.5 × %W) + 16 × %N   (tungsten-bearing grades)
PRENN = %Cr + 3.3 × %Mo + 30 × %N   (high-nitrogen grades, some sources)
Coefficients: Cr = 1.0 (base former); Mo = 3.3 (strongly stabilises passive film, promotes repassivation); N = 16 or 30 (retards pit initiation, enriches at pit surface). All values are nominal wt% from the alloy specification midpoint.
Important: PREN is calculated from nominal composition midpoints. Heat-to-heat variation, surface condition (mill finish vs. pickled vs. electropolished), and heat-treatment history all shift actual pitting resistance significantly. Always use PREN as a comparative ranking tool, not an absolute threshold.

Role of Each Element

Chromium (Cr) — coefficient 1.0

Forms the primary Cr₂O₃ passive film. Minimum ~10.5 wt% required to be classified as stainless steel. Higher Cr widens the passive range and lowers the critical pitting temperature (CPT). Coefficient of 1.0 reflects its baseline contribution.

Molybdenum (Mo) — coefficient 3.3

Most potent per-weight alloying element for pitting resistance. Enriches at the pit/crevice boundary as MoO₄²⁻, inhibiting pit propagation and promoting repassivation. Coefficient 3.3 reflects ~3× the effect of Cr per wt%. Above ~7 wt%, Mo destabilises the austenite matrix (sigma-phase risk).

Nitrogen (N) — coefficient 16

Strongly stabilises austenite (allows Ni reduction in lean duplex), slows pit initiation by adsorbing at the passive film surface, and locally raises pH at pit fronts via NH₄⁺ formation. Coefficient 16–30 varies by source; 16 is standard for most duplex and austenitic grades.

Tungsten (W) — coefficient 1.65 (via Mo equivalency)

Present in some super-duplex grades (e.g. Zeron 100, SAF 2507W). Similar mechanism to Mo but roughly half the effectiveness per wt%. Accounted for as 0.5 × %W added to %Mo in the PRENW formula. Coefficient reflects 3.3 × 0.5 = 1.65.

PREN Threshold Bands

PREN < 18

Low resistance. Not suitable for chloride service. Risk of pitting even in potable water at elevated temperature.

PREN 18–25

Moderate. Suitable for fresh water, mild industrial, food processing. Not recommended above ~50 °C in chloride.

PREN 25–35

Good. Handles moderate chloride (brackish water, marine atmosphere, dilute process streams). 316L comfortable here.

PREN 35–40

High. Suitable for seawater at moderate temperature (<40 °C), aggressive chemical processing. 2205 and 904L range.

PREN > 40

Very high. Full seawater service, offshore, desalination, hot chloride acids. Super duplex and 6Mo austenitic range.

Key Takeaway — 304 vs. 316 vs. 2205: The Mo addition in 316/316L raises PREN from ~19 to ~25 — enough to handle marine atmosphere and mild process chloride. Moving to 2205 duplex (PREN ~36) adds both Mo and N, roughly doubling chloride resistance over 316L while also doubling yield strength. 2507 super duplex at PREN ~44 is the threshold for reliable seawater immersion service.

Additional Selection Considerations

Factor Effect on PREN Requirement Guidance
Crevice geometry present Raises required PREN by ~5–8 units (CCT < CPT by ~15–20 °C) Specify CCT rather than CPT; design to eliminate crevices where possible
Weld heat-affected zone Local Cr depletion in austenitic grades can reduce effective PREN by ~2–3 units Use L-grade or stabilised grade; post-weld anneal or pickle/passivate
Surface finish Ra 0.5 µm (electropolished) vs. Ra 3.2 µm (mill finish) can shift CPT by ~10 °C Specify surface finish in addition to PREN for critical applications
pH < 5 Passive film destabilised; effective PREN reduced; need higher-Mo grade Consult corrosion engineer; consider Ni-alloys or Ti if pH < 3 with chloride
SCC risk (austenitic SS) PREN does not address SCC; duplex grades inherently more resistant Use duplex (2205, 2507) in hot chloride + tensile stress environments regardless of PREN
