Weld Metal Solidification — Fundamentals

Weld metal solidification begins epitaxially at the fusion boundary: solid grains from the base metal act as nucleation sites and grow into the liquid pool. Growth is competitive — grains whose easy-growth direction (⟨100⟩ in cubic metals) is most closely aligned with the steepest temperature gradient survive at the expense of misaligned neighbours. The result is a columnar, dendritic structure with grain boundaries roughly parallel to the heat-flow direction.

Weld Metal Microstructure — Carbon & Low-Alloy Steel

On cooling from the austenite range, weld metal in carbon and low-alloy steels transforms to a complex mixture of ferritic constituents. ESAB classifies these as follows (IIW nomenclature):

Grain Boundary Ferrite (GBF)
Allotriomorphic ferrite forming at prior austenite grain boundaries (PAGB). First to nucleate on cooling. Proportional to prior austenite grain size. Reduces toughness at high fractions.
Widmanstätten Ferrite (WF)
Side-plate or intragranular plates growing from GBF or PAGBs. Characteristic parallel lath morphology. Present in medium-carbon, coarse-grained weld metals. Degrades toughness.
Acicular Ferrite (AF)
Intragranularly nucleated, randomly oriented fine laths (0.5–3 µm). Nucleates on non-metallic inclusions (TiO, MnO, Al₂O₃). Provides the best combination of strength and CVN impact toughness. Target microstructure for ESAB low-alloy wires.
Bainite & Martensite
Form at higher hardenability or faster cooling. Upper bainite (lath packets) acceptable; lower bainite and martensite raise hardness and HIC risk. Martensite HV typically >350 in low-alloy steel HAZ.

Weld Metal Inclusions & Acicular Ferrite Nucleation

The density of non-metallic inclusions (NMIs) in the weld pool controls acicular ferrite volume fraction. ESAB research (Thewlis et al.) established that inclusions 0.2–2.0 µm diameter are the most effective AF nucleants. Inclusions are formed by deoxidation reactions during solidification.

Optimising for Acicular Ferrite

  • Maintain weld metal O content 200–400 ppm (enough NMIs; excess O degrades toughness)
  • Add Ti (0.01–0.05 wt%) to form TiO nucleants; balance with B (3–15 ppm) to suppress GBF
  • Control Mn/Si ratio: Mn 1.2–1.8%, Si 0.2–0.5% for spinel inclusion chemistry
  • Avoid very high heat input: coarse prior-austenite grains suppress intragranular nucleation
  • Multi-pass welding reheats prior beads, refining grain size and increasing AF%
ESAB Wire Design: ESAB Dual Shield and OK AristoRod series are formulated with Ti–B additions specifically to maximise acicular ferrite at heat inputs of 1–3 kJ/mm.

HAZ Sub-Zone Definitions — ESAB / IIW Classification

The heat-affected zone (HAZ) is the region of base metal whose microstructure and properties are altered by welding heat without melting. ESAB defines four sub-zones based on peak temperature experienced during the weld thermal cycle.

Coarse-Grain HAZ (CGHAZ) 1100 – Tsolidus Austenite grain coarsening; on cooling: coarse GBF, Widmanstätten, bainite, or martensite depending on CE Lowest toughness; highest HIC risk; largest martensite laths
Fine-Grain HAZ (FGHAZ) 950 – 1100°C Complete austenitisation with controlled grain growth; refined ferrite + pearlite on cooling Best toughness of all HAZ zones; similar to normalised BM
Intercritical HAZ (ICHAZ) Ac1 – Ac3 (710–895°C) Partial austenitisation; mixed ferrite + austenite on heating → retained ferrite + martensite-austenite (M-A) islands on cooling M-A constituent reduces toughness; relevant to CTOD & low-T testing
Subcritical HAZ (SCHAZ) 200 – Ac1 No phase transformation; tempers existing martensite/bainite; carbide spheroidisation at high end Softening in Q&T steels; stress relief zone
Multi-Pass Welding HAZ Complexity: Each subsequent pass reheats the CGHAZ of the previous pass. Reheated CGHAZ (RCHAZ) peaks at Ac1–Ac3 → produces intercritical reheated CGHAZ (ICCGHAZ) with concentrated M-A at prior austenite grain boundaries. This is the lowest-toughness region in multi-pass welds.

Austenite Grain Growth in the CGHAZ

Above ~1000–1100°C, grain-boundary pinning particles (carbides, nitrides) dissolve and austenite grains grow rapidly. ESAB data shows grain growth follows an Arrhenius relationship:

d² − d₀² = A · t · exp(−Q / RT) d = grain diameter (µm); A = material constant; Q = activation energy; R = 8.314 J/(mol·K); T = absolute temperature (K)

For C–Mn steels without microalloy pinning, rapid coarsening occurs above 1100°C. A CGHAZ grain size of 150–400 µm is typical at 1.5–3.0 kJ/mm heat input.

t8/5 Cooling Time — Key Control Parameter

t8/5 = time to cool from 800°C to 500°C; determines transformation products in the CGHAZ via the CCT diagram. ESAB tabulates critical t8/5 values per steel grade for CCT-diagram entry.

CCT Diagrams & HAZ Transformation Products

Continuous Cooling Transformation (CCT) diagrams for the CGHAZ differ fundamentally from bulk steel CCT curves because the austenite grain size is much larger (150–400 µm). Coarser austenite shifts ferrite/bainite start lines to longer times, increasing the tendency for bainite and martensite formation.

Reading a CGHAZ CCT Diagram

  1. Identify the steel CE (IIW or Pcm) to select the correct diagram.
  2. Calculate or measure t8/5 from welding parameters (heat input, preheat, plate geometry).
  3. Draw a cooling curve from 800°C to 500°C across the t8/5 interval.
  4. Read intersection phases: ferrite start, bainite start, martensite start (Ms) temperatures.
  5. Estimate hardness and toughness from the phase fractions reached.
Critical hardness limit: CGHAZ hardness > 350 HV10 indicates significant martensite and elevates HIC risk. AWS D1.1 and EN 1011-2 both target ≤ 350 HV10 in the HAZ for non-preheat-exempt joints.

Martensite-Austenite (M-A) Constituent

M-A is a high-carbon phase formed when carbon-enriched austenite in the ICHAZ or ICCGHAZ partially transforms to martensite on cooling, with the remainder retained as austenite. ESAB identifies M-A as the primary toughness-limiting phase in modern HSLA and X-grade pipeline steels.

  • M-A islands: hard (HV 600–900), brittle micro-regions in a softer ferritic matrix
  • Preferred locations: prior austenite grain boundaries and lath packet boundaries in CGHAZ; pearlite colony boundaries in ICHAZ
  • Carbon enrichment: solute redistribution during incomplete austenitisation raises C in untransformed austenite from 0.07% to >0.5%
  • M-A fraction increases with: higher carbon, faster cooling, coarser prior austenite grain size
  • Detection: LePera etch (M-A appears white/tan); EBSD; nanoindentation

M-A Reduction Strategies (ESAB)

  • Reduce base metal carbon to < 0.07% (X70/X80 pipeline spec)
  • Use low heat input multi-pass procedures to reheat and temper CGHAZ
  • Post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) at 600–650°C decomposes M-A → tempered martensite + carbides
  • Add Ti (TiN pinning) to limit CGHAZ grain growth → less M-A formation surface area
  • Avoid ICCGHAZ by designing pass sequence so reheating peak T < Ac1 or > Ac3 (not intercritical)

Overview of Weld Cracking Types

ESAB classifies weld cracking into three temperature regimes. Understanding the regime is the first step in prevention because root causes and remedies differ fundamentally between them.

Hot Cracking (High Temperature)
Occurs during or just after solidification (solidification cracking) or in the partially melted zone (liquation cracking). Temperature range: from liquidus to ~1000°C. Driving force: tensile strain on a thin liquid film at grain boundaries. Prevention: control composition (low S, P, C) and reduce welding stresses.
Cold Cracking / HIC (Low Temperature)
Occurs at or below 200°C, often hours to days after welding. Requires all three: susceptible microstructure (martensite), hydrogen, and tensile stress. Prevention: preheat, PWHT, low-hydrogen practice, reduce restraint. Also called hydrogen-induced cracking (HIC), underbead cracking, delayed cracking.
Reheat / Stress-Relief Cracking
Occurs during PWHT or elevated-temperature service in alloy steels containing Cr, Mo, V, Nb. Carbide precipitation strengthens grain interiors while grain boundaries remain weak → intergranular creep fracture during stress relaxation. Prevention: slow heat up, controlled PWHT temp, restrict Cr+Mo+V+Nb sum.

Solidification Cracking (Hot Cracking in Weld Metal)

Mechanism

As the weld pool solidifies, a brittle temperature range (BTR) exists between the coherence temperature (where dendrites interlock) and the solidus. Within the BTR, liquid films exist between dendrite arms/grain boundaries. If welding-induced tensile strain across these films exceeds the ductility of the semi-solid, cracking initiates. ESAB notes that cracks appear at the weld centreline (centreline cracking) or at crater cracks (last point to solidify).

Susceptibility Indices (Empirical)

UCS = 230C + 190S + 75P + 45Nb − 12.3Si − 5.4Mn − 1 Units Cracking Susceptibility (UCS). UCS < 10: low risk; 10–30: moderate; > 30: high. (Lancaster, Metallurgy of Welding)
HCS = (1000S + P) / (25Mn + Cr + Ni + 0.5Mo) · (C/100) Hebble-Christensen Hot Cracking Susceptibility Index (Austenitic SS). HCS < 4: safe; > 4: hot-crack prone.

Prevention

  • Limit S + P in base and filler metal
  • Maintain Mn/S > 25 in C-Mn weld metal
  • In SS: target 4–8% δ-ferrite (FA or F solidification mode)
  • Use convex weld bead profile (avoid deep concave craters)
  • Fill craters before arc extinction
  • Reduce welding speed (allows liquid feeding of cracks)

HAZ Liquation Cracking

Liquation cracking occurs in the partially melted zone (PMZ) — base metal grains immediately adjacent to the fusion boundary that experience peak temperatures above the local solidus (due to constitutional liquation or low-melting phases at grain boundaries). ESAB identifies this as a distinct mechanism from solidification cracking.

Constitutional Liquation Mechanism

  1. Carbides, sulfides, or other second-phase particles (e.g. NbC, TiC, MnS) dissolve rapidly as temperature rises toward the solidus.
  2. Local carbon/solute enrichment depresses the local melting point of the surrounding matrix (constitutional liquation).
  3. A thin liquid film forms around the particle and spreads along the grain boundary.
  4. Tensile strain from welding shrinkage opens the film → intergranular crack in the HAZ.

Most Susceptible Materials

  • Nickel-base superalloys (Inconel 718, Waspaloy): NbC, Ni₃Nb (δ-phase) liquation
  • Austenitic stainless steels: NbC liquation at high Nb
  • High-carbon steel HAZ: cementite liquation at grain boundaries
  • Aluminium alloys: low-melting Mg₂Si, CuAl₂ eutectic phases

Prevention

  • Reduce heat input → narrow PMZ width → less liquid film area
  • Use filler metal that produces compressive shrinkage stresses in HAZ
  • Homogenisation anneal before welding (dissolves precipitates more uniformly)
  • For Ni alloys: solution annealing + quench to eliminate NbC pre-weld

Hydrogen-Induced Cracking (HIC) — Cold Cracking

ESAB describes HIC as requiring the simultaneous presence of all three conditions (the "three-factor triangle"). Remove any one factor and cracking cannot occur.

① Susceptible Microstructure
Untempered martensite is most susceptible. Hardness > 350 HV10 in the CGHAZ is a common threshold. Coarse lath martensite > fine martensite. Bainite has intermediate susceptibility. Ferrite + pearlite is immune.
② Hydrogen
Atomic hydrogen absorbed from the arc atmosphere (moisture, hydrocarbons, rust) diffuses into the weld and HAZ. Diffusible H threshold for cracking in CGHAZ: typically 5–10 mL/100g. Hydrogen is mobile at RT, accumulates at stress concentrators.
③ Tensile Stress
Residual welding stresses, restraint from fixture or geometry, or applied service loads. Triaxial stress states at root or toe stress concentrations are most damaging. Stress drives hydrogen to regions of dilatation (crack tips, grain boundaries).

Prevention Protocol (ESAB)

  1. Reduce hardenability: select base metal with CE ≤ 0.40 (IIW) where possible; use Q&T steel with controlled CE.
  2. Reduce hydrogen: use low-H electrodes (E7018-H4); dry, rebake electrodes per AWS D1.1 Table 4.2; clean joint faces (no rust, moisture, grease).
  3. Preheat: per Lincoln Electric / AWS D1.1 preheat table or carbon equivalent formula. Minimum preheat slows cooling, allows H diffusion out, reduces martensite fraction.
  4. Interpass temperature: maintain minimum (≥ preheat); limit maximum to avoid grain growth (typically ≤ 250°C for C-Mn steels).
  5. Post-weld hydrogen release bake: 200–250°C for 1–4 h immediately after welding (before cooling to room temp) drives residual H out.
  6. PWHT: 600–650°C stress-relief tempers martensite, removes residual stress, and reduces H trapping sites.

Reheat Cracking & Lamellar Tearing

Reheat Cracking (Stress-Relief Cracking)

Most common in Cr-Mo-V steels (P91, P22, 2.25Cr-1Mo) and some austenitic SS during PWHT. Carbide precipitation in grain interiors (M₂₃C₆, VC, MoC) pins dislocations, preventing relaxation by creep inside grains. Stress relaxation is forced to the weaker grain boundaries → intergranular fracture during heat treatment.

ΔG = Cr + Cu + Mo + V + Nb + Ti − 2 Graville susceptibility index. ΔG > 0 → susceptible to reheat cracking. (TWI / ESAB)
  • Prevention: slow heat-up rate (< 80°C/h) through 300–600°C range
  • PWHT temperature selection: above or below peak carbide precipitation range
  • Avoid notches at weld toes; TIG-dress or grind toes in susceptible materials
  • Post-weld NDT after PWHT by MT/PT (surface intergranular cracks)

Lamellar Tearing

Occurs in the base metal (HAZ or beyond) when through-thickness (Z-direction) stresses act on MnS inclusion stringers elongated by rolling. The crack follows a stepped path along inclusion planes.

  • Primarily affects heavy structural plates (> 25 mm) with high Sulphur (S > 0.015%)
  • T-joints, corner joints, and cruciform joints with large weld cross-sections are highest risk
  • Z-direction reduction in area (ZRA) < 15% indicates susceptibility
  • ESAB prevention strategies:

Low-S steelSpecify Z-grade plate: S ≤ 0.010%, ZRA ≥ 35% (EN 10164: Z35)

Carbon Equivalent — Weldability Indices

Carbon equivalent (CE) is a single-number index that combines the hardenability contributions of all alloying elements relative to carbon. ESAB and AWS both use CE as the primary tool for preheat selection and weldability assessment.

CEIIW = C + Mn/6 + (Cr+Mo+V)/5 + (Ni+Cu)/15 IIW formula (AWS D1.1, EN 1011-2). Best for C-Mn and microalloyed steels with C > 0.18%. All elements in wt%.
Pcm = C + Si/30 + (Mn+Cu+Cr)/20 + Ni/60 + Mo/15 + V/10 + 5B Ito-Bessyo (Pcm). Best for low-carbon steels (C < 0.18%), HSLA, pipeline steels. More sensitive to B and V. Used in Japanese JIS standards and ESAB pipeline guides.

Schaeffler & WRC-1992 Diagrams — Stainless Steel Weld Metal Prediction

Schaeffler Diagram (1949) — Composition-Phase Map

Maps Creq vs. Nieq to predict weld metal phase constitution. Originally calibrated for dilution welding of dissimilar stainless steels. Regions: fully austenitic (A), austenitic + martensite (A+M), austenitic + ferrite (A+F), fully ferritic (F), martensite (M).

DeLong Diagram (1956) & WRC-1992

DeLong added nitrogen to the Nieq formula (30N) and added Ferrite Number (FN) contours — more accurate than Schaeffler. WRC-1992 further refined for duplex SS and modern austenitic grades; separates the martensite region and uses Ferrite Number up to 100 FN.

Practical use (ESAB):
  • Calculate Creq and Nieq for the diluted weld metal (accounting for base metal dilution %)
  • Plot on WRC-1992 diagram to read predicted FN
  • Target FN 4–8 for standard austenitic SS
  • For duplex: target FN 40–60 (≈ 40–60% ferrite)
  • Verify with magnetic ferrite tester (Magne-Gage / Fischer FERITSCOPE) after welding
FN vs. ferrite %: At low ferrite contents (<10 FN), FN ≈ ferrite %. Above 10 FN the relationship diverges. Use WRC-1992 FN contours directly rather than converting to %.
