NDT Methods — Physics, Techniques & Limitations

VT  ·  RT  ·  UT  ·  MT  ·  PT  ·  ET  —  how each method works and why it has blind spots

Every NDT method works by detecting a physical contrast between a sound region and a region with a discontinuity. That contrast is governed by physics — and the physics directly determines what each method can and cannot find. A method that works excellently for a surface crack may completely miss a subsurface planar defect; a method that finds volumetric voids effortlessly may produce no signal from a tight crack with touching faces. Understanding the underlying physics lets you predict a method's limitations before you ever pick up the equipment — and it is the basis for all competent inspector decision-making. Content below follows the educational framework of the ISU Center for Nondestructive Evaluation (CNDE) NDT Resource Center.

Visual Testing VT

Visual testing is the oldest and most fundamental NDT method and is required before any other NDT method by both AWS D1.1 (Clause 6.5) and ASME Section V. It detects surface-breaking discontinuities by direct or aided observation and remains the fastest, least expensive, and most versatile method when properly executed.

Physics — Optics and Resolution
Human Eye Resolution Limit
dmin ≈ 0.1 mm  at  250 mm (10″) viewing distance
This is the photopic (bright-light) resolution limit of a normal eye. Below this size, discontinuities cannot be reliably distinguished without optical aids. At typical inspection distances (12–24″), the resolution degrades further — emphasizing why adequate lighting and inspection distance matter.
1
Ensure adequate illumination: minimum 100 fc (1075 lux) at the inspection surface per ASME Section V Article 9; AWS D1.1 allows less but recommends this minimum.
2
Inspect at no more than 24″ (600 mm) from the surface; angle of incidence should be no less than 30° to the surface.
3
Use aids as needed: magnifier (up to 10× before requiring "enhanced VT"), borescope, remote camera for inaccessible areas.
4
Measure indications with calibrated weld gauges (undercut depth, fillet leg size, crown height, weld width).
5
Document all findings; sign and date report. VT must be completed before any other NDT begins.
Key Limitations of VT
  • Surface only — no information about the weld interior whatsoever; subsurface defects (HAC, LOP, LOF, slag) are completely invisible
  • Access required — both sides of the joint must be accessible; weld roots in pipes are often inaccessible from outside
  • Lighting critical — inadequate or single-direction lighting hides shallow undercut and cracks; shadows must be used to reveal depth
  • Inspector fatigue — resolution degrades with fatigue; rotation of inspectors on long production runs required
  • Tight cracks — a cold crack whose faces are closed under compressive stress may produce no visual contrast even at the surface

Radiographic Testing RT

RT uses ionizing radiation (X-rays or gamma rays) to create a two-dimensional shadowgraph of the internal structure of a component. It is uniquely suited to detecting volumetric defects (porosity, slag) and the most reliable method for evaluating root conditions (LOP). The physics of attenuation and image formation directly determine what RT can and cannot reveal.

Physics — Beer-Lambert Attenuation Law
Intensity of Radiation After Passing Through Thickness x
I = I₀ × e−μx
I₀ = initial beam intensity  |  I = transmitted intensity at film/detector μ = linear attenuation coefficient (cm−1) — depends on material density and photon energy x = material thickness in the beam path (cm) A void (μ ≈ 0) transmits more radiation → more photons reach the film → darker area on the radiograph. A tungsten inclusion (μ very high, high density) transmits fewer photons → lighter (brighter) area — the only inclusion that appears bright.
Geometric Unsharpness (Ug) — Setup Quality Limit
Geometric Unsharpness Formula (ASME Article 2 / AWS D1.1 Annex K)
Ug = f × b / F
f = focal spot (source) size  |  b = object-to-film distance (OFD)  |  F = source-to-object distance (SOD = SFD − b) Limit: Ug ≤ 0.020″ (0.51 mm) per AWS D1.1. ASME uses tables in Article 2, App. B. To reduce Ug: increase SFD, decrease focal spot size, or minimize OFD (film tight against part).
1
Select radiation source: X-ray tube (adjustable kV, controlled energy) or isotope (Ir-192 for 12–75 mm steel; Se-75 for thin pipe; Co-60 for very thick sections).
2
Set up geometry: source-to-film distance (SFD), object-to-film distance minimized, single-wall or double-wall technique depending on access.
3
Place IQI (Image Quality Indicator): wire IQI or plaque IQI on source side; essential wire must be visible on processed film to verify 2% sensitivity.
4
Expose and process film (or acquire digital image); verify density in the area of interest: 1.8–4.0 H&D units per ASME; 1.8–3.5 per AWS D1.1.
5
Interpret on a calibrated light box in a darkened room; characterize, measure, and disposition each indication against code acceptance criteria.
Worked Example — Geometric Unsharpness Verification
Example: Is this RT setup acceptable per AWS D1.1?

Setup A — Small industrial X-ray tube, close source-to-film distance:

f (focal spot) = 2.0 mm SFD (source-to-film distance) = 600 mm Plate thickness = 25 mm → worst-case OFD = 25 mm (film on far side) SOD = SFD − OFD = 600 − 25 = 575 mm Ug = f × OFD / SOD = 2.0 × 25 / 575 = 0.087 mm (0.0034″) Limit: 0.51 mm (0.020″) → ACCEPTABLE ✓

Setup B — Same tube, source moved much closer (production shortcut):

f = 2.0 mm, SFD reduced to 150 mm, plate = 25 mm, OFD = 25 mm SOD = 150 − 25 = 125 mm Ug = 2.0 × 25 / 125 = 0.40 mm (0.016″) Limit: 0.51 mm → ACCEPTABLE — but barely; now imagine f = 4.0 mm (worn tube): Ug = 4.0 × 25 / 125 = 0.80 mm (0.031″) → REJECT setup ✗

Lesson: Moving the source closer OR using a worn tube with a larger focal spot both increase Ug. Blurred images miss fine cracks and produce false-negative results. Always verify Ug before each exposure setup.

Key Limitations of RT
  • Orientation-dependent — planar defects (LOF, tight cracks) aligned parallel to the radiation beam produce little or no contrast; they can be completely missed. The beam must pass through the defect thickness, not along it.
  • Radiation safety — requires exclusion zones, dosimetry, licensed procedures; often requires night-shift shooting to clear the work area.
  • No depth information — RT is a 2D projection; you see an indication but cannot determine at what depth in the section it sits. Two exposures from different angles (stereoradiography) can triangulate depth but are rarely practical.
  • Double-wall limitations — small-diameter pipe requires double-wall technique; superimposition of images complicates interpretation.
  • Material density — very thick sections absorb too much radiation for useful images; Co-60 extends range but creates hazard.
  • Film storage and aging — ASME requires film retention for minimum 3 years; digital detectors avoid this but require calibration management.

Ultrasonic Testing UT

UT uses high-frequency sound waves (typically 1–10 MHz in weld inspection) to probe the interior of a material. It is the preferred method for detecting planar defects (cracks, LOF) — which RT routinely misses — and is the primary inspection method for thick plate, pressure vessel nozzle welds, and HAZ cracking. Understanding acoustic impedance and Snell's law explains both the power and the geometry of UT.

Physics — Acoustic Impedance and Reflection
Acoustic Impedance & Reflection Coefficient
Z = ρ × v       R = &left;(Z₂ − Z₁) / (Z₂ + Z₁)&right;²
Z = acoustic impedance (kg/m²·s = MRayl)  |  ρ = density  |  v = sound velocity R = fraction of incident energy reflected at an interface Steel: Z ≈ 45.7 MRayl  |  Air: Z ≈ 0.0004 MRayl  →  R ≈ (45.7/45.7)² ≈ 100% A steel-air interface (crack face) reflects essentially all of the incident energy — this is why even a tight, closed crack produces a strong UT signal.
Snell's Law — Angle Beam Probe Geometry
sin(θ₁) / v₁ = sin(θ₂) / v₂
Used to calculate the refraction angle of sound when it crosses from the wedge material into steel. vL in steel ≈ 5,960 m/s (longitudinal)  |  vS in steel ≈ 3,230 m/s (shear) First critical angle: incident angle at which refracted L-wave reaches 90° → only shear wave propagates in steel. 70°, 60°, and 45° shear wave probes are the standard for weld inspection. The probe angle (in steel) is labeled on the probe.
Pulse-Echo Depth Calculation
Defect Location from Angle Beam UT
Slant path: L = vS × t / 2
Depth: d = L × cos(θ)
Surface distance: s = L × sin(θ)
t = round-trip travel time (time on A-scan display)  |  θ = probe refraction angle in steel Divide by 2 because the pulse travels out and back.
1
Calibrate instrument on IIW (V1) or ASTM E164 reference block: set range, velocity, and gain. Establish DAC (Distance Amplitude Correction) curve from side-drilled holes per D1.1 or ASME.
2
Apply couplant (gel, glycerin, water) to ensure acoustic coupling between probe and part surface; without couplant, air gap causes 100% reflection at surface.
3
Scan the weld with the probe at the calculated index point, moving at no more than 6 in/s (150 mm/s) with 10% overlapping passes.
4
Record any indication with amplitude ≥ 20% DAC (AWS D1.1 reporting threshold). Characterize: measure amplitude vs. DAC, determine beam path length, calculate depth and location.
5
Classify per D1.1 Table 6.3 (Class A, B, C, D) or ASME Article 5 acceptance criteria. Document all results.
Worked Example — Calculating Defect Depth from Angle Beam UT
Example: Locate a defect using a 70° shear wave probe on steel

Given: A signal echo appears on the A-scan at t = 18.5 μs after the initial pulse. The probe is a 70° shear wave transducer. Steel shear wave velocity vS = 3,230 m/s = 3.23 mm/μs.

Slant path: L = vS × t / 2 = 3.23 mm/μs × 18.5 μs / 2 = 29.9 mm Depth: d = L × cos(70°) = 29.9 × 0.342 = 10.2 mm from surface Surface dist: s = L × sin(70°) = 29.9 × 0.940 = 28.1 mm from probe index point

The indication is located 10.2 mm below the surface and 28.1 mm ahead of the probe. For a 20 mm thick plate, this depth places the defect approximately mid-thickness — consistent with a sidewall LOF indication in the weld pass region.

Check for second leg (skip): If the beam has already bounced off the bottom surface, the true depth is 2T − d = 40 − 10.2 = 29.8 mm — but since T = 20 mm and d < T, this is a first-leg indication, no correction needed.

Key Limitations of UT
  • Requires couplant — no acoustic signal without a liquid coupling medium; dry contact probes have much lower sensitivity; rough surfaces degrade coupling.
  • Near-surface dead zone — the initial pulse and probe ringing create a dead zone of 2–5 mm from the surface where defects cannot be detected with conventional probes. Near-surface defects require TOFD or creeping wave probes.
  • Orientation-dependent — a planar defect must be oriented to reflect energy back toward the probe. Defects at an unfavorable angle (e.g., 45° LOF in a V-groove weld with a 70° probe) may produce weak signals that go unrecorded.
  • Coarse grain noise — austenitic stainless steels and nickel alloys have coarse, anisotropic grain structures that scatter sound waves, creating "grass" noise on the A-scan that masks real indications. Specialized low-frequency probes and TOFD techniques are required.
  • Calibration block match — calibration must be performed on a block of the same material and geometry as the test piece. Using a mild steel block to calibrate for stainless inspection is not valid (different velocity and attenuation).
  • Sizing accuracy — the 6 dB drop method and DAC methods provide approximate sizing. Phased array UT (PAUT) and TOFD improve sizing accuracy but require more complex equipment and procedures.

Magnetic Particle Testing MT

MT detects surface and near-surface discontinuities in ferromagnetic materials by revealing leakage of the magnetic flux at cracks and other discontinuities. It is fast, inexpensive, and highly sensitive to surface cracks — making it the primary method for post-weld inspection of arc strikes, weld toe cracks, and HAC on carbon and low-alloy steels. Understanding the magnetic circuit explains why field direction is so critical.

Physics — Flux Leakage at a Discontinuity
Magnetic Flux and Leakage Field — Why Cracks Are Visible
B = μr μ0 H      ∮ B · dA = 0 (flux is conserved)
B = magnetic flux density (Tesla)  |  H = applied field intensity (A/m)  |  μr = relative permeability of the material Ferromagnetic steel: μr ≈ 100–1000 (highly permeable — field prefers to travel inside steel) A crack interrupts the flux path. Since flux cannot easily cross the air gap (μr,air = 1), it bulges out above the crack surface — creating a leakage field. Fine iron particles in the MT bath are drawn to this leakage field, forming a visible indication that is wider than the actual crack opening.
Critical Principle: Field Direction vs. Crack Orientation

Leakage field intensity is maximum when the crack is perpendicular (90°) to the applied magnetic field. Detection falls off rapidly as the angle decreases below 45°. A crack running exactly parallel to the field produces no leakage and may not be detected at all. This is why AWS D1.1 and ASME Section V both require MT to be performed in two perpendicular field directions to ensure all crack orientations are covered.

1
Select magnetization method: AC yoke (best for surface cracks, allows demagnetization), DC yoke or prod (deeper penetration, detects subsurface), or cable wrap (for complex shapes).
2
Verify yoke lift test: AC yoke ≥ 4.5 kg (10 lb); DC yoke ≥ 18 kg (40 lb) lift on a steel plate before each inspection day (ASME Article 7).
3
Apply wet fluorescent particles (bath concentration 0.1–0.4 mL/100 mL) or dry powder while the field is applied or immediately after (residual method for high-coercivity materials).
4
Inspect under UV-A (365 nm) black light at ≥ 1000 μW/cm² for fluorescent MT in a darkened area; ambient light ≤ 2 fc.
5
Rotate field 90° and repeat steps 3–4 to detect cracks in the perpendicular orientation. Two field directions ensure coverage of all crack orientations 0°–90°.
Worked Example — Field Direction Selection for a Weld Inspection
Example: Determine required yoke positions for a butt weld

A horizontal butt weld runs along the X-axis. The primary failure modes are:

Concern 1: Longitudinal (centerline) cracks — run parallel to weld (along X-axis) → To detect, field must be perpendicular to X = field in Y-direction → Yoke position: yoke legs straddle the weld (field across the weld) Concern 2: Transverse cracks — run perpendicular to weld (along Y-axis) → To detect, field must be perpendicular to Y = field in X-direction → Yoke position: yoke legs along the weld (field along the weld) Concern 3: Weld toe HAC — often at 45° to the weld axis → Detected by both field directions above (45° is > 30° from each field → adequate leakage) Result: Two yoke positions required, perpendicular to each other, both along the weld Move yoke in 20% overlapping passes; effective field zone extends ~25–50 mm from each leg contact.
Key Limitations of MT
  • Ferromagnetic materials only — completely ineffective on austenitic stainless steels, aluminum, copper, titanium, and most non-ferrous alloys. Use PT on these materials.
  • Surface and near-surface only — sensitivity decreases rapidly with depth. Reliable detection typically within ~3 mm of the surface (wet method); deeper subsurface cracks require UT.
  • Field direction — cracks parallel to the field may not be detected; two perpendicular directions are always required.
  • Paint and coatings — thick coatings (over ~0.5 mm) reduce field intensity and mask indications; usually must be removed. Thin primer is sometimes acceptable if lift test passes.
  • Residual magnetism — AC yoke self-demagnetizes; DC prod magnetization leaves residual field that must be removed (risk of accelerating corrosion or interfering with machining operations).
  • Geometric false indications — abrupt changes in section, weld toes, and material boundaries create local flux concentrations that can mimic crack indications ("geometric indications"). Inspector experience is required to distinguish these.

Liquid Penetrant Testing PT

PT detects open surface-breaking discontinuities in any non-porous solid material by exploiting capillary action to draw a colored or fluorescent dye into cracks. Because it has no material restriction (unlike MT), it is the universal surface inspection method for non-ferromagnetic materials — austenitic stainless steel, aluminum, titanium, and nickel alloys. Understanding capillary physics explains both how it works and what it cannot detect.

Physics — Capillary Action and Surface Tension
Capillary Pressure Driving Penetrant into a Crack
ΔP = 4γ cos(θ) / d
ΔP = capillary pressure (Pa) driving penetrant into the crack γ = surface tension of the penetrant liquid (N/m) — lower γ means better wetting θ = contact angle between the penetrant and the crack wall — smaller θ means better wetting (PT penetrants are formulated with θ near 0°) d = crack opening width (m) — narrower cracks actually have higher capillary pressure — penetrant wicks deeper The developer reverses this process: its fine powder particles create capillary channels that draw penetrant back out of the crack and spread it laterally, making the indication visible against the white developer background.
1
Pre-clean: Remove all contaminants (oil, grease, paint, rust, scale) from the surface and inside the crack. Contamination coats the crack walls and prevents penetrant entry — the most common cause of missed indications.
2
Apply penetrant: Spray, brush, or dip. Ensure the surface is uniformly wet. Time the dwell from when application is complete — not from when it was started.
3
Dwell (soak) time: 5–60 minutes depending on material, crack type, and temperature. Tighter cracks and lower temperatures require longer dwell. Do not allow penetrant to dry.
4
Remove excess: Water-washable (Type I) with water spray; solvent-removable (Type II) with dry cloth then solvent-dampened cloth; emulsifiable (Type III/IV) with emulsifier + water rinse. Never flush surface directly with solvent.
5
Apply developer, inspect, post-clean: Apply thin, uniform developer coat; wait developer dwell time; inspect under UV-A (fluorescent) or white light (visible dye); document and classify indications; clean developer and penetrant after inspection.
Worked Example — Temperature Adjustment for Cold-Weather Inspection
Example: Adjusting PT procedure for cold conditions

A 304L stainless steel pressure vessel weld is to be inspected outdoors at 45°F (7°C). Standard penetrant instructions specify a 10-minute dwell at 50–125°F.

Issue: 45°F is below the 50°F minimum temperature for the standard procedure. Options per ASME Section V Article 6: Option A — Heat the part: Heat inspection area to ≥50°F and maintain throughout the inspection. Use heat gun or portable heater; verify with contact thermometer. Most straightforward approach. Option B — Cold-temperature qualified procedure: Qualify the procedure specifically for the low-temperature range using a cracked reference specimen (PSM-5 or equivalent block with documented cracks). Verify the procedure detects all known cracks on the reference specimen at 45°F. Increase dwell time per qualification (typically 1.5× → 15 min minimum). Acceptable if procedure is qualified and documented. Option C — Abandon PT, use MT or UT instead: 304L stainless is non-ferromagnetic → MT not applicable. UT can detect the defect but has different sensitivity to surface cracks. PT remains preferred for surface cracks on stainless — heat the part (Option A).
Key Limitations of PT
  • Open surface discontinuities only — cracks that are closed under residual compressive stress, filled with corrosion products, paint, scale, or grease will not accept penetrant. Surface preparation is everything.
  • Porous materials — porous castings, sintered metals, and coatings absorb penetrant uniformly and produce a broad background bleed-out that masks real indications. PT is impractical on such materials without masking.
  • Temperature sensitive — penetrant viscosity changes with temperature; below ~50°F (10°C) penetrant becomes too viscous to enter tight cracks. Above ~125°F (52°C) penetrant evaporates, leaving the crack empty.
  • Subsurface defects undetectable — PT is strictly a surface method; internal porosity, LOP, LOF, and subsurface cracks are completely invisible.
  • Cleaning requirement — pre-cleaning and post-cleaning are essential; inadequate pre-cleaning is the leading cause of missed indications. Post-cleaning must remove all developer and penetrant residue to avoid contamination in service (especially in food, medical, or high-purity applications).
  • Material compatibility — some penetrants are not compatible with certain materials (e.g., chloride-containing penetrants can cause chloride SCC in austenitic stainless; sulfur-containing penetrants can attack nickel alloys). Always verify material compatibility from the manufacturer's data sheet.

Eddy Current Testing ET

ET induces circulating electrical currents (eddy currents) in a conductive material by applying an alternating magnetic field from a coil. Cracks, corrosion, or material property changes disturb the eddy current pattern, altering the coil's electrical impedance in a detectable way. ET is non-contact, fast, and particularly powerful for tubing inspection, coating thickness measurement, and surface crack detection. The skin depth equation is the key to understanding ET's depth limitation.

Physics — Electromagnetic Induction and Skin Depth
Skin Depth δ — How Deep Eddy Currents Penetrate
δ = 503 / √(f × μr × σ)
δ = standard depth of penetration (mm) — depth at which eddy current density is 1/e (≈37%) of surface value f = frequency (Hz)  |  μr = relative magnetic permeability (1 for non-ferromagnetic; 50–500 for steel)  |  σ = electrical conductivity (MS/m) Key relationship: Higher frequency → shallower skin depth → more sensitive to surface, but less to subsurface. Lower frequency → deeper penetration but weaker signal from surface defects. High permeability (ferromagnetic steel) → very shallow skin depth at any frequency — ET is barely practical on ferromagnetic materials without special techniques.
1
Select frequency based on required depth of penetration: high frequency (100 kHz–1 MHz) for surface crack detection; low frequency (1–50 kHz) for subsurface or through-wall testing in tubing.
2
Choose probe type: absolute (single coil, sensitive to gradual changes); differential (two coils, rejects gradual property variation, excellent for cracks); encircling (for tube inspection).
3
Calibrate on reference notch standards (ASTM E2338 for weld inspection; ASME Section V Article 8 for tubing) at the test frequency. Set null balance and gain.
4
Scan at controlled speed and lift-off (distance from coil to surface must be constant); maintain consistent probe orientation relative to expected crack direction.
5
Interpret impedance plane signals: characterize signal phase angle and amplitude to distinguish crack from lift-off, coating thickness variation, or corrosion.
Worked Example — Skin Depth at Different Frequencies and Materials
Example: Calculate skin depth for carbon steel and aluminum at two frequencies

Use δ = 503 / √(f × μr × σ) where σ is in MS/m and f is in Hz.

Material A: Carbon steel — σ = 6.6 MS/m, μr = 100 (ferromagnetic) Material B: Aluminum 6061 — σ = 24.5 MS/m, μr = 1 (non-magnetic) At f = 1,000 Hz (1 kHz): Steel: δ = 503 / √(1000 × 100 × 6.6) = 503 / 812 = 0.62 mm Alum.: δ = 503 / √(1000 × 1 × 24.5) = 503 / 156.5 = 3.2 mm At f = 10,000 Hz (10 kHz): Steel: δ = 503 / √(10000 × 100 × 6.6) = 503 / 2570 = 0.20 mm Alum.: δ = 503 / √(10000 × 1 × 24.5) = 503 / 495 = 1.0 mm Conclusion: • At 10 kHz, ET in carbon steel only sees the top 0.2 mm — surface-only method. • High permeability (μr = 100) severely limits depth even at low frequency. • Aluminum has 5× deeper penetration than steel at the same frequency. • To inspect a 1.0 mm deep subsurface crack in steel, you would need ~100 Hz, but at that frequency sensitivity to small cracks is much reduced. ET on ferromagnetic steel is rarely used for deep inspection — UT is more appropriate.
Key Limitations of ET
  • Conductive materials only — requires electrical conductivity; cannot be used on plastics, ceramics, concrete, or other non-conductors.
  • Shallow depth in ferromagnetic materials — as shown above, high permeability limits penetration to <1 mm at practical frequencies. ET is most valuable on non-ferromagnetic materials (aluminum, stainless, titanium, copper alloys, Inconel).
  • Lift-off effect — any variation in the distance between the probe and the surface creates a large impedance change that can mask defect signals. Surfaces must be smooth and consistent; probes must be spring-loaded or guided.
  • Edge and geometry effects — coil response changes near the edges of a part, at welds (composition and hardness change), and at geometry transitions. These are "benign" indications that can obscure real defects and require experienced interpretation.
  • Defect orientation — ET is most sensitive to cracks perpendicular to the eddy current flow direction. A crack parallel to the current (parallel to the coil scan direction) may produce a weak or no signal. Rotating the probe or using rotating field techniques helps.
  • Requires interpretation experience — unlike PT (visible indication) or VT, ET output is an abstract impedance vector signal. Distinguishing crack signals from coating thickness, conductivity variation, and geometry requires trained personnel with a strong understanding of the physics.

Why RT misses what UT finds — and vice versa (the fundamental reason):
RT creates contrast from thickness/density differences: a void has less material in the beam path, so more radiation gets through, making the film darker. A tight planar crack (faces nearly touching) has almost no thickness reduction in the beam direction — so it produces almost no contrast. UT, however, detects acoustic impedance interfaces: even a crack with touching faces has an air-filled gap at the tip, and Zair/Zsteel ≈ 0 → 100% reflection regardless of how thin the gap is. This is why UT finds cracks that RT completely misses, and why RT finds porosity clusters that UT may not register reliably.

Industrial Case Studies

The following case studies are representative of real-world NDT findings drawn from pipeline, pressure vessel, structural, and process industry practice. Each case shows the appearance of the indication as it actually appears to the inspector — simulated radiograph film, A-scan display, UV fluorescent image, visible dye bleed-out, and impedance plane signal — alongside the interpretation, code disposition, and the key lesson the case illustrates.

How to read these case studies

The left panel of each card shows the simulated instrument output: what the inspector actually sees — not a sketch of the defect, but a representation of the NDT image or signal. The right panel provides the scenario, what was found, and the code-based disposition. Reading instrument output is a learned skill; these illustrations develop that visual recognition before you see the real thing.

RT
Case 1 — API 5L X65 Pipeline Girth Weld: Root Porosity + IRP
Oil & gas — onshore natural gas transmission pipeline, 24″ diameter, 0.500″ wall
Scenario

New construction, cross-country gas pipeline. Cellulosic E6010 root + E8010 fill and cap. Production weld #47 selected for mandatory RT per API 1104 (100% RT on first 100 welds for each welder qualification). Film density 2.3 H&D in the area of interest.

What the RT revealed
  • Cluster porosity — 7 individual pores over a 32 mm length; largest pore 6 mm diameter. Measured against API 1104 Table 9.6.2.
  • Incomplete root penetration (IRP) — a continuous linear dark line at the root bead centerline running 90 mm. Indicates the root bead did not fuse fully across the land.
Code Evaluation — API 1104 §9.3 & §9.6
  • Cluster porosity: aggregate area exceeds 1 in² per any 12 in of weld — REJECT
  • IRP: any length of IRP is cause for rejection on a gas transmission line — REJECT
Disposition & Corrective Action

Weld cut out and re-made. Root cause: welder transitioned from downhill to uphill progression midway through; IRP formed at transition point; porosity from trapped shielding gas from damp electrode coating. Welder requalification on root pass technique required.

Key lesson: RT is the standard method for pipeline girth weld root inspection because it shows the full 360° root profile in a single shot. IRP produces a continuous linear dark line at the exact centerline — it cannot be confused with a crack (which is irregular) or porosity (which is rounded). Recognizing the difference between a geometric linear indication and a volumetric one is fundamental RT interpretation skill.
UT
Case 2 — ASME VIII Pressure Vessel Nozzle: HAZ Hydrogen Cracking
Petrochemical — hydrocracker reactor vessel, 2.25Cr-1Mo (P5 group), 100 mm wall thickness
Scenario

Hydrocracker reactor vessel in post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) inspection. Nozzle-to-shell weld on 2.25Cr-1Mo steel, P-Number 5A, 100 mm wall. Mandatory UT per ASME Section VIII Div. 1 UW-11(a) on all full-penetration welds. 45° and 60° shear wave probes used.

What the UT revealed
  • 45° probe, scanning from shell OD: strong echo at 95 mm beam path, amplitude 138% DAC. Calculated depth: L×cos45° = 67 mm from surface (33 mm from ID — mid-thickness to ID).
  • 60° probe confirms same location, amplitude 115% DAC from perpendicular incidence. Defect length by 6 dB drop method: 38 mm.
  • Characteristic: planar indication at fusion line, parallel to weld axis. No back-scatter at 70°. Consistent with HAZ hydrogen cracking (cold cracking).
Code Evaluation — ASME VIII Div. 1 Appendix 12

Any planar (crack-like) indication exceeding DAC is automatically cause for rejection regardless of length. REJECT — all amplitudes >100% DAC are unacceptable

Disposition & Root Cause

Weld excavated by grinding; metallographic section confirmed untempered martensite + hydrogen micro-cracks in the coarse-grain HAZ. Root cause: preheat dropped below 300°F (149°C) during interruption; hydrogen from cellulosic electrode moisture. Repair required 400°F minimum preheat, low-hydrogen E9016 electrodes with bake-out, and PWHT immediately after welding.

Key lesson: RT would likely have missed this crack — it was a tight, planar LOF-type crack with faces nearly touching. The A-scan amplitude tells you how reflective the interface is; a near-100% reflection coefficient (crack faces + air) gives strong echoes even from cracks too tight for RT. This is the canonical example for why thick-wall pressure vessels use UT, not RT.

Case 3 — Highway Bridge Girder: Weld Toe Fatigue Crack
Structural — highway bridge, A572 Gr.50 steel, transverse stiffener-to-web fillet weld, 15-year in-service inspection
Scenario

Routine in-service inspection of a plate girder highway bridge per AASHTO MBE and AWS D1.5 Bridge Welding Code. Transverse stiffener-to-web fillet weld on a high-traffic (Category C fatigue detail) zone. AC electromagnetic yoke, wet fluorescent MT (ASTM E709). Two yoke passes: field along weld axis, then perpendicular.

What the MT revealed
  • Sharp, well-defined fluorescent line originating at the weld toe at the tension flange end of the stiffener, propagating diagonally into the web.
  • Indication length: 52 mm measured along the crack path. Width of particle accumulation ~3 mm (indication width, not crack width).
  • Fatigue crack morphology: single, clean, relatively straight — consistent with fatigue rather than HAC (which branches) or SCC (which shows branching tree pattern).
Code Evaluation — AWS D1.5 / AASHTO

Any crack indication is cause for rejection and repair. Fatigue cracks at Category C details require engineering evaluation of remaining life. REJECT — crack, any length

Disposition & Corrective Action

Crack arrested by drilling a 12 mm stop hole at the crack tip (confirmed by MT after drilling). Weld toe ground and re-welded per AWS D1.5 repair procedure. Bridge downgraded from Category C to Category E' detail pending re-evaluation. Inspection frequency increased to 2-year cycle.

Key lesson: Weld toes are stress concentration points — fatigue cracks almost always start there in cyclically loaded structures. The fluorescent indication is wider than the physical crack because particles accumulate in the leakage field halo. This magnification effect actually makes small cracks easier to see — it is part of what makes fluorescent MT more sensitive than visible MT.

Case 4 — 316L Stainless Pipe Weld: Service-Induced Stress Corrosion Cracking
Process — pharmaceutical plant, 316L SS process piping, Cl⁻ environment, post-service inspection at 3-year turnaround
Scenario

Turnaround inspection at a pharmaceutical plant. 316L SS process piping (2″ Sch.40), service fluid contains trace chlorides from cleaning-in-place (CIP) solutions. Visible dye PT (Type II solvent removable, Form D non-aqueous developer), per ASME Section V Article 6 and ASTM E165.

What the PT revealed
  • Multiple branching red indications in the HAZ immediately adjacent to the weld toe — the sensitized zone where carbide precipitation reduces corrosion resistance.
  • Indication pattern: branching, tree-like structure over a 35 mm region. Branching morphology is the hallmark of SCC (versus a single straight fatigue crack or a sharp linear weld crack).
  • Bleed-out took approximately 8 minutes to fully develop — indicating tight cracks requiring longer developer dwell. No indications on the weld cap itself.
Code Evaluation — ASME B31.3 / Client Spec

Any crack indication is rejectable. Branching SCC in process piping requires root cause analysis before repair. REJECT — crack, any length

Disposition & Root Cause

Section cut out; metallographic examination confirmed transgranular SCC in the sensitized HAZ. Root cause: concentrated chloride residues from CIP bleach solution not fully flushed before drying. Repair used 316L with controlled heat input (max 65 kJ/in) to minimize sensitization. Process change: mandatory DI water rinse with conductivity verification after CIP before drying.

Key lesson: PT cannot be used on this material with MT (austenitic SS is non-ferromagnetic). It also cannot be performed blind — surface must be free of all CIP residues before PT or the penetrant will not enter the cracks. The branching pattern that distinguishes SCC from fatigue or hydrogen cracking is visible to PT but only if interpreted correctly — the morphology of the bleed-out is diagnostic.

Case 5 — Power Plant Condenser: OD Pitting at Tube Support Plate
Power generation — steam surface condenser, 90/10 Cu-Ni tubing, 25 mm OD × 1.2 mm wall, 20,000-tube bundle
Scenario

Planned outage inspection of a 20,000-tube condenser bundle in a 500 MW coal plant. 90/10 Cu-Ni tubing (25 mm OD, 1.2 mm wall), 15 years service. Absolute + differential probe combination at 300 kHz, automated data acquisition at 1 m/s pull speed. ASME PCC-2 Article 2.2 acceptance criteria (plug tubes >40% wall loss).

What the ET revealed
  • Phase angle 52° — matching the OD calibration reference sector (~50°). This identifies the defect as originating on the outside diameter surface, at a tube support plate location (high turbulence zone).
  • Signal amplitude plots between the 40% and 60% OD notch calibration signals → interpolated at approximately 46% wall loss.
  • Scan identified 847 tubes with OD indications >40% wall loss at the same support plate elevation — systematic attack from under-deposit corrosion (biofouling) in cooling water.
Code Evaluation — ASME PCC-2 / EPRI

Tubes with >40% wall loss plugged per plant guidelines. Tubes 30–40% wall loss scheduled for re-inspection next outage. PLUG — 847 tubes (>40% wall loss) MONITOR — 623 tubes (30–40%)

Disposition & Root Cause

847 tubes plugged with titanium plugs. Root cause analysis: biofouling colonization at support plate baffle gaps creating stagnant zones; microbially influenced corrosion (MIC) progressed to under-deposit OD pitting. Corrective action: enhanced biocide program (continuous chlorination 0.5 ppm residual), support plate geometry modification on next bundle replacement.

Key lesson: The phase angle of the ET signal is as important as its amplitude — it tells you whether the defect is on the ID or OD surface, which determines the root cause and the repair strategy. A signal at 50° (OD) means the problem is in the cooling water side; at 130° (ID) it means the process fluid side. Without phase discrimination, you know there is a defect but not where it is — amplitude alone is insufficient for tube inspection diagnosis.
