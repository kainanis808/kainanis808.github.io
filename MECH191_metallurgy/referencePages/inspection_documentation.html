Reading Inspection Documentation

Material Test Reports  ·  Weld Inspection Records  ·  NDT Reports

Every fabricated structure or pressure component that leaves a shop carries a paper trail — a set of documents that prove the material was what it claimed to be, the welder was qualified, the procedure was followed, and the finished product met the acceptance criteria. Learning to read these documents is a core skill for welding inspectors (AWS CWI), quality engineers, and anyone involved in materials procurement or structural fabrication. This reference covers the three major document types: Material Test Reports (MTRs), weld inspection and procedure records, and nondestructive testing (NDT) reports.

Material Test Reports (MTRs)

A Material Test Report (also called a Mill Test Report or Mill Certificate) is a document issued by the steel producer certifying that the material shipped meets the requirements of the specified standard. It is the primary traceability document for engineering materials. Every piece of material used in ASME Code or AWS D1.1 work must be accompanied by a valid MTR, and the heat number on the MTR must be traceable to the marking on the actual material.

Heat (Ladle) Analysis vs. Product Analysis

Analysis Type When Taken How Reported What It Means
Heat (Ladle) Analysis From a sample of molten steel in the ladle, before casting Always reported on the MTR — it is the primary chemistry record Represents the average composition of the entire heat; small variations across the cast product are normal
Product Analysis From a sample taken from the finished product (plate, bar, pipe) Only reported when required by the specification or purchaser More representative of actual local composition; specification limits are slightly wider than ladle limits to account for normal segregation

ASTM product analysis tolerances (the allowable deviation from ladle analysis limits) are defined in ASTM A29 for bars and A6 for structural shapes and plate.

Carbon Equivalent — Weldability Assessment

Carbon equivalent (CE) is calculated from the MTR chemistry to estimate how susceptible a steel is to hydrogen-induced cracking (HAC) in the heat-affected zone. It is the primary tool for determining preheat requirements when the WPS does not specify them by heat. Two formulas are in common use:

IIW Carbon Equivalent (CEIIW) — best for C > 0.18%
CE = C + Mn/6 + (Cr+Mo+V)/5 + (Ni+Cu)/15
All values in weight percent from the MTR chemistry section.
Elements not listed on the MTR are taken as zero.
Pcm (Ito–Bessyo) — better for low-carbon steels (C ≤ 0.18%)
Pcm = C + Si/30 + (Mn+Cu+Cr)/20 + Ni/60 + Mo/15 + V/10 + 5B
B = boron content in weight percent (often 0 for structural steels).

CE Thresholds and Preheat Guidance (AWS D1.1 Annex I basis)

CEIIW Range Weldability Typical Min. Preheat (°F / °C) Practical Notes
< 0.35 Excellent None required (normal conditions) Most A36 and A53 Gr. B steels fall here; preheat still required in cold weather
0.35 – 0.45 Good Typically 50 – 125 °F  /  10 – 50 °C Most A572 Gr. 50 and A516 Gr. 70 steels; low-hydrogen electrodes recommended
0.45 – 0.60 Fair — increased HAC risk 150 – 400 °F  /  65 – 200 °C A514, higher-carbon alloy steels; low-hydrogen process (H4 or H8) required; preheat verified by contact thermometer or Tempilstik
> 0.60 Poor — high HAC risk 400 °F+  /  200 °C+ Requires careful WPS development; possibly PWHT; consult D1.1 Annex I or qualified engineer
MTR verification checklist — minimum steps before accepting material: (1) Confirm the specification and grade on the MTR match the drawing requirement exactly. (2) Confirm the heat number on the MTR matches the physical marking stamped or stenciled on the material. (3) Confirm every reported chemistry value is within the specification limits. (4) Confirm all mechanical test results meet specification minimums (and maximums, where applicable). (5) Confirm the certification statement cites the correct specification and bears an authorized signature. (6) If ASME Code work, confirm "SA-" prefix — not just "A-."

Welding Inspection Records (AWS CWI Documentation)

A Certified Welding Inspector (CWI) is responsible for generating and reviewing a set of records that collectively prove a weld was made correctly from start to finish. AWS D1.1 and ASME Section IX both require that these records be maintained. Together they form the quality record package for a weld joint.

Pre-Weld, In-Process, and Post-Weld Inspection Stages

Pre-Weld Inspection
  • Verify WPS is applicable to the joint
  • Verify welder is qualified (check WPQ/WQT)
  • Review MTR — material matches WPS base metal
  • Inspect joint fit-up (root opening, bevel angle, root face, alignment)
  • Verify preheat is applied and measured
  • Verify correct filler metal is staged (check AWS classification)
  • Verify filler metal storage / electrode oven temperature (for LH electrodes)
In-Process Inspection
  • Monitor preheat and interpass temperature continuously
  • Verify arc parameters (amperage, voltage, travel speed) match WPS range
  • Calculate and record heat input per pass if required
  • Inspect each pass for slag removal and visual quality
  • Verify pass sequence matches WPS
  • Document any deviations — do not allow unauthorized changes to WPS variables
Post-Weld Inspection
  • Visual inspection (VT) — required before any NDT
  • NDT as required by code and contract (RT, UT, MT, PT)
  • Dimensional inspection of finished weld
  • PWHT chart review (if required)
  • Final accept / reject disposition and signature
  • Compile and file complete quality record package

WPS Review — Essential Variables to Verify Before Welding

Essential Variable What the Inspector Checks Why It Matters
Welding process Process on WPS (e.g., SMAW, FCAW) matches process actually being used Changing process invalidates the WPS; requires separate qualification
Base metal P-number / group Material MTR spec matches the P-number listed on the WPS Different P-numbers have different preheat, heat input, and HAZ behavior
Filler metal F-number & A-number Electrode or wire AWS classification matches WPS filler metal entry Wrong filler metal can result in under-strength or cracking-susceptible welds
Thickness range qualified Actual base metal thickness ≤ maximum qualified thickness on WPS WPS is only valid for the thickness range proven in the PQR test coupon
Welding position Actual joint orientation (1G, 2G, 3G, 4G, etc.) is covered by WPS Gravity affects weld pool; a WPS qualified in flat position does not cover overhead
Preheat / interpass temperature Minimum preheat is met; maximum interpass is not exceeded Inadequate preheat → HAC; excessive interpass can reduce toughness
Heat input range Calculated HI per pass is within the WPS-qualified range High HI → coarse grain, reduced toughness; low HI → rapid cooling, HAC risk
Post-weld heat treatment PWHT required by code or WPS is performed and charted PWHT relieves residual stress and reduces HAZ hardness; omitting it voids the WPS

Heat Input Calculation

Heat input is an essential variable on many WPSs and must be calculated from recorded arc parameters:

Arc Heat Input (kJ/in) — GMAW, SMAW, FCAW, SAW
HI (kJ/in) = [ Volts × Amps × 60 ] ÷ [ Travel Speed (in/min) × 1,000 ]
Example: 26 V × 180 A × 60 ÷ (8 in/min × 1,000) = 35.1 kJ/in
Some codes (e.g., AWS D1.1 2020+) use a thermal efficiency factor η for processes such as GTAW (η ≈ 0.60) and SAW (η ≈ 1.0).

Welder Qualification (WPQ / WQT) — What to Verify

Field on the WPQ What to Check
Welder name and stamp / ID Must match the individual actually performing the work; stamps must be applied to production welds per code
Process qualified Process on WPQ matches process to be used (e.g., SMAW qualification does not cover FCAW)
Position(s) qualified Per AWS D1.1 Table 4.12: a 3G qualification qualifies 1G and 3G; a 4G qualifies 1G and 4G; 3G+4G qualifies all groove positions
Thickness range Actual weld joint thickness falls within the welder's qualified range
Qualification date Under D1.1: qualification expires if the welder has not used the process for more than 6 months. Confirm currency.
Test results Bend test results (root bend, face bend, side bend) all accepted; no cracks > 1/8″ in any direction
Authorizing signature Signed by an authorized company representative or CWI; unsigned WPQs are not valid

Fit-Up Inspection — Key Measurements

Parameter What It Is Typical D1.1 Tolerance
Root opening (gap) Distance between the root faces of the two members before welding Per joint detail on WPS; excessive gap requires backing or reduced groove angle
Root face (land) Thickness of the ungrooved portion at the base of the joint Typically 1/16″ ± 1/16″ for prequalified CJP groove welds
Groove angle (included) Total included angle of the groove between the two bevel faces Varies by joint type; e.g., V-groove min. 45° for prequalified single-V
Joint mismatch (hi-lo) Offset between the faces of two members being joined end-to-end (butt joint) Max. 1/10 of the thinner member thickness, not to exceed 1/8″ (D1.1 5.22.1)
Straightness / distortion Deviation of the joint from its specified geometry before welding begins Must be within drawing tolerances; pre-cambering is often used to compensate for weld shrinkage
Cleanliness Joint surfaces free of mill scale (within 2″), rust, moisture, paint, oil, and other contaminants D1.1 5.15: surfaces must be free of injurious material; mill scale is acceptable if proven not to impair weld quality for the WPS used

Reading NDT Reports

Each NDT method produces a written report that a CWI or Level II examiner must be able to interpret. Reports contain standardized fields for setup parameters, indication descriptions, and accept/reject dispositions. Understanding the terminology and how indications are characterized is essential for both writing and reviewing these records.

Discontinuity Type Rejection Criterion Notes
Cracks Any crack of any size — reject No minimum size; all cracks are rejectable by VT
Surface porosity (individual) Diameter > 3/8″ — reject Porosity < 3/8″ is acceptable in static loading
Undercut Depth > 1/32″ parallel to primary stress; > 1/16″ transverse to primary stress Cyclic loading: max. 1/32″ regardless of direction
Overlap Any overlap — reject Overlap creates a notch that initiates fatigue cracks
Fillet weld size deficiency More than 2″ in any 12″, or any deficiency > 1/16″ Undersized fillet is the most common VT finding on structural work
Weld profile (convexity) Convexity exceeding 1/16″ + 0.1 × face width Excessive reinforcement does not increase weld strength and creates stress risers

AWS D1.1 UT Indication Classification (Table 6.3 Summary)

Class Indication Characteristics Typical Flaw Type Acceptance
A Amplitude ≥ DAC with length ≤ 3/4″ Porosity, small inclusions Accept (static loading only)
B Amplitude ≥ DAC with length ≤ 3/4″, at specific depth zones Inclusions, minor LF Accept — depends on depth zone
C Amplitude ≥ DAC, length between 3/4″ and 2″, or at any critical depth Larger LF, elongated inclusions Reject for cyclically loaded; conditional for static
D Amplitude ≥ DAC, length > 2″, or any linear indication Cracks, large planar LF Reject — all loading types

DAC = Distance Amplitude Correction curve, established during calibration. Amplitude above DAC = strong reflector / large or planar flaw. The depth zones (2T/3 rule) account for the fact that flaws near the root and near the cap are more harmful than mid-thickness flaws.

UT vs. RT — which record to expect: AWS D1.1 allows UT to substitute for RT on plate thickness ≥ 5/16″ (8 mm). RT is preferred for thin sections and for detecting volumetric flaws (porosity, inclusions). UT is superior for detecting planar flaws (cracks, LF) in thick plate. The examination method required is specified in the contract documents or by the Engineer — check the project quality plan before assuming which report will be generated.

Nonconformance Reports (NCRs) and Repair Documentation

When an inspection reveals that a material, weld, or process does not meet the specified requirement, a Nonconformance Report (NCR) must be issued before any repair or disposition takes place. The NCR is the formal record that a problem was identified, evaluated, dispositioned, and closed — it protects both the fabricator and the owner from undocumented repairs.

NCR Required Fields

Field Content Why It Is Required
NCR number Unique sequential identifier Enables traceability; NCRs must be logged and tracked to closure
Date issued Date the nonconformance was discovered Establishes timeline; required for quality trend analysis
Description of nonconformance Objective statement of what was found, where, and how (e.g., "UT indication Class D at weld W-31, 4.2″ from A-end, 0.8″ length") Must be specific enough that the repair welder and re-inspector can locate the exact defect
Reference specification and clause The code requirement that was not met (e.g., "AWS D1.1:2020 Table 6.3, Class D linear indication") Identifies the applicable acceptance standard; dispositions must be against the same standard
Disposition One of: Use-As-Is (engineering evaluation required), Rework / Repair, Reject / Scrap Disposition must be authorized by qualified personnel (Engineer of Record for Use-As-Is)
Repair WPS reference WPS number used for the repair weld Repair welds require a qualified WPS — often a separate "repair WPS" — and a qualified welder
Re-inspection results Results of the repeat inspection (VT + applicable NDT) after repair The same method that originally found the defect must be repeated to confirm repair success
Closure signature CWI or authorized inspector signature confirming re-inspection acceptance Open NCRs block final acceptance of the component
Repair weld documentation chain: NCR issued → disposition approved → repair WPS selected and reviewed → qualified welder assigned → repair performed → VT of repair → NDT of repair (same method as original rejection) → results recorded on NCR closure → NCR signed closed. Every step must be documented; verbal approvals are not acceptable on code work.

Use-As-Is Disposition — When It Is Permitted

A "Use-As-Is" disposition allows a nonconforming item to remain in service without repair. This is only permitted when an engineering evaluation demonstrates that the nonconformance does not affect the structural or pressure integrity of the component. Key requirements:

  • Must be authorized in writing by the Engineer of Record (or the Authorized Inspector for ASME Code work)
  • Cannot be used to accept cracks under AWS D1.1 — cracks are never acceptable Use-As-Is
  • Engineering evaluation must document the analysis basis (stress level, fatigue category, flaw size vs. fracture toughness)
  • The disposition is permanently recorded on the NCR and in the quality record package
